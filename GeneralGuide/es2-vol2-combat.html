The 3-Phase Combat System & Weapon Ranges

Combat resolves across three range phases ‚Äî your weapon choices determine which phase you dominate
‚öîÔ∏è Phase Sequence and Range Resolution MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Every battle is divided into three consecutive phases: Long Range ‚Üí Medium Range ‚Üí Short Range. Both fleets fire simultaneously in each phase using whatever weapons have accuracy in that range band. The phase you fight in is not random ‚Äî it is determined by the battle cards both sides play before combat begins. Each card sets a preferred range; the interaction between both cards' preferences resolves which phase actually occurs (or if both agree, you skip to that range directly).

Weapon accuracy by range band ‚Äî this is the core table to memorize:

Weapon TypeLong RangeMedium RangeShort RangeDefense Target
Missiles~90‚Äì100%~65%~15%Kinetic shields / Flak
Kinetic (Cannons)~30%~65%~95‚Äì100%Energy shields
Beam (Lasers)~50%~85%~50%Armor plating

The weapon-defense counter system: Kinetics bypass energy shields (deal full damage to hull). Beams bypass armor plating. Missiles are countered by Flak modules (reduces missile accuracy dramatically). Penetration modules partially bypass all defenses simultaneously ‚Äî covered in the advanced section of battle card design.

FLOTILLA LANE POSITIONING
Each fleet's ships are arranged in three lanes: Top, Middle, Bottom. Middle lane ships fire at all three enemy lanes. Top and Bottom ships fire at two lanes each. Cross-lane shots are resolved as Long Range regardless of the actual phase ‚Äî so a Bottom-lane ship firing at the enemy's Top lane uses Long Range accuracy for that shot even during Short Range phase. Put your highest-DPS ships in the Middle lane. Put your tanks (Protector role) in the lanes where they'll absorb the most fire.

Fleet Composition & Ship Design Roles

Three exclusive ship roles with different module slots ‚Äî never mix them on the same hull
üöÄ The Three Combat Roles and Their Module Constraints MECHANIC ‚ñæ

When designing a ship, you must choose one of three roles: Hunter, Coordinator, or Protector. Each role unlocks a different set of module slots and locks out the others. You cannot build a ship that combines Hunter offensive modules with Protector defensive modules ‚Äî the role choice is exclusive.

RoleModule AccessPrimary FunctionFleet Count
HunterWeapon slots (kinetic, beam, missile), targeting modulesDamage output ‚Äî the DPS backbone of your fleet3‚Äì5 per fleet
CoordinatorSupport modules (fleet buffs, siege tools, manpower transport), limited weaponsSiege bombardment + fleet-wide bonuses. Essential for invasion.1‚Äì2 per fleet
ProtectorHeavy defense modules (shields, armor, Flak), support weaponsAbsorb damage, counter missiles via Flak, extend fleet lifespan1‚Äì2 per fleet

A standard combat fleet for mid-game: 3 Hunter ships (weapon-focused, Medium hull), 1 Coordinator (siege + fleet buff modules), 1 Protector (Flak + heavy armor). The Coordinator is the most misunderstood role ‚Äî without it, you cannot reduce system defenses before invasion, and you lose the fleet-wide bonus modules that multiply your Hunters' output.

NEVER BUILD "GENERALIST" SHIPS
The most common fleet design mistake is trying to put weapons AND defenses on the same ship by splitting between Hunter and Protector. You can't ‚Äî the role system prevents it ‚Äî but players still try by choosing Hunter, then filling unused slots with weak defensive items. A Hunter with weapon slots should be entirely dedicated to weapons. A Protector's defense budget should go entirely to shields + armor + Flak. Dilution reduces both ships to mediocrity.

Battle Cards: Range Setting + Passive Bonuses

Cards do two things simultaneously ‚Äî most players only notice one of them
üÉè How Battle Cards Actually Work MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Before each battle phase, both sides secretly select one battle card. Cards do two simultaneous things that most players conflate into one:

  • FUNCTION 1Set a preferred combat range for that phase. The interaction between your card and the opponent's card resolves which range the phase actually occurs at. If both cards prefer Long Range, the phase occurs at Long Range. If they prefer different ranges, the outcome depends on the specific card interaction (shown in the card tooltip before confirmation). This is what most players know.
  • FUNCTION 2Apply a passive fleet-wide bonus for the entire duration of the phase. Every card has a secondary effect independent of range ‚Äî things like "+20% missile damage this phase," "+15% evasion," "+X% crit chance," or "enemy Flak effectiveness reduced by 30%." These bonuses apply regardless of which range the phase resolves at. This is what most players miss entirely. A Long Range card that loses the range contest still applies its passive bonus to your fleet during that phase.

Faction-specific power cards are the most dramatic example of Function 2. The United Empire's Sheredyn Formation card grants +50% Penetration to all weapons for that phase ‚Äî bypassing half of the opponent's shields and armor regardless of weapon type. Selecting this card is not primarily about range; it's about the Penetration bonus.

BUILDING A CARD STRATEGY
1. Know your dominant weapon type (Missiles, Kinetics, or Beams) and select cards that favor that range as PRIMARY.
2. Among cards that favor your preferred range, pick the ones with the strongest passive bonus for your fleet composition.
3. Keep 1 "surprise" card in your hand that favors a different range ‚Äî if an opponent reads your card pattern and counter-picks, unpredictability costs them a phase.
4. Always read the full tooltip of each card before the battle ‚Äî the passive bonus is listed underneath the range preference, and it changes the calculus entirely.

Ground Invasion: Troops, Manpower & Post-Battle Choices

Winning the space battle is only half the job ‚Äî ground invasion is a separate system with permanent consequences
ü™ñ How Ground Combat Works Mechanically MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Once you've won a space battle and achieved orbital supremacy, you can initiate a ground invasion. Ground combat is entirely separate from fleet combat and uses the Manpower resource ‚Äî a reserve pool that converts into troops for the invasion.

Three troop types are available, each countering one other (rock-paper-scissors):

Troop TypeCountersWeak AgainstBest Against
InfantryAerialMechanizedLightly-defended systems; early game invasions
MechanizedInfantryAerialSystems with heavy Infantry garrisons
AerialMechanizedInfantrySystems with Mechanized garrisons; rapid capture

Before invading, you must survey the defender's troop composition ‚Äî visible in the system tooltip once you have orbital control. This tells you which troop type to bring. Then you select how much Manpower to commit. The conversion ratio is not 1:1 ‚Äî each Manpower unit converts into a troop strength value that depends on your current Manpower-to-Troops tech level (upgradeable in the Military tech tree). At base tech, 10 Manpower ‚âà 1 troop unit.

SIEGE BOMBARDMENT BEFORE INVASION
System defenses (Fortresses, Defense Turrets, Ground Batteries) actively resist invasion and boost the defender's troop strength. Before committing Manpower, use your Coordinator ship's Siege bombardment action to reduce these. Siege is triggered from the fleet action menu when orbiting the target system ‚Äî each turn of bombardment reduces defense structure HP. One to three turns of Coordinator siege before invasion dramatically reduces your Manpower cost and casualty rate. Never invade a well-fortified system without sieging first.
üèõÔ∏è Post-Invasion Choices: Occupy, Pillage, or Raze ‚Äî Permanent Consequences STRATEGY ‚ñæ

After winning the ground battle, you choose what to do with the captured system. This is one of the most permanent decisions in the game and cannot be undone:

OptionImmediate EffectLong-term EffectWhen to Use
OccupySystem transfers to your empire with all buildings intact; population stays but with heavy Approval penaltyFull system becomes yours permanently. Highest long-term value if you can manage the Approval crash.Always for high-value systems (many planets, luxury resources, strong FIDSI potential)
PillageLarge one-time Dust loot drop; system returns to neutrality (not occupied)System is available to re-colonize later ‚Äî or an opponent might claim it. No permanent control.When you're in a war of economic attrition and need Dust now; or when the system is not worth the Approval cost of occupation
RazeAll buildings on every planet are destroyed; system left as uninhabitable ruins temporarilySystem is permanently weakened ‚Äî future colonizer (you or opponent) starts from zero with no improvements. The planet slots remain but all development is gone.Only when you cannot hold the system AND don't want an opponent to benefit from its development. A "scorched earth" play.
HANDLING THE OCCUPATION APPROVAL CRASH
Occupying a system collapses its local Approval ‚Äî often to 20‚Äì30%. This means all the FIDSI in that system runs at severe penalty for many turns. Before you invade, pre-position a Counselor Hero to assign as governor the moment occupation happens. The Counselor's Tier 4 skill (available at 12 skill points) forces the system's Approval to Ecstatic regardless of base penalties. Combined with 1‚Äì2 Happiness improvements built immediately after occupation, the system becomes productive within 5‚Äì10 turns instead of 30+.
INVASION TACTICS: BLITZ vs. PROTECT vs. DRAFT
During the ground battle resolution screen, you also select an invasion tactic:
Blitz: Maximizes damage dealt but takes higher casualties. Best when you have overwhelming Manpower advantage.
Protect: Minimizes your casualties at the cost of slower progress. Best when your Manpower reserve is limited and you need to preserve troops for future invasions.
Draft: Conscripts local population as troops mid-battle, reducing civilian population but adding troop strength. Best used when you're narrowly losing the ground battle and need emergency reinforcement ‚Äî but be aware it permanently reduces the system's population by the drafted amount.

Behemoths: Types, Roles & Strategic Uses

The most powerful ships in the game ‚Äî and most players never build any of them
üåë What a Behemoth Is and How to Unlock One MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Behemoths are a special ship class ‚Äî larger than any standard ship hull ‚Äî unlocked by researching "Behemoth Construction" in the Military tech tree (Stage 4). Unlike standard ships, a Behemoth has no fixed role: instead it has a large pool of modular slots that accept mission-specific equipment packs called Behemoth Specializations. You build a Behemoth hull, then equip it with one of four specialization kits that determine its entire function.

Only one Behemoth can exist in your fleet at a time (by default). It moves with the fleet and participates in combat, but its value is usually in its out-of-combat capabilities.

The four Behemoth types:

TypeSpecialization ModulesPrimary UseWhen to Build
JuggernautMassive weapon arrays, extra Flak, reinforced hull beyond any standard shipPure combat ‚Äî adds a fifth combat ship to your fleet with hull HP dwarfing Large shipsSupremacy or Conquest wars where fleet combat is constant
ObliteratorPlanetary bombardment arrays, enhanced siege modules, population reduction beamsDestroy system defenses faster than Coordinator siege, and optionally reduce enemy system populationsBefore major Conquest invasions ‚Äî 1‚Äì2 turns of Obliterator siege removes what would take 5+ Coordinator turns
CitadelDefensive turrets, fleet repair arrays, Flak walls, support weaponsTurns a system into a forward operating base; provides fleet-wide repair between battlesHolding contested chokepoints or supporting a long campaign far from home territory
EconomicFIDSI multiplier modules, system output amplifiers, resource generation boostsOrbiting an underdeveloped system to massively boost its FIDSI output while stationed thereEarly-to-mid game when developing new colonies ‚Äî the FIDSI bonus while orbiting can be larger than several turns of improvements
üí° The Economic Behemoth ‚Äî The Most Underbuilt Ship in the Game ADVANCED ‚ñæ

Of the four types, the Economic Behemoth is the least intuitive and the most neglected ‚Äî and probably the highest-value option for non-Supremacy victory paths. Here's why:

  • HOW IT WORKSThe Economic Behemoth's specialization modules apply a percentage multiplier to all FIDSI production in the system it's currently orbiting. The bonus is active only while the Behemoth is stationed there ‚Äî it's a mobile FIDSI amplifier. The multiplier stacks with System Development bonuses, Governor hero bonuses, and percentage-multiplier buildings.
  • ROTATION STRATEGYBecause the bonus follows the ship, you can rotate the Economic Behemoth between your most important systems on a schedule. Spend 5 turns orbiting your primary Industry system (completing ships faster), then 5 turns over your Science hub (compressing tech timelines), then 5 turns over a newly colonized system to accelerate its early development. One Behemoth serving three systems is more efficient than three separate empire improvements.
  • COMPOUNDINGThe FIDSI multiplier from the Behemoth stacks multiplicatively with System Development level bonuses. A system at Level 3 System Development already has large percentage bonuses applied. The Behemoth's multiplier sits on top of that already-amplified base. On a fully developed system, the Behemoth's presence can add more FIDSI per turn than any buildable improvement.
  • TIMINGBuild the Economic Behemoth as soon as Military Stage 4 is researched if you're pursuing Science, Economy, or Score victory. For Conquest, build the Obliterator first. For Supremacy all-out war, the Juggernaut. But if you're not in active fleet combat, the Economic Behemoth is almost always the correct choice.

Behemoth Terraforming: Planet Type Conversion

Transforming planets permanently upgrades systems ‚Äî removing anomalies as a side effect
üåç How Terraforming Works MECHANIC ‚ñæ

An Economic or a specially-configured Behemoth can carry Terraforming Module equipment (unlocked via Science & Exploration Stage 4 tech). When orbiting a system, the Terraforming module opens an action menu showing every planet in the system and the conversion paths available for each. You select a target planet and a new planet type, then the Behemoth begins a multi-turn conversion process (typically 5‚Äì10 turns depending on conversion distance in the "planet type tree").

When terraforming completes:

  • TYPE CHANGEThe planet's base FIDSI profile changes completely to match the new type. A Barren planet converted to Fertile now produces Food-heavy output instead of its old profile. A Tundra converted to Arctic gives Science output instead of marginal Food.
  • ANOMALY CLEARANCEAll anomalies specific to the old planet type are removed automatically. This is an alternative to Anomaly Correction tech ‚Äî and it's a stronger one, because you simultaneously upgrade the planet's base output. If you have a heavily anomaly-damaged planet and the Behemoth available, terraforming is better than building Anomaly Correction improvements.
  • NEW CURIOSITIESAfter conversion, the planet receives new curiosity slots appropriate to its new type. These can contain positive anomalies, resources, or unique modules. Terraforming effectively "resets" the planet's curiosity history, giving you a fresh set of potential bonuses to investigate.
  • POP HAPPINESSPopulation already living on the planet may experience an Approval shift ‚Äî they now live on a planet type that may or may not match their racial preference. Some pop types will become happier (e.g., Sophon pops moved onto an Arctic planet after terraforming love it). Others may become less happy. Check racial preferences before selecting a target planet type.
BEST TERRAFORMING TARGETS
Priority targets for terraforming:
1. Barren planets ‚Üí Arctic (unlock Science output in your Science system)
2. Any planet with severe negative anomalies that would cost many turns of Anomaly Correction industry
3. Lava planets near population-heavy systems ‚Üí Fertile (expand population cap and Food)
4. Any planet whose type conflicts with its assigned population's racial preference ‚Äî fixing the type can give a permanent per-pop Approval bonus worth more than most improvements.

Turn 30 Assessment: Committing to a Victory Path

The most important single decision you'll make ‚Äî and the framework to make it correctly
üìä The Turn 30 Decision Framework STRATEGY ‚ñæ

Around turn 30, you have enough information to make an informed victory path commitment. Here's the four-question framework:

  • Q1What is my galaxy density? Open the galaxy map. Count the total systems and estimate what percentage you currently control. If the map is dense with systems and you've already claimed 25%+, Conquest is viable. If systems are sparse and spread out, Conquest will take too long ‚Äî pivot to Science or Economic.
  • Q2What is my FIDSI profile? Open F3. If your Science per turn is already above 200 and growing fast, Science victory is your natural path. If your Dust income is extremely high from Trade Companies, Economic is reachable. If your Industry is dominant and you have multiple Military-research completions, Conquest or Wonder are viable.
  • Q3Who is my strongest opponent and what are they pursuing? Check F5 scores. If the leading opponent is racing Science, either race them or block them ‚Äî don't ignore the leaderboard. Committing to Economic victory while someone is 70% through Science is a path to losing.
  • Q4What political party am I able to lock in power? Your dominant political party determines which empire-wide laws you can entrench. Entrenched Scientists (+Science%) push you toward Science victory. Entrenched Pacifists (+Dust) push Economic. Match your political lock-in to your intended path ‚Äî they should compound each other, not conflict.
COMMIT HARD AND STOP HEDGING
The most common mid-game failure mode is "hedging" ‚Äî keeping resources invested in multiple victory conditions simultaneously out of indecision. Science victory requires all 4 tech quadrants at Stage 5. Economic requires sustained Dust investment. These are incompatible with also building massive conquest fleets. After turn 30, pick one path and redirect 80% of your decision-making toward it. The remaining 20% can hedge against opponents interfering.
