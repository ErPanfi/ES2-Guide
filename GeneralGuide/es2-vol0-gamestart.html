Game Start
Essentials

The choices you make in the first 20 turns compound into everything that follows. This volume is the on-ramp: how to read the FIDSI indicators, how scouting and probes actually work, how to engage with minor factions and heroes early, how the political system operates from turn one, how to navigate the four tech trees without wasting science, and how to recognise which victory condition you're actually building toward. Deep mechanical treatment of each system lives in later volumes ‚Äî this is what you need to not fall behind irreversibly before you get there.

01

The First 20 Turns: Priority Order

Industry is your foundation. Everything else depends on how fast you build it.
‚ö° The Core Opening Logic MECHANIC ‚ñæ

The single most important resource in the early game is Industry. Without it, you cannot build improvements, which means you cannot specialize your systems for Food, Science, Dust, or Influence ‚Äî and you cannot build ships. Every turn spent at low Industry is a turn where every other FIDSI output is also suppressed. The player who establishes an Industry base early will rapidly outpace one who doesn't, even if the slower player did something "productive" in those turns instead.

The near-universal first research priority across factions is Xenolinguistics, because it unlocks the Xeno-Industrial Infrastructure improvement ‚Äî a flat Industry bonus that on a Temperate or Fertile planet effectively doubles your home system's Industry output in one build. This is true regardless of your intended victory condition. Research you can't build is not progress. Build the Industry base first, then build toward your goals.

COMMON MISTAKE
Rushing Dust or Science improvements before establishing Industry. Dust and Science feel urgent because their numbers are visible and low ‚Äî but Industry determines how fast you can build anything, including the buildings that generate Dust and Science. Fix Industry first.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
üìã Turn-by-Turn Priority Framework STRATEGY ‚ñæ

This is a faction-agnostic baseline. Specific factions will deviate ‚Äî Vodyani, Riftborn, and Cravers have very different opening constraints ‚Äî but most majors follow this skeleton closely.

  • T1Start Xenolinguistics in the research queue. Queue Xeno-Industrial Infrastructure in your home system's construction queue. Send your starting explorer outward along the best star lane ‚Äî you need system information before committing your colony ship.
  • T1Assign your starting hero to the home system as governor, not into a fleet. Governors gain XP from every completed system improvement. Three to four builds in the first 15 turns can push a hero to level 4‚Äì5 before you need them elsewhere. The XP gains from governing an active system far exceed what an unexperienced explorer fleet provides in the same window.
  • T1Check your starting approval and decide on your first law. Many factions start at Happy (70‚Äì84% approval), which means a moderate boost ‚Äî but the Toys for Boys law (available from the Industrialist party at game start) has a specific consideration: it imposes an Industry penalty in exchange for a Science and Dust bonus. If your Industry base is already weak, do not take it. The Cram Exam Act (Scientist party) is generally the better early pick if you want a Science edge without the Industry tradeoff.
  • T3‚Äì6Scout before colonizing. Your colony ship should move toward candidate systems but not commit until you know what's there. A 1-planet system one hop away is almost always worse than a 4‚Äì5 planet system 3 hops out, even accounting for the delay. Each colonization costs Approval empire-wide ‚Äî make each one count.
  • T6‚Äì10Get Xeno-Industrial Infrastructure built, then immediately queue Public-Private Partnerships (the Science tree equivalent) if Science is a priority, or start developing your colony ship and second explorer. You want two scouts mapping simultaneously as quickly as feasible.
  • T10‚Äì15Spend Influence on minor factions near your borders as soon as you find them. The relationship grows passively once you start investing. Early Friendly status secures their system against opponents and begins yielding their tribute bonuses (manpower, Science, Dust, or other outputs depending on the minor faction type).
  • T15‚Äì20Reassess your hero assignment. If your home system is well-developed and a second system needs a governor bonus, transition your hero. If a military or exploration role is needed, move them to a fleet ‚Äî but only after the early governor XP gains are secured.
APPROVAL AWARENESS
Approval is a FIDSI multiplier ‚Äî but Industry is exempt. Food and Influence are affected at the system level; Dust and Science are affected at the empire level. Breakpoints are at 30, 70, and 85: being at 31% and 69% produces identical output. Dropping below 70% loses the +15% Dust/Science bonus; dropping below 30% applies a ‚àí15% malus on top. Design your early colonization and law choices to stay above 70% minimum, and above 85% if feasible. Full detail in Vol.2.

FIDSI: What It Is and Why It Matters

Five indicators. Two local, three global. All of them competing for your attention. ‚Üí Full treatment in Vol.1
üìä The Five Indicators Explained MECHANIC ‚ñæ

FIDSI stands for Food, Industry, Dust, Science, Influence. These five outputs are what your empire produces each turn. They divide into two categories: Food and Industry are local ‚Äî they are calculated per system and cannot be shared across systems. Dust, Science, and Influence are global ‚Äî they are summed empire-wide and available to you regardless of where they were generated.

IndicatorWhat It DoesKey Early Priority
üåæ Food Grows population in its system. Each new population unit requires a threshold of Food accumulated. Some Food is also converted to Manpower (used in ground invasions). Medium ‚Äî enables pop growth which scales all other outputs
‚öôÔ∏è Industry Builds improvements and ships. All construction in a system is paid from that system's Industry output. Cannot be transferred between systems. Highest ‚Äî the foundation everything else requires
üí∞ Dust Your currency. Pays upkeep for buildings, heroes, and fleets. Used in the Marketplace to buy strategic and luxury resources, ships, and heroes. Also used to buy out construction queues if you research the relevant technology. Low-medium ‚Äî needs to be positive; don't neglect upkeep
üî¨ Science Funds research. Your science-per-turn determines how fast you move through the technology tree. Science output is empire-wide ‚Äî all your systems contribute to one shared research pool. Medium ‚Äî Xenolinguistics is T1; after that, match research speed to your build capacity
‚ú® Influence Spent on diplomacy: senate actions, interacting with minor factions (praise, bribe, assist quests), proposing agreements with major factions, declaring war or truces. Medium ‚Äî critical for minor faction diplomacy in turns 1‚Äì20
POPULATION MULTIPLIER
Each population unit assigned to a planet produces the base FIDSI of that planet type. More population means more total output ‚Äî which is why Food (which grows population) is indirectly one of the most important early indicators even though its direct output seems modest. A system with 5 pops producing 20 Industry each yields 100 Industry. The same system with 2 pops produces 40. Population is your production multiplier.

The system management screen (click any colonized system) shows you base production per planet. Press Space in that screen to see each planet's output per population ‚Äî an easy way to decide where to place population for maximum output in a given FIDSI. You can also press Space at the galaxy map level for an empire-wide summary.

UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
‚ö†Ô∏è Approval: The Hidden Multiplier MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Approval is not a sixth FIDSI indicator ‚Äî it is a multiplier, but it does not affect all five outputs equally. Industry is entirely exempt from Approval modifiers. The effect operates in two separate domains:

System Approval affects Food and Influence output for that individual system. Empire Approval (a population-weighted average across all systems) affects Dust and Science empire-wide. The breakpoints and their multipliers are:

ThresholdSystem (Food & Influence)Empire (Dust & Science)
0 ‚Äî Mutinous / Traitorous‚àí75%‚àí30%
10 ‚Äî Unhappy / Defiant‚àí30%‚àí15%
30 ‚Äî ContentNo modifierNo modifier
70 ‚Äî Happy / Loyal+10%+15%
85 ‚Äî Ecstatic / Devoted+25%+30%

There is no gradient between breakpoints ‚Äî 31% and 69% approval produce identical output. This is why colonizing too aggressively can actively weaken your existing empire: each new system drags Empire Approval down, and if it crosses below 70% you lose the Loyal bonus on all Dust and Science empire-wide. Industry is unaffected throughout. Full mechanical detail is in Vol.2.

The First Steps Out of Home: Scouting & Probes

Information before commitment. ‚Üí Full treatment in Vol.3
üî≠ How Probes Work MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Exploration ships have a special module that launches probes. Probes are fired into space and travel for a limited number of turns (their "lifetime"), revealing systems and anything in their path within their detection range. Once used, probes have a cooldown before they regenerate ‚Äî the cooldown depends on the probe type. You start with basic probes; improved probes (larger range, faster travel, longer lifetime) are unlocked via the Science & Exploration tech tree.

Probes serve two functions: map revelation (fire into space to reveal star lanes and systems along a vector) and curiosity investigation (send a probe to a specific curiosity marker on a planet or system to collect its reward). The curiosity investigation function is the most high-value use early on ‚Äî each investigated curiosity can yield resources, loot, Influence, or population, and also generates XP for any hero whose fleet contains the probe source.

HERO + SCOUT SYNERGY
Attaching a hero to your scout fleet means the hero gains XP for every curiosity their fleet investigates, even if the probe came from a scout ship in the same fleet rather than the hero's own ship. This is the fastest XP source in the early game ‚Äî each curiosity gives a meaningful XP chunk. Keep your hero with scouts for the first 8 turns or so (Normal speed), then reassign to governor once the early curiosity pool is exhausted.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
üöÄ Scout Ship Design: Probes vs. Speed STRATEGY ‚ñæ

There is a useful distinction between two scouting roles. Probe-heavy scouts (exploration chassis loaded with probe launchers) are ideal for investigating curiosities in and around your home cluster ‚Äî they carry more probes per turn, can investigate multiple curiosities before running dry, and regenerate faster in place. Speed scouts (colonizer or other chassis loaded with engines, no probes) move across the galaxy extremely fast, revealing the map and locating opponents, minor factions, and chokepoints. They cannot investigate curiosities.

The optimal early game typically uses both: probe-heavy explorers covering your home cluster and newly claimed systems, fast-moving hulls racing out the star lanes to claim geographic information before opponents do. Once you've mapped your immediate neighborhood, the fast scouts are still useful in the late game for locating enemy home systems and providing early warning of incoming fleets.

MOVEMENT TIMING
When a ship moves to a destination that takes more than one turn, it completes the final leg at end-of-turn processing, not at the start of your next turn. This means if you want to arrive and act in the same turn (attack a pirate, investigate a curiosity), you must move manually rather than relying on the auto-path completion. If you let the path auto-complete, you arrive at end-of-turn and lose the action window for that turn.

Minor Factions: First Contact & Early Gains

Free resources, free pop, free strategic depth ‚Äî if you engage before opponents do. ‚Üí Full treatment in Vol.3
ü§ù How Minor Faction Relations Work MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Minor factions are single-system civilizations scattered across the galaxy. They have a diplomatic relation bar that runs from 0 to 100. As you raise this bar using Influence (via Praise actions or Assist quests), they begin yielding passive bonuses ‚Äî typically a mix of Manpower, Science, Dust, or Influence per turn based on the specific minor faction type. At 100 relation you gain the option to assimilate them, incorporating their system into your empire and their population type into your gene pool.

You can also assimilate them aggressively through military conquest ‚Äî declare war, land troops, take the system. This is faster if you already have a military presence and the relation is low, but it costs the diplomatic approach's free bonuses and draws Approval and diplomatic penalties. Peaceful assimilation via Influence is almost always the better early-game path.

ActionCostEffect
PraiseInfluence (scales up each use)+Relation points. Fast and repeatable but increasingly expensive.
Assist (Quest)Complete a quest objectiveLarge relation jump on completion. Free if the quest objective aligns with what you're doing anyway.
Investigate CuriositiesProbes (free)Large relation jump per curiosity investigated in their system. The cheapest and fastest lever in the early game.
Assimilate (Influence)High Influence cost, requires 100 relationGains their system and population. Peaceful, no Approval penalty.
Assimilate (Military)Manpower + fleet + Approval hitGains their system immediately. Bypasses relation requirement but costs military resources and triggers Approval penalties.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
üéØ Early-Game Minor Faction Priorities STRATEGY ‚ñæ

Even without assimilation, an Allied minor faction (relation at or near 100) yields passive bonuses every turn, and their population will naturally migrate into your nearest systems over time. That migration is valuable: each minor faction pop type has different FIDSI affinities and political party alignment, which matters for both production optimization and Senate control.

  • 01Investigate their planet curiosities first ‚Äî each gives a large, free relation jump. If you have an explorer in the area, this is the cheapest path to Friendly status available.
  • 02Check what they produce before investing Influence. Minor factions that provide Science, Dust, or Influence per turn are typically higher priority than pure Manpower providers in the early game unless you're planning an aggressive expansion.
  • 03Secure border-adjacent minor factions early, regardless of bonus quality. A minor faction at Allied status on your border prevents opponents from claiming that system through diplomacy ‚Äî they'd have to use military force instead, which costs them resources and time, and gives you warning.
  • 04Note their political alignment. Minor faction pops that end up in your systems vote in your Senate elections according to their faction's party affinity. This can be used intentionally to reinforce the party you want in power.
PIRATE INTERACTION
If Pirates are enabled, they spawn from minor faction systems. The minor faction's location and your relation level affect how pirate activity spreads into your space. At high relation, you can sometimes leverage the minor faction's own defense ships. At low or zero relation, their system is a pirate spawn point that you have no leverage over.

Heroes: Early Leveling & Quick XP

Your starting hero's first 15 turns define their usefulness for the rest of the game. ‚Üí Full treatment in Vol.5
ü¶∏ XP Sources and the Early Leveling Window MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Heroes gain XP from three main sources: governing a system (XP trickles in each turn they are assigned as governor), fleet actions (battles, and crucially ‚Äî investigating curiosities while assigned to a fleet), and exploring new nodes (your hero gains XP the first time their fleet enters a previously undiscovered system). Curiosity investigation XP is especially high-value in the early game because curiosities are abundant and the hero-to-scout attachment costs nothing to set up.

The practical opening decision is therefore: scout with your hero for the first several turns to stack curiosity XP, then transition to governor. A Sophons guide in the KB notes that exploring two curiosities at 6 XP each (with a 25% hero XP bonus) yields ~15 XP by turn 2 ‚Äî enough for the first skill unlock almost immediately. Against that, a hero sitting idle on the home system waiting for improvements to complete earns less XP per turn until the build queue becomes active enough to provide meaningful governor ticks.

SEQUENCING TIP (Normal Speed)
A common recommendation from experienced players: attach your hero to the scout fleet for approximately the first 8 turns to capture curiosity XP, then reassign to the home system as governor. This gives you level 2‚Äì3 early enough to invest a meaningful skill before the midgame governor XP kicks in. Exact timing varies by faction and starting system.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
üéì Hero Classes and Your Starting Hero MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Heroes come in four broad archetypes that shape their skill tree specializations. Before you hire or reassign a hero, look at their class and their four starting skills to understand what role they're designed for:

ClassSpecializationBest Assignment
GuardianFleet warfare and industrial productionCombat fleet or high-Industry system
OverseerSystemwide resource bonuses and fleet developmentGovernor of a high-value system
SeekerExploration, movement, probe bonusesScout fleet ‚Äî especially early game
CounselorTrade, diplomacy, empire-wide influence bonusesGovernor or senate-influencing role

Your starting hero's class should inform your opening assignment. An Overseer-class starting hero is typically best placed immediately as governor. A Seeker-class hero may be worth keeping in the scout fleet longer for exploration bonuses. The key principle: don't assign a hero to a role their skill tree doesn't support ‚Äî a combat-specialized Guardian assigned as governor will level up but invest points in skills that do nothing for system output.

The Political System: The First Laws

The Senate screen is not optional. Your first law is worth reading carefully. ‚Üí Full treatment in Vol.2
üèõÔ∏è Senate Mechanics Overview MECHANIC ‚ñæ

The Senate screen (click the Senate icon in the top UI) is where political parties, laws, government types, and elections are managed. Each system in your empire sends representatives to the Senate based on its population composition. The party with the most representatives leads, and the leading party's default law activates automatically. You can also pass additional laws up to a limit determined by your government type ‚Äî between 2 and 6 active laws depending on government.

Elections happen every 10‚Äì20 turns depending on game speed. Between elections, parties gain experience the longer they hold seats. That experience unlocks progressively stronger laws: Level 1 laws are cheap and available from turn 1; Level 2‚Äì4 laws require the party to have held Senate positions long enough to accumulate the required political experience. This means there is a compounding value to keeping a party in power consistently ‚Äî their laws get better the longer they stay.

POPULATION AND POLITICS
Every population type has a natural affinity for certain political parties. The populations you grow, assimilate from minor factions, or acquire through conquest directly shape your Senate composition. Planning which minor faction populations you want in your systems is partly a political planning exercise, not just a FIDSI one.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
üìú The First Laws: What's Worth Passing STRATEGY ‚ñæ

At game start, the parties available depend on your faction's default population type and starting government. Most factions will have access to Industrialist and Scientist laws at minimum. The standout options for turn 1‚Äì10:

  • INDUSTRIALISTMineral Miseries Act (Level 2): Gives +1 research generation on strategic resource deposits and +15% Industry on strategic resource deposits. Strongly positive if you have strategic resources in your home system or nearby colonies. One of the best early laws if your starting systems have good strategic resource coverage.
  • INDUSTRIALISTPollution: Not Our Problem (Level 3): Gives a flat Industry bonus per population empire-wide. At 125 population this can be +1000 Industry across your empire ‚Äî a massive number. Requires the Industrialist party to have reached Level 3 political experience, so it's not turn-1 but is a medium-term goal if Industrialists are your dominant party.
  • SCIENTISTCram Exam Act (Level 1): Increases Science but reduces Approval per population. If your Approval is comfortably above 85%, this is a free Science bonus. If you're struggling with Approval, avoid it.
  • SCIENTISTMovement bonuses and reduced improvement costs are also available in the Scientist tree ‚Äî worth reviewing if exploration or rapid construction are your current priorities.
  • RELIGIOUSPacifist Codex (locks Approval to Content): Forces empire-wide Approval to a fixed level ‚Äî Content minimum (or Happy if you're a Republic). This is an extremely powerful tool for aggressive expanders who would otherwise suffer severe Approval penalties from rapid colonization. It frees you from Approval management entirely and lets you focus on growth. One of the strongest early laws in the game if you can access it.
TOYS FOR BOYS
The Industrialist default law (Toys for Boys) gives +Dust and +Science bonuses at the cost of an Industry penalty. If your Industry base is weak ‚Äî which it almost certainly is in the first 10 turns ‚Äî this law actively slows your construction rate. Many experienced players explicitly avoid it early. The Cram Exam Act (Scientist) is generally the better trade if you want an output boost in those turns.

The 4 Tech Trees: Navigation & First Research Priorities

Every tech you research makes all future techs more expensive. Don't research what you can't use. ‚Üí Per-tree deep dives in Vol.1‚Äì4
üó∫Ô∏è How the Tech Tree Works MECHANIC ‚ñæ

The tech tree is a circular structure divided into four quadrants (Military, Economy & Trade, Science & Exploration, Empire Development) each broken into five rings. To access Ring 2 in a quadrant, you must research a set number of techs in Ring 1 of that same quadrant ‚Äî progress in one quadrant does not unlock rings in another. Each quadrant has its own unlock progression.

When you unlock a new ring within a quadrant, you also receive a set of free technologies ‚Äî unlocked automatically just for reaching that ring level. These free techs include things like ship modules, buildings, and combat upgrades that you receive without spending Science. They are a core part of the tree's value structure and worth checking before deciding which ring to unlock next.

THE COST SCALING RULE
Every technology you research increases the cost of all subsequent techs, across all quadrants. Researching a tech you won't use for 30 turns is not neutral ‚Äî it makes every useful tech you want more expensive right now. Research only what you plan to use immediately or in the near term. This is the single most important efficiency principle in the tech system.

Looking at the Horizon: Victory Conditions

Six ways to win. You need to decide which one you're building toward ‚Äî some require mid-game commitment points that cannot be reversed.
üèÜ The Six Victory Conditions MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Victory conditions can be toggled on and off in the game setup but are fixed once the game begins. All active victory conditions run simultaneously ‚Äî any faction (including you) can win by any active condition. The game immediately warns you when any faction or alliance reaches 50% and 75% of the threshold for any condition. Ignoring those warnings is fatal.

ConditionRequirementKey Constraint
‚öîÔ∏è Conquest Control a set number of star systems (varies by galaxy size ‚Äî e.g. 22 on a Normal galaxy) Being in an alliance increases the threshold ‚Äî your ally's systems count toward the requirement. Suitable for expansionist factions (United Empire, Cravers, Nakalim). Hissho and Umbral Choir are typically discouraged from this path due to their tall-play affinities.
üëë Supremacy Control all major factions' home systems Alliance members' home systems count as progress. Vaulters' "home system" is their first-colonized system, not a fixed location. Umbral Choir can achieve this by placing Sanctuaries at all home systems without military conquest.
üî¨ Science Research all four Endless Technologies: Might of the Endless (Military+Empire), Wealth of the Endless (Military+Economy), Power of the Endless (Science+Empire), Genius of the Endless (Science+Economy) Each requires three Tier 5 techs from its two quadrants to unlock. Alliance membership increases the number required. Best for Sophons, Riftborn, Vaulters. Nakalim are especially penalized on this path.
üí∞ Economic Accumulate a set total Dust amount over the entire game (e.g. 450,000 Dust on Normal speed, Normal galaxy size) This is cumulative Dust earned, not Dust in hand. Best for Lumeris. Industrialist and Militarist factions can also pursue it efficiently.
üèóÔ∏è Wonder Build a set number of Obelisks of All Space-Time (2‚Äì5 depending on galaxy size) Obelisks are unlocked only after reaching Empire Development Ring 5. The first Obelisk costs 20,000 Industry + 125 Titanium + 125 Hyperium + 80 Adamantian + 80 Antimatter + 40 Orichalcix + 40 Quadrinix ‚Äî each subsequent Obelisk costs 50% more. Obelisks can be captured from other factions. Best for high-Industry factions (Riftborn, United Empire).
üìä Score Have the highest score when the turn limit runs out (150/300/450/600 turns on Fast/Normal/Slow/Endless) Score is calculated from population, systems, system levels, tech researched, hero levels, quests, laws, battles won, and wonders built. A fallback condition that rewards balanced play. All factions are equally positioned for it.

üéØ Commitment Windows and Early Signals STRATEGY ‚ñæ

Not all victories require the same level of early commitment. Understanding when you need to start actively building toward each condition determines how much flexibility you retain in the opening game.

  • CONQUESTCommitment starts at game start. Geographic position is established in turns 1‚Äì20. If you don't secure key star lane positions and system counts early, catching up requires military action later ‚Äî which is far more expensive in Industry, Manpower, and Dust than colonization.
  • SUPREMACYMedium-early commitment. You need enough military strength to threaten or capture home systems, which typically requires mid-game fleet development. The strategic decision of which home systems to target should happen by turn 30‚Äì40.
  • SCIENCEEarly investment in Science output and tech tree progression. The four Endless Technologies each require three Tier 5 techs from specific quadrant pairs ‚Äî meaning you must reach Ring 5 in those quadrants before you can even begin researching the Endless techs. This is a long commitment chain. Start pushing Science immediately if this is your path.
  • ECONOMICModerate early commitment. Dust accumulates over the entire game ‚Äî strong early Dust income compounds dramatically. Lumeris should essentially always default to this path; other factions need a dedicated Dust strategy (trade routes, marketplace, trade company tech) to reach the threshold at competitive speed.
  • WONDERLate commitment point but requires early setup. Empire Development Ring 5 is the gating unlock ‚Äî you must push this quadrant deliberately. Strategic resource accumulation (Titanium, Hyperium, Adamantian, Antimatter, Orichalcix, Quadrinix) must begin early because each Obelisk requires large quantities of all six. Begin stockpiling by mid-game.
  • SCORENo specific commitment needed ‚Äî Score rewards everything. The danger is being outpaced in any category. If you're not winning by turn 200 and Score is active, check which components you're losing: systems, tech rings, population, or quests.
ALLIANCE TRAP
Forming an alliance increases the requirements for Conquest, Supremacy, and Science victories simultaneously. Unless you're deliberately coordinating a joint win with your ally, or using a temporary alliance for protection and then breaking it, alliances tend to make victory conditions harder to achieve. The Supremacy victory specifically requires all remaining major faction home systems ‚Äî your ally's home system counts, meaning you'd have to eventually betray them or coordinate a joint win.
