Ships, Combat & Military

The military layer isn't just about who has the biggest fleet. It's about timing, weapon matching, hull size leverage, and knowing when to take the fight vs. when to siege. This volume covers every mechanical layer from ship design to ground invasion.

01

The Military Tech Tree

What it unlocks, what it doesn't, and the unlock dependency you must not miss
üî¨ Structure and Role of the Military Tree MECHANIC ‚ñæ

The Military tree is one of four research quadrants. Its primary deliverables are weapon modules, defense modules, Command Point increases, and battle tactic sets. It does not unlock ship hulls ‚Äî those come from the Empire Development tree. This split is the most common early-game trap: players who invest in Military tech before unlocking the appropriate hull from Empire Development end up with strong modules sitting on weak, small hulls.

CRITICAL DEPENDENCY
Ship hull upgrades live in Empire Development, not Military. Researching Military Tier 2‚Äì3 modules before you have the corresponding Empire Development hull (Hunter, Coordinator, Carrier) means you're paying Science to improve small Attacker/Protector hulls that have 2‚Äì3√ó fewer effective module slots than their medium/large equivalents. Always check which hull you can currently equip before deciding where to invest Science.

The Military tree also unlocks Manpower buildings (Flash Camps: +2000 Manpower Capacity), and Zero-Lag Command / Pervasive C5AI improvements that expand your fleet CP ceiling empire-wide. These CP upgrades are passive and apply to all fleets ‚Äî they are among the highest-value Military techs per Science-spent because they apply a multiplier to every fleet you own.

WHAT MILITARY DOES NOT PROVIDE
Strategic resource exploitation abilities for advanced modules are in the Economy & Trade tree, not Military. If you need Titanium or Hyperium to equip upgraded modules, you're looking at the wrong tree. Similarly, Behemoth Blueprints (the base Behemoth unlock) is in Empire Development Era 2 ‚Äî Military only provides Juggernaut and Obliterator blueprints (both Military Era 4).

Building a Ship: Slots, Modules & Resources

Hull size is the multiplier. Modules are what you multiply.
üõ†Ô∏è Hull Sizes, CP Cost and the Module Multiplier MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Every ship hull has a size class (Small, Medium, Large) which determines its CP cost, base HP, and ‚Äî critically ‚Äî a module multiplier that amplifies the effectiveness of every module installed on it.

Hull SizeCP CostModule MultiplierTypical RolesApprox Base HP
Small 1 CP √ó1 Attacker, Protector, Explorer, Colonizer 1,500‚Äì4,000
Medium 3 CP √ó2 Hunter, Coordinator 6,000‚Äì8,500
Large 6 CP √ó4 Carrier 14,000

The module multiplier is the most misunderstood mechanic in ship design. A single weapon module on a Large hull provides the same firepower as four of the same module on a Small hull ‚Äî but costs 6 CP instead of 4 CP (four Small ships). The trade-off is efficiency vs. concentration: larger hulls deal more damage per module, but they're harder to replace and lose their full contribution when destroyed. The practical implication: always match your weapon tech tier to your current hull tier. Using Tier 2 modules on Tier 3 hulls (Hunter, Coordinator) is a significant upgrade that doesn't require any new research.

THE EFFECTIVE MODULE COUNT RULE
Count modules by multiplied value, not raw count. A Hunter (Medium, √ó2) with 4 weapon slots has 8 effective weapon slots. A Carrier (Large, √ó4) with 3 weapon slots has 12 effective weapon slots. When comparing ships, always apply the multiplier before drawing conclusions about which ship packs more punch.

The counter relationships matter in practice:

  • KINETIC counters SQUADRONSFighters and bombers are also destroyed by Flak. Without any kinetic weapons in your fleet, enemy squadrons from Coordinator/Carrier hulls operate unopposed.

PRACTICAL WEAPON PROGRESSION
Early (Military Era 1‚Äì2): Kinetic Slugs are best. They match most combat scenarios (short-to-medium range engagements) and provide Flak protection against pirates and other early threats that commonly use missiles.

Mid (Military Era 3+): Transition primary slots to Lasers. Lasers have nearly double the DPS of same-tier Beams, cost fewer strategic resources (Hyperium vs. nothing), and have 100% accuracy at medium range ‚Äî the most common range for most battle tactic combinations. Keep at least one Kinetic slot per larger ship for Flak protection.

Late (Military Era 4‚Äì5): Laser primary + Shield Interference Beam on combat heroes (disables shields for 6s on hit) + optional Railguns if you have the DLC. Swarm Missiles (also DLC) overwhelm Flak because each "shot" fires 10 projectiles.

üõ°Ô∏è Defense Modules: Absorption, Penetration & the Half/Half Rule MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Ships have two defense types: Hull Plating (reduces damage to health) and Shields (reduces damage to the shield bar, which regenerates after battle). The damage calculation is multiplicative, not additive:

Absorption = Defense / (Defense + Hull Weakness)

Hull Weakness by size: Small = 100, Medium = 250, Large = 600. This means the first defense module on any hull provides the largest marginal absorption gain ‚Äî subsequent modules of the same type have diminishing returns because Defense is always competing against a fixed Hull Weakness denominator.

Penetration reduces absorption as a percentage of absorption, not as a flat subtraction. If a ship has 80% Shield Absorption and is hit by kinetic weapons (50% Shield Penetration), the effective absorption is 40% ‚Äî not 30%. Even a 20% absorption value retains 10% effective absorption against a 50% penetrating weapon.

Damage done = Weapon damage √ó (1 ‚àí Damage Absorbed by Shield) √ó (1 ‚àí Damage Absorbed by Hull)

THE HALF/HALF DEFENSE RULE
Split defense slots evenly between Hull Plating and Shields. Both types have diminishing returns when stacked ‚Äî a second Plating module adds less marginal absorption than the first did. Mixing both types maximizes coverage against both projectile (hull-facing) and energy (shield-facing) weapons. If you have an odd number of defense slots, favor Hull Plating: ships have more base health than shield value, so the absorption multiplier on hull provides larger absolute damage reduction.

Note: Shields absorb all incoming damage first, then health takes the remainder. Shields regenerate fully after every battle ‚Äî they are your renewable combat buffer. Hull Plating absorption persists throughout the battle, providing ongoing reduction even when shields are down.

Manpower: Generation, Consumption & Troop Types

The sixth FIDSI resource ‚Äî often starved, rarely understood
üë• How Manpower Works MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Manpower is produced by converting a fraction of each system's Food output into empire-wide Manpower stock. The baseline conversion rate comes from the Colonial Base system improvement (10%). Additional Military techs and faction-specific buildings can raise this rate. Conversion stops automatically when the Manpower stock is full.

Manpower is then split two ways: it staffs your ship fleets (giving them a combat damage bonus), and it provides system defense troops (the troops that fight off invaders). Ships replenish their manpower at a fixed rate per turn while inside your influence zone ‚Äî they do not need to be orbiting a specific system. Manpower replenishment stops while a system is under siege or active combat.

MANPOWER DAMAGE BONUS
A ship's current manpower level directly affects its combat damage output. The formula is: Damage Bonus = (Current Ship Manpower / Max Ship Manpower) √ó 20%. A ship at 0 manpower deals 20% less damage than the same ship at full manpower. This is why Umbral Choir (who cannot carry manpower) has a structural combat disadvantage. Never let fleets go into battle significantly under-manned.

‚öôÔ∏è Troop Types: Infantry, Armor & Air MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Your empire's manpower converts to ground troops using a rock-paper-scissors system. The troop distribution is set empire-wide from the Military screen (Manage ‚Üí Troops Breakdown). Changing the distribution costs a small amount of Manpower (25 per 10% shift).

The counters are: Infantry beats Air, Armor beats Infantry, Air beats Armor. Infantry is cheap per manpower cost but has low health ‚Äî ideal for defensive depth where manpower is abundant. Armor has the most health per unit and counters the most common AI troop type (Infantry). Air deals the most raw damage but costs the most manpower per unit and destroys improvements at a higher rate.

ARMOR IS THE DEFAULT META
Most AI factions deploy a majority Infantry force. Armor counters Infantry and has the best health pool. The practical recommendation: set your distribution to 100% Armor once you've unlocked it (requires the Autonomous Construction tech). At the point where you're conducting major invasions, enemy Air units are rare enough that the 100% Armor risk is acceptable. Revisit only if you observe a specific faction running heavy Air.

Troop upgrades cost Dust and strategic resources and unlock via tech milestones. Upgraded Infantry, Armor, and Air gain +20% Health and +20% Damage at Level 1, with further bonuses at Levels 2‚Äì4 requiring progressively rarer strategic resources (Titanium/Hyperium ‚Üí Adamantian/Antimatter ‚Üí Orichalcix/Quadrinix).

Ship Cloaking & Detection

Detection is always one tier harder than cloaking. Plan accordingly.
ü´• How Cloaking Works MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Cloaking modules (Penumbra DLC) are equipped on ships to conceal the entire fleet from enemy vision. A single cloaked ship in a fleet makes the whole fleet invisible. Cloaked fleets can move freely through star lanes, bypass blockades, and ignore defending fleets in orbit ‚Äî the guarding fleet cannot stop them unless it has an anti-cloaking module or the cloaked fleet is detected.

While cloaked, a fleet cannot: use the Guard action, perform an invasion, or take most actions that would reveal its presence. Decloaking costs all remaining movement points for that turn.

CLOAK AMBUSH IN COMBAT
Certain cloaking modules (e.g. Cloak Ambush) allow your fleet to remain invisible during the opening phase of a battle, preventing the enemy from targeting you until you reach a desired range. Combined with the Turtle battle card (pushing to short range), this means your fleet arrives at optimal range without absorbing long-range fire in the process.

Detection asymmetry: Detecting a cloaked fleet at tech tier N requires anti-cloaking tech at tier N+1. A fleet cloaked with Era 4 modules can only be detected by Era 5 anti-cloaking. This means offensive cloaking is structurally easier than defensive detection ‚Äî the cloaker is always one step ahead of the pursuer at equal tech levels.

Detection methods:

  • Anti-Cloaker ModulesEquip on any ship in a fleet. When the fleet is at the same system as a cloaked enemy fleet, the cloaked fleet can be directly targeted and engaged. Without this module, the cloaked fleet can only be pulled into combat incidentally (by attacking a visible fleet in the same system that happens to share a system with the cloaked fleet).
  • Detection ProbesAnti-cloaking probes (unlocked by Antimatter Channeling and Quadrinix Toroids) orbit a system and permanently reveal any cloaked ships entering that system. These are ideal for border defense ‚Äî station one probe per critical system rather than maintaining a standing anti-cloaker fleet.
  • Incidental CombatIf a cloaked fleet shares a system with a visible fleet, and you attack the visible fleet, the cloaked fleet is pulled into combat automatically. This does not require any anti-cloaking tech.

The 3-Phase Combat System & Weapon Ranges

Combat is deterministic. Learn the range resolution table and you know the outcome before the battle starts.
‚öîÔ∏è Phase Structure and Range Resolution MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Every space battle has exactly three phases, each 40 seconds of in-game time. Combat begins with flotillas at the farthest range dictated by either side's battle tactics card. Each phase, if a flotilla's chosen tactic calls for a closer range than the current range, it closes by one increment (Long ‚Üí Medium ‚Üí Short).

The range resolution matrix (S = attacker's target range, opponent = defender's target range):

Attacker\DefenderLong (L)Medium (M)Short (S)
Long (L)L / L / LL / M / ML / M / S
Medium (M)L / M / MM / M / MM / S / S
Short (S)L / M / SM / S / SS / S / S

Read: if the attacker uses a Long-range tactic and the defender uses a Short-range tactic, the three phases play out at Long ‚Üí Medium ‚Üí Short (i.e., the attacker dictates Phase 1 range, and each fleet closes toward their preferred range). Both sides "converge" ‚Äî combat always starts at the farthest of the two preferred ranges and moves toward the closer one.

STRATEGIC IMPLICATION
If you're running a laser-dominant fleet (optimal at Medium), and the enemy chooses a Short-range tactic while you choose Medium, the phases play out M/S/S ‚Äî meaning two of three phases are at sub-optimal range for your lasers. To lock in Medium-range combat, both sides must choose a Medium-range tactic. If you can't control the enemy's tactic, design ships that are effective at multiple ranges (or accept the range variance and account for it with accuracy margins).

üó∫Ô∏è Flotillas, Lanes & Morale MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Your fleet's ships are distributed across up to three lanes (Top, Center, Bottom). Each lane forms a flotilla. Lane access requirements:

  • CENTERAlways available. Must have at least 1 CP of ships.
  • TOP LANERequires at least 5 CP total in your fleet AND at least 2 ships.
  • BOTTOM LANERequires at least 10 CP total in your fleet AND at least 3 ships.

Ships fire at the opposing flotilla in the same lane. If there are no enemies in their lane, they fire cross-lane at an adjacent lane (Top ‚Üî Center, Bottom ‚Üî Center; Top and Bottom cannot fire at each other). Cross-lane fire is always treated as Long range, regardless of the tactic card.

MORALE BONUS
Having more active flotillas (lanes with at least one surviving ship) than the enemy grants a morale advantage: +10% Damage and +5% Accuracy per extra lane, up to a maximum of +20% Damage and +10% Accuracy (two extra lanes). This means letting a flotilla be destroyed early ‚Äî even a small one ‚Äî can hand the enemy a significant damage multiplier for the rest of the battle. Protect all three lanes or go in with a deliberate single-lane concentration strategy.

Ship distribution strategy options in the battle setup screen:

  • OPTIMAL RANGEPlaces each ship in the lane that matches its weapons' optimal range. Maximizes individual ship effectiveness but risks creating an uneven flotilla vulnerable to early destruction.
  • BALANCED (DEFAULT)Balances optimal range placement with even distribution. Generally the safest option ‚Äî prevents a single flotilla from becoming too weak while still accounting for weapon ranges.
  • EVEN DISTRIBUTIONEqualizes CP across all lanes. Maximizes morale advantage chance but may sacrifice individual ship optimization.

üèÉ Retreating, Guarding & Refitting MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Retreat: The defender may retreat before combat. If a valid starlane connecting to a non-hostile system exists, the fleet takes ‚àí60% maximum health damage (destroying any ship already below 60% HP) and begins moving along that lane. If no valid exit lane exists, retreating destroys the entire fleet ‚Äî the game warns you before you confirm. Heroes whose fleet is destroyed in combat return to the Academy injured and unavailable for several turns.

Guard: A fleet assigned to "Guard" a system stops any enemy fleet from passing through ‚Äî the guarded fleet removes all movement points from any enemy entering the system for that turn. Cloaked fleets bypass a Guard unless detected. In Cold War, guarding a system adjacent to an enemy's system imposes ‚àí50% Dust, Science, and Influence on the guarded system and generates negative AI relations.

Refit: Ships can be redesigned and retrofitted at any friendly system. Cost = 30 Dust base + 1 Dust per additional Industry cost of the new design vs. the current one (both adjusted for inflation). Damaged ships must be repaired before refitting, which typically adds significant Dust cost. This means it's often cheaper to maintain up-to-date designs from the start than to retrofit a large fleet mid-war.

Battle Cards

Three types of card, three categories of effect. The range cards define the combat ‚Äî the bonus cards define the outcome.
üÉè Card Slots, Categories & Unlock Path MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Every fleet has battle tactic slots. You begin with 3 slots and can expand to 5 by researching Universal Aerodynamics (Military 4) and Quantum Communications (also Military 4). Each slot holds one card, chosen before combat from your available card pool.

Battle tactics card do two things: set the range your flotillas will attempt to reach, and grant a combat bonus. Cards fall into three bonus categories:

CategoryIconAttacker/Defender SplitEffect Timing
Offense‚ö° Lightning bolt100% Attacker / 0% DefenderDuring combat

The attacker/defender split is crucial: Offense cards only benefit the attacking fleet, Defense cards only benefit the defending fleet. Post-Battle cards benefit both regardless of role.

Your fleet is a system, not a collection of ships. Design the system first.
üöÄ Ship Roles and Targeting Priorities MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Each ship hull has a role that determines which enemy ships it targets preferentially and what combat bonus it grants its flotilla. Understanding these targeting priorities lets you build fleets that operate predictably ‚Äî and counter fleets that don't.

This is the defensive spine of a mixed fleet: Coordinators intercept damage while Hunters output it. An all-Hunter fleet deals more raw damage but is entirely composed of high-value targets that the enemy's focus fire will chew through quickly.

COMPOSITION RULE OF THUMB
The most cost-effective standard fleet for a mid-game military power: 4‚Äì6 Hunter-class ships as primary damage dealers, supplemented by 1‚Äì2 Coordinators as damage sponges, filling remaining CP with smaller ships. This provides three-lane access (10+ CP total), Coordinator-sourced Guardian protection, and concentrated Hunter firepower. Add a Carrier once you've researched Liquid Composites and have Adamantian and Antimatter to afford it.

üéØ Squadrons: When to Use Them STRATEGY ‚ñæ

Coordinator, Carrier, and Behemoth hulls can equip squadron modules (Fighters and Bombers). Squadrons have 100% accuracy at all ranges, are destroyed by enemy Flak or enemy Fighters, and disappear when the battle ends regardless of whether their launch ship is alive.

Bombers deal high DPS with a multiplier against Large/Behemoth hulls (√ó1.5) and a penalty against Small hulls (√ó0.5). Against an enemy running Carriers or a Juggernaut, Bombers deal damage equivalent to top-tier Kinetic weapons ‚Äî without requiring your fleet to close to short range.

Fighters deal low damage to ships, but are the primary counter to enemy Bombers. Even one Fighter module in your fleet forces enemy Bombers to fight through attrition before reaching your ships.

THE FLAK PROBLEM
Squadrons are entirely negated by enemy Flak (generated by kinetic weapons). If the enemy fleet runs even one Kinetic slug module per large ship, your Bombers will take heavy losses before landing damage. Squadrons reward you when the enemy has no kinetics ‚Äî which is increasingly rare past the early game. Default recommendation: treat squadrons as a counter-specific niche, not a default composition element, and ensure your own fleet always runs at least one Kinetic module to maintain Flak protection.

‚ö° Juggernaut: Fleet Anchor & Ion Wave STRATEGY ‚ñæ

The Juggernaut (Military Era 4: Juggernaut Blueprints) is the combat Behemoth specialization designed for fleet engagement. Unlike the base Military Behemoth (which cannot join a regular fleet), the Juggernaut can be embedded inside an existing fleet and fights alongside your smaller ships. This is the critical difference: your Hunter and Coordinator ships protect the Juggernaut while it applies its full √ó4 module multiplier firepower.

The Juggernaut can be upgraded further along two paths:

  • DRAGONUT MODULE UPGRADEAdds more battle modules ‚Äî increases the Juggernaut's weapon slots further, making it an even more dominant DPS platform.
  • ION WAVEAn ability that destroys all enemy ships in a system without conventional combat ‚Äî triggered when enemy fleets are orbiting the same system. Activating Ion Wave damages only enemy fleets, not your own. This effectively negates the need for a conventional battle against prepared defensive fleets.
JUGGERNAUT vs STANDALONE MILITARY BEHEMOTH
The standard Military Behemoth cannot join a fleet and therefore fights without support. Against an equal-value fleet of regular ships, the Behemoth takes concentrated fire and becomes expensive to repair (repairs cost significantly more than replacing smaller ships). The Juggernaut circumvents this by joining your regular fleet ‚Äî your Coordinators absorb fire meant for it while it deals Behemoth-class damage. Prefer the Juggernaut over standalone Military Behemoths for direct fleet combat.

üí• Obliterator & Citadel: System Destruction & Defense MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Obliterator (Military Era 4): Fires a charged shot that destroys an entire star system ‚Äî all improvements, population, and infrastructure. The shot has a multi-turn reload time and a cost in strategic resources (Quadrinics and Orichalcix) that decreases the longer the Obliterator waits between shots. When fired, every empire in the galaxy receives a notification that an Obliterator shot has been fired (though only the owner knows the target). The shot is visible as it travels, and its slow speed may allow targeted empires to move fleets away from the impact system. Moving your own fleets away from the target system before firing is mandatory ‚Äî the Obliterator shot destroys friendly fleets in the impact zone too.

Ground Invasion: Troops, Manpower & Post-Battle Choices

The siege is the setup. The invasion is the payoff. Know both.
üè∞ Sieging: Before the Invasion MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Orbiting an enemy system with a fleet (at least one ship with a weapon module) while no other enemy fleet is present initiates a siege. While sieged, the system cannot: replenish its defensive manpower, send or receive civilian ships, or produce strategic or luxury resources. Larger ships, more weapon modules, or siege support modules increase the manpower drain rate.

Sieging before invading is almost always correct. A full-manpower system defense can be 900‚Äì1200+ troops. A system drained by siege may have 200‚Äì400 when you actually invade. This is the difference between a fast win and a multi-turn war of attrition that bleeds your fleet's manpower reserves.

SIEGE DURATION AS PLANNING TIME
Use the siege turns to: check your troop composition (switch to 100% Armor if not already), position a second fleet to reinforce if the primary fleet is driven off, and monitor whether the enemy is sending a relief fleet. A siege broken by an incoming enemy fleet resets the system's manpower replenishment ‚Äî plan for the threat before it arrives.

ü™ñ Invasion Mechanics & Ground Tactics MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Ground battles play out as a war of attrition across multiple turns. Each turn, both attacker and defender deploy a limited amount of their banked manpower (default: 500 Manpower Deployment Limit). Deployed manpower converts to troops according to the troop cost table. Both sides choose a tactic each turn that modifies Deployment Limit, troop health and damage, and collateral damage to population and improvements.

INVASION DEPLETES YOUR FLEET'S MANPOWER ‚Äî AND YOUR FLEET KNOWS IT
When you launch an invasion, your fleet immediately deploys all of its manpower to the surface. This means your orbiting ships lose their 20% manpower damage bonus for the duration of the ground battle. A fleet that just unloaded 2,400 manpower is now fighting at ‚àí20% effective damage if an enemy relief fleet arrives. Plan accordingly: either have a second combat fleet ready to intercept incoming threats, or ensure your ground campaign resolves before the enemy can respond. You can refill fleet manpower at a friendly system and deliver additional troops mid-invasion ‚Äî but leaving the siege temporarily allows the system to begin replenishing its defensive manpower.

Attacker tactics:

  • ORBITAL BOMBARDMENTGuaranteed pre-battle damage. Useful when the defender's troop type counters yours. High collateral damage (kills population and destroys improvements) ‚Äî generally avoid unless fighting Pirates (who have neither).
  • GUERRILLA WARFAREReduces your deployment limit but also reduces casualty rate. Best when the defender has a manpower advantage and you need the battle to last longer without losing all your troops.
  • BLITZMaximizes deployment limit with some collateral damage risk. As the attacker after a successful siege (which depleted defender manpower), Blitz ends battles fastest. The standard choice for most invasions.

Defender tactics:

  • DRAFTIncreases Deployment Limit AND converts population into additional defensive manpower ‚Äî one of only two ways to replenish defenders mid-invasion (the other is sending a fleet with manpower to arrive during the siege). Expensive in population but may save the system.
  • PROTECT SYSTEMReduces troop death and collateral damage. Best when your defensive force is still substantial ‚Äî trades damage efficiency for endurance.
  • TACTICAL SURRENDERConcedes the planet immediately but halves the winner's system ownership growth rate (13 turns to integrate at normal speed vs. 26 turns). Use this when you have a counter-attacking fleet incoming ‚Äî the attacker's extended integration window gives you time to retake the system before they can consolidate.

Two military win conditions ‚Äî Conquest and Supremacy ‚Äî require fundamentally different strategies
üìä Conquest vs. Supremacy: Requirements & Strategies STRATEGY ‚ñæ

Conquest: Control a fixed percentage of the galaxy's star systems. The threshold depends on galaxy size:

Key rule: being in an alliance increases the system threshold because your allies' systems add to your combined total. Alliances are not neutral for Conquest ‚Äî they raise the bar.

Supremacy: Capture and hold all major factions' home systems simultaneously. This is the hardest military victory condition to achieve because it requires neutralizing every surviving empire's home. It does not require destroying their fleets or occupying all their systems ‚Äî only their home system. A targeted strike strategy (hit home systems while leaving the rest of their empire intact) is generally faster than full conquest of each empire.

TURN 30 CHECKPOINT ‚Äî MILITARY ASSESSMENT
By turn 30 on Normal speed you should be able to answer:
For Conquest: How many systems do I control vs. the target (22 for Normal)? Am I growing faster than rival factions? Which factions are within military striking distance?
For Supremacy: Do I know the location of all home systems? Is my fleet strong enough to take and hold a home system while withstanding counter-attacks? Do I have enough Manpower to sustain an invasion without my own systems becoming vulnerable?

If you're pursuing Conquest and a rival has already hit 11 systems (first warning), you have roughly 11 more systems to close. At normal expansion rates, that's 5‚Äì10 turns ‚Äî immediately evaluate whether you can close the gap via colonization or must pivot to military blocking actions.
