Espionage

ES2's diplomatic system is more mechanical than most 4X games ‚Äî relation scores change by specific amounts from specific triggers, and knowing those triggers lets you manage relationships proactively. The Penumbra DLC's hacking system adds a parallel covert layer that most players ignore entirely. Both are covered here at full depth.

The Diplomatic Relation Score System

Every diplomatic event moves the relation score by a specific, predictable amount ‚Äî not at random
ü§ù How Relation Scores Are Calculated MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Every faction pair in the game has a hidden Relation Score ranging from roughly ‚àí100 (maximum hostility) to +100 (maximum friendship). The score is visible in the F6 Diplomacy screen as a bar and a number. Every diplomatic action, military event, and passive situation moves this score by a specific delta each turn or as a one-time event.

THE "PERMANENT MEMORY" RULE
War declarations leave a permanent scar on the relation score that never fully decays. Even if you sign peace 50 turns later and maintain positive relations, the opponent AI will carry a hidden hostility modifier from the war declaration permanently. This means factions you've gone to war with once are significantly harder to form long-term alliances with. If you need a future ally, avoid declaring war on them ‚Äî even in wars of opportunity.

War Declarations, Cold War & Peace Treaties

War has three escalation stages ‚Äî knowing them prevents you from stumbling into full conflict unprepared
‚öîÔ∏è Cold War ‚Üí War ‚Üí Peace: The Three Stages MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Military conflict in ES2 escalates through three distinct states, and transitioning between them has specific mechanical effects:

  • COLD WARThis is an intermediate hostile state below full war. In Cold War, you can perform hostile actions like Blockade (fleet orbiting their system without full combat) and Privateers (licensed pirates attacking their trade routes), but you cannot invade their systems or trigger full fleet combat. Cold War is declared from the F6 Diplomacy screen. The primary purpose is economic pressure ‚Äî Blockading their Trade Company routes reduces their Dust income while you build up military for a real war. Each turn in Cold War also generates Influence that can be used to push the Senate toward a war declaration.
  • WARFull war enables all hostile actions: fleet combat, ground invasion, system capture. A war declaration drops relation by ‚àí40 immediately and starts generating "War Score" for both sides ‚Äî the accumulated advantage from battles won, systems captured, and fleets destroyed. War Score determines what peace terms you can demand at the treaty table. High positive War Score = you can demand significant concessions. Negative War Score = you'll have to offer concessions to end the war.
  • PEACE TREATYPeace is negotiated in F6 ‚Üí select the faction ‚Üí Treaty. Your available demands are gated by your current War Score relative to theirs. Available peace terms include: return all captured systems to original owners (White Peace), transfer specific systems permanently to the winner, demand reparation Dust payments, or force-release an allied faction from their obligations. Accepting unfavorable peace terms while you have a positive War Score is one of the most common tactical errors ‚Äî if you're winning, keep the war going until you've taken what you want.
AI WAR DECLARATION TRIGGERS
The AI is far more likely to declare war on you when: (1) your military score drops well below theirs (visible in F5 Scores), (2) you have fleets in their territory without Military Access rights, (3) your relation score drops below ‚àí30 without any treaties, or (4) another faction offers them an alliance against you. Monitor F5 Military score constantly ‚Äî if an opponent's military power is 2√ó yours with no active treaty, expect a war declaration within 10‚Äì15 turns. Pre-emptively offer a Non-Aggression Pact before their score diverges that far.

Senate Coalitions & Offensive Politics

The Senate is not just an internal resource ‚Äî it's a weapon you can aim at leading opponents
üèõÔ∏è Galaxy-Wide Senate Mechanics MECHANIC ‚ñæ

The Senate is a galaxy-wide institution. Every major faction holds a number of Senate seats proportional to their Influence stat relative to the total Influence of all factions. The senate votes on special laws ‚Äî and unlike the per-empire political laws, Senate laws apply to every faction in the galaxy simultaneously. This is what makes the Senate an offensive weapon.

  • SEATSYour Senate seat count = your Influence output / total galaxy Influence output √ó total seats. A faction that dominates Influence production dominates the Senate. The Religious party's entrenched law gives empire-wide Influence bonuses ‚Äî running Religious in power is the fastest way to Senate dominance.
  • PROPOSALSAny faction with sufficient seats can propose a Senate law from the available pool. Laws range from "+Industry to all empires" (broadly helpful) to "Embargo all empires currently at war" (potentially devastating to aggressive players) to "Technology sharing" (forces all factions to share their current research progress ‚Äî disastrous if you're winning the tech race, great if you're behind).
  • OFFENSIVE USEThe most powerful offensive Senate plays target the current leader. If an opponent is close to Economic victory, propose "Dust production penalty to all empires" ‚Äî it slows everyone including you, but it slows the leader proportionally more. If an opponent relies on their military, propose "Fleet maintenance cost doubled." Use the Senate as a delay mechanism against whoever is closest to winning while you close the gap.
ALLY SENATE SEAT SHARING
An Alliance agreement combines your Senate votes with your ally's ‚Äî you vote as a bloc. Two medium-Influence empires allied can overpower one high-Influence empire in Senate votes. If you're losing the Influence game individually, forming an Alliance with another faction purely for Senate vote pooling is a legitimate strategic play. The Alliance does come with defensive war obligations, so choose an ally who isn't likely to drag you into an unwanted war.

Hacking: The Penumbra Espionage System

A covert layer that operates entirely independently of diplomacy, fleets, and territory ‚Äî available to any faction with the Penumbra DLC
üïµÔ∏è How the Network Works: Bandwidth, Nodes, and Reach MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Hacking is a parallel action economy that runs alongside your FIDSI and military. Its core currency is Bandwidth ‚Äî generated per turn by your home node, capped by your node level, and spent on hacking operations. Every active hacking operation costs Bandwidth per turn; unused Bandwidth above the cap is wasted, so you always want active operations running.

To hack a target system, your hacking network must have a path that reaches it through star lane connections. Hacking travels at 2.9 distance units per turn along a star lane. You can also hack across open space if you have the Free Movement tech (0.75 units/turn, capped at 23 units distance). Once a hacking operation reaches the target node, the outcome resolves ‚Äî but if the defender has a defensive program installed, a Trace is launched that travels back toward your source at twice your hacking speed over empty space. The key tactical insight: routing your hack through two intermediate nodes usually gives you enough "travel time" for the hack to complete before the trace arrives. A single-hop direct hack is usually too fast to outrun the trace.

NO DEFENSIVE PROGRAM = FREE HACK
If the target system has no defensive program installed, no trace is launched at all ‚Äî you can hack it freely with zero risk. Conversely, if your own systems have no defensive programs, they are sitting ducks. Place at least Encrypt on your home system and any critical Science or Dust system from the moment you unlock hacking.

Backdoors are persistent entry points your hacking operations can create in enemy systems. A backdoor allows you to launch new operations from that node directly ‚Äî effectively extending your network into their territory and granting increased hacking speed on neighbouring nodes. Crucially, hacking started from a backdoor does not trigger a trace on the starting planet, making backdoors extremely valuable for sustained covert campaigns.

Sleepers are embedded population units resulting from a successful hack. They grant you continuous vision of the system and drain a small amount of that system's economy each turn. The Dust drain is disguised under comical names ("Wardrobe Malfunctions", "Ocean Moisturizing", "Charitable Donations") that make it hard for the victim to notice without active monitoring.
