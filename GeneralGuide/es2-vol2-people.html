Inside your Empire:
People & Ideas

Approval is the multiplier that governs your expansion rate. Every new colony reduces it; every tier drop costs you empire-wide Dust and Science. This volume covers the full Approval mechanics, the Empire Development tech tree, how outposts are founded and grown, how to evaluate a system before colonizing, how Influence generates and expands your territorial borders, and how the political system ‚Äî parties, laws, elections, and government types ‚Äî shapes your strategic options turn by turn.

01

Approval Mechanic: FIDSI Multiplier, Overcolonization & Overpopulation

Approval is not a happiness meter. It is a multiplicative gate on your economy ‚Äî and it reacts to every expansion decision you make.
üòä The Approval Tiers & Their FIDSI Impact MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Approval operates in two independent scopes. System Approval modifies Food and Influence output for that individual system. Empire Approval ‚Äî a population-weighted average of all system approvals ‚Äî modifies Dust and Science for your entire empire simultaneously. Industry is immune to Approval at every tier, in every scope.

Approval is a discrete tier system, not a continuous gradient. Being at 31% and 69% is mechanically identical ‚Äî both are Content, with zero modifier. The breakpoints that matter are 30, 70, and 85:

‚ö†Ô∏è Overcolonization: The Expansion Penalty MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Every empire has a maximum number of systems it can support before incurring an overcolonization Approval penalty. When your system count exceeds this limit, each excess system applies a ‚àí10 Approval penalty empire-wide. This is a direct hit to Empire Approval, which means it immediately affects your Dust and Science multiplier.

The overcolonization limit is not fixed. All known sources that raise it:

The Federation government's threshold bonus is unique: it scales with the number of Heroes currently acting as political representatives for leading parties ‚Äî meaning a hero-heavy Senate build can grant +3 or +4 threshold bonus systems simultaneously. This compounds with tech unlocks, making Federation the best government for wide play.

Additional Approval penalties that stack with overcolonization: razing systems applies ‚àí5 Approval per razed system permanently (a Republic government applies ‚àí15 per raze due to its law intensification). Mutinous populations after conquest also reduce system-level Approval temporarily.

THE COMPOUND FAILURE STATE
Overcolonization is dangerous not because the penalty is large per colony, but because it compounds with everything else. If you're at 72% Approval (Happy) and colonize one extra system dropping you to 62% (Content), you instantly lose +15% on all Dust and Science empire-wide. The new colony may take 10‚Äì20 turns to generate enough output to pay back that empire-wide loss. Expand just ahead of your limit, not into it.

üîß Overpopulation & the Spaceport Relief Valve MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Overpopulation is a separate mechanic from overcolonization. A system becomes overpopulated when its population count exceeds 70% of maximum capacity ‚Äî importantly, there is no penalty until you clear that 70% threshold. The formula:

Overpopulation Penalty = (Current Pop ‚àí Max Pop √ó 0.7) √ó 3.75

Below the 70% threshold the result is negative, which the game floors to 0 ‚Äî no penalty. Once you cross it, the penalty scales linearly at 3.75 Approval per excess pop. On a system with 10 max pop, being at 8 pops (80% capacity) generates a ‚àí3.75 Approval penalty; at 9 pops (90%) it's ‚àí7.5. Overpopulation applies as a system-level Approval penalty, which cascades into Empire Approval.

The Spaceport (unlocked at Level 2 modernization) is the primary relief valve: you can transfer surplus population from a full system to an underdeveloped one. This has two benefits simultaneously ‚Äî it relieves the Approval penalty on the full system and accelerates development of the receiving system by providing labor immediately rather than waiting for it to grow organically.

Population transfer is also a strategic optimization tool independent of overpopulation. A fully developed home system at max pops generates more FIDSI per pop by exporting surplus pops to new colonies than by sitting at overcapacity. The Spaceport essentially converts excess population growth on mature systems into colony-building acceleration on developing ones.

APPROVAL RECOVERY TOOLKIT
When Approval drops below a critical tier, options in order of speed:

1. Enact a Happiness law ‚Äî the Toys for Boys law (Scientist party) applies +25 Approval empire-wide. Caveat: it also costs ‚àí10% Industry on all systems. Only activate it when the Approval gain outweighs the Industry loss ‚Äî typically from mid-game when your Industry base is large enough that 10% is trivial compared to +25% on Dust and Science.

2. Luxury resource improvements ‚Äî assigning a happiness luxury resource (Jadonyx, Dust Trees, etc.) to system slots gives permanent per-pop Approval bonuses. These are permanent and don't carry the Industry penalty.

3. Population management ‚Äî moving unhappy population types out of systems via Spaceport, or adding population types with high natural Approval, shifts the system-level average.

4. Sell an outpost ‚Äî the planet broker allows selling underperforming outposts. This removes the overcolonization penalty immediately, refunds Dust, and may be the correct choice for systems that will never generate enough FIDSI to justify the Approval cost.

The Empire Development Tech Tree

Food, Approval, ship hulls, political development, and overcolonization thresholds. The expansion infrastructure tree.
üåø Tree Structure & Priority Logic MECHANIC ‚ñæ

The Empire Development quadrant governs the infrastructure for population growth, Approval management, expansion capacity, and ship hull access. It is the tree most directly tied to how fast and how wide your empire can grow without degrading.

Stage 1 ‚Äî Food and colony foundations: Early Stage 1 techs provide the basic colony ship hull and Food production improvements. The Sustainable Farms improvement (early Stage 1) is the core Food building for most factions, providing a flat Food bonus per system. Getting Food-producing improvements built in your main systems early accelerates population growth, which accelerates every other output.

Stage 2 ‚Äî Approval and hull upgrades: This stage contains most of the basic Approval improvements and the first meaningful ship hull upgrade (Hunter-class hulls, which are significantly stronger than starter hulls). The Ecologist party's "Hardship Ready" colonization law tech appears here, allowing colonization of non-standard planet types without separate tech prerequisites (for factions that can access it).

Stage 3 ‚Äî Overcolonization threshold and political development: Stage 3 is where the overcolonization limit extension technologies live. Xeno-Anthropology (the government change tech) also appears here ‚Äî this is the tech that lets you switch government type mid-game, a significant strategic decision. Additional Food-to-population scaling improvements appear at this stage.

Stage 4 ‚Äî Advanced hulls and political entrenchment: Carrier-class hulls (the largest standard hulls) unlock in Stage 4. Advanced Approval management improvements appear here, as well as the System Shield improvement (protection against Obliterator shots).

HULL TIER MATCHING
A critical principle from the community: ship hull size matters more for combat effectiveness than the tier of modules installed. Matching hull tier to module tier produces stronger ships than mixing. Stage 2 Military techs on Hunter hulls (Stage 3 Empire Development) outperform Stage 2 Military techs on starter hulls. Research hull upgrades in Empire Development alongside military module upgrades ‚Äî don't let one outpace the other by too many stages.

Expansion: Colony Ships & Founding an Outpost

Colonization is an investment, not a reflex. Every outpost you plant pulls Food from an existing system and costs Approval empire-wide.
üöÄ The Outpost Mechanics MECHANIC ‚ñæ

To colonize a system, you need a Colony Ship equipped with a colonization module. When the ship reaches a system containing a habitable planet (a planet whose type you have the technology to colonize), you plant an outpost. The Colony Ship disappears on use ‚Äî it is consumed. The system now shows as an outpost, not a full colony.

Outposts require a Food supply to grow into full colonies. The Food supply comes from a connected system, not from the outpost itself. By default this is the nearest connected system, but you can manually reassign the supply source to a closer or more Food-productive system to accelerate growth. The growth timeline is visible in the outpost screen.

Ways to accelerate outpost growth:

  • MANUAL SUPPLY SOURCEAssign the closest high-Food system as the supply source. Food traveling fewer hops arrives faster. A system 1 hop away supplies faster than one 3 hops away.
  • SPEND INFLUENCEInvesting Influence increases the Food supply rate to the outpost for several turns, shaving turns off the growth timer.
  • SPEND DUSTSame effect ‚Äî Dust can also accelerate outpost development.
  • CONTESTED OUTPOSTIf two factions plant outposts in the same system simultaneously, they race for control. You can spend Manpower to raid the enemy outpost's Food supply, slowing their growth and accelerating yours. Conversely, blockading the system with a fleet prevents any Food supply ships from reaching an enemy outpost.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
üåæ The Food Supply Draw MECHANIC ‚ñæ

When an outpost is growing, it draws approximately 40% of the Food production from its supply source system. This is significant: one active outpost consumes nearly half the Food surplus of its donor system. Two simultaneous outposts drawing from the same system can consume most of its Food output ‚Äî potentially causing population shrinkage in the donor system if it was already running lean.

Before planting a second outpost, check whether your primary Food-producing system can support two supply draws without going negative. If it cannot, either develop another high-Food system to act as the second supply source, or stagger your colonizations so the first outpost completes (becoming a self-sustaining colony) before the second Food draw begins.

OUTPOSTS AS RESOURCE NODES
An outpost does not need to become a productive colony to pay for itself. Even an undeveloped outpost produces the strategic and luxury resources on its planets ‚Äî these can be sold on the Marketplace immediately for Dust. A system with a good Titanium deposit starts generating Marketplace income the turn you plant the outpost, before a single pop has grown there. This framing ‚Äî outpost as a mining probe rather than a future colony ‚Äî changes how you evaluate colonization targets.

Evaluating Systems Before Colonizing

Population space and FIDSI potential vs. the Approval penalty. The question is never "is this system good?" ‚Äî it's "does this system pay back its Approval cost?"
üî≠ The Colonization Decision Framework STRATEGY ‚ñæ

Every colonization triggers two costs: an Approval hit (overcolonization penalty if you're at or near your limit) and a Food draw from an existing system. Against these costs you're betting on the target system's future FIDSI output. The decision is a payback period calculation, not a binary "good system or bad system."

Factors that make a system worth colonizing despite an Approval hit:

  • PLANET COUNTMore planets means more population slots, which means more total FIDSI at full development. A 4‚Äì5 planet system pays back its Approval cost faster than a 1‚Äì2 planet system because its production ceiling is much higher.
  • PLANET TYPESMatch planet types to your empire's current needs. If Industry is your bottleneck, a system with multiple Hot planets is immediately valuable. A system with all Cold planets is only valuable if Science is a priority and you have Cold-production improvements built.
  • STRATEGIC RESOURCESSystems with Titanium or Hyperium start generating Marketplace income as an outpost. Adamantium and above require curiosity detection to find and exploitation tech to use ‚Äî don't colonize for a resource you can't yet exploit.
  • LUXURY RESOURCESA system with Tier 2 or Tier 3 luxury resources you currently lack is valuable immediately as a supply source. Check whether you already have sufficient quantities of that luxury before prioritizing colonization for it.
  • STRATEGIC POSITIONSometimes the right reason to colonize a mediocre system is to deny it to a rival or to establish a forward presence near a contested border. This is a valid reason ‚Äî just be honest that you're paying an economic cost for a strategic benefit.
SYSTEMS TO AVOID
Small systems (1‚Äì2 planets) with no strategic resources and no luxury resources rarely pay back their Approval cost within a game's timeline. They produce modest FIDSI at full development and consume a colonization tech slot, a Colony Ship (Industry cost), and an overcolonization point. Skip these unless they serve a specific strategic purpose. You can always colonize them later after your expansion limit is higher.

ü™ê Planet Traits: Class, Fertility, Climate & Size MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Every planet has four traits that together determine its FIDSI output, Approval impact, and population ceiling: Class (the specific type ‚Äî Terran, Lava, Barren, etc.), Fertility (Fertile / Neutral / Sterile), Climate (Hot / Temperate / Cold), and Size (Tiny through Huge). Class is the most important single trait because it encodes specific base FIDSI values per pop. Fertility and Climate encode the pattern of those values and which tech/faction bonuses apply to the planet.

Fertility ‚Äî the population and Approval axis. This is the trait that most directly shapes expansion cost:

  • FERTILEHigher Food output, more even FIDSI spread, pop cap 5‚Äì10 per planet, 0 Approval penalty. Techs and faction bonuses for Fertile planets are numerous. These are your growth engine planets ‚Äî the ones that let you stack population without hurting Approval. Types: Atoll, Ocean, Forest, Terran (Temperate/Fertile); Jungle, Boreal (Hot/Cold Fertile); Monsoon (Temperate Fertile).
  • NEUTRALMiddling pop cap 3‚Äì9, ‚àí1 to ‚àí3 Approval penalty. Moderate FIDSI output ‚Äî often a mix of Food and one other resource. Types: Savannah, Steppes, Monsoon (Temperate); Mediterranean, Tundra (Hot/Cold Neutral); Snow, Arid (Cold/Hot Neutral).
  • STERILEExtreme FIDSI peaks in one resource, but low or zero in others. Pop cap 1‚Äì6, ‚àí5 to ‚àí8 Approval penalty. Higher Dust focus overall. Tech and faction bonuses are concentrated in Industry/Dust. Types: Ash, Desert, Lava (Hot Sterile); Barren, Ice, Arctic (Cold Sterile); Toxic (Temperate Sterile).

Climate ‚Äî which FIDSI stat the planet spikes.

  • HOTHigher Industry per pop. Techs and faction bonuses that target Hot planets grant Industry bonuses. Examples: Lava (16 Industry/pop base), Ash (12 Industry), Desert (8 Industry, 1 Food). Hot planets are your forge worlds.
  • COLDHigher Science per pop. Techs and faction bonuses that target Cold planets grant Science bonuses. Examples: Barren (16 Science/pop base), Ice (12 Science), Arctic (8 Science, 1 Food). Cold planets are your research labs.
  • TEMPERATELower, more balanced Industry and Science ‚Äî fewer tech/faction bonuses attach to the Temperate tag. The Temperate advantage is population density and Approval: Fertile Temperate planets (Atoll, Ocean, Forest, Terran) have zero Approval penalty and pop caps of 6‚Äì10, making them the best long-term growth platforms.

Size ‚Äî the pop cap multiplier. All planet types can appear in Tiny, Small, Medium, Large, or Huge variants. Size, combined with the class's base pop range, sets the maximum population on that planet. A Huge Terran is significantly more valuable than a Tiny Terran ‚Äî same FIDSI per pop, but far more pops. When scanning a system in the exploration view, the planet graphic's relative size is a quick proxy for this before you get detailed scout data.

Gas Giants ‚Äî a special case. Gas planets (G.Frozen, G.Cold, G.Temperate, G.Warm, G.Hot, G.Burning) require the Extreme Atmospherics tech and have unique mechanics. They are always a maximum pop cap of 1 (no growth, never scale with size), produce zero Food, and require the Stage 3 E&T tech to colonize. In exchange they output extreme single-resource FIDSI per pop: G.Burning = 26 Industry/pop; G.Temperate = 26 Dust/pop; G.Frozen = 26 Science/pop; G.Cold = 14 Industry + 14 Science; G.Warm = 10 Industry + 18 Dust; G.Hot = 14 Industry + 14 Science. Their ‚àí5 Approval penalty and permanent pop cap of 1 mean they never grow ‚Äî the entire value is that one pop's output. Place your highest-output pop type on a Gas Giant and it punches far above its system slot.

READING A SYSTEM AT A GLANCE
When you're deciding whether to colonize, the two questions to answer from the pre-scout view are: (1) How many planets? (2) What color/climate icons are dominant? A 4-planet system with two Hot icons and two Cold icons is a strong balanced system that produces Industry and Science at full development. A 3-planet system of all Sterile Hot types (Ash/Lava) is a production forge but carries a heavy Approval load and low pop potential ‚Äî valuable in a mature empire, punishing early. A 5-planet Fertile Temperate system (Atoll/Ocean/Terran) is the expansion dream: zero Approval hit, high pop ceiling, fast payback.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
üìä The Approval Cost‚ÄìBenefit Calculation STRATEGY ‚ñæ

The calculation is not complex once framed correctly. When considering a colonization that will drop your Empire Approval by one tier:

Step 1: Identify which Approval tier you're in now, and which tier you'd drop to. The difference in Dust + Science multiplier is the cost per turn (e.g., dropping from Ecstatic to Happy costs 15% on all Dust and Science).

Step 2: Apply that percentage to your current empire-wide Dust and Science output. If you produce 500 Dust/turn and 300 Science/turn, a 15% loss costs 75 Dust and 45 Science per turn ‚Äî every turn until you recover the Approval tier.

Step 3: Estimate how many turns the new colony will take to reach useful output (typically 10‚Äì20 turns for a basic outpost to start contributing meaningfully). Multiply the per-turn loss by those turns to get the total cost window.

Step 4: Ask whether the new colony will eventually produce more than that cost, and within what timeframe. A 5-planet system with good types will ‚Äî a 1-planet barren rock probably won't, ever.

This framework applies to all factions. The specific numbers change depending on your current output scale and the tier you're crossing, but the logic is identical. Expansion is not unconditionally good ‚Äî it is conditional on the payback period being acceptable.

Terraforming: Mechanics, Direction & Strategic Calculus

Terraforming trades per-pop FIDSI output for higher population capacity. It is a mid-to-late game tool ‚Äî and not always the right answer.

PRIMARY TERRAFORMING CEILING
Primary Terraforming alone (no Climate Engineering) can only reach Steppes, Savannah, Snow, or Tundra ‚Äî all Neutral fertility. It cannot reach Fertile planets. Advanced Terraforming (Climate Engineering) is required to push a planet to Jungle, Boreal, Terran, Ocean, Atoll, or Monsoon. If you only have Primary unlocked, the planet improves but remains Neutral.

Riftborn exception: The Riftborn have inverted planet preferences ‚Äî Sterile planets are their best performers and Fertile planets generate severe Approval penalties. Therefore, for Riftborn, the direction of terraforming is reversed: they terraform from Fertile toward Sterile planets...Doing the opposite would be actively harmful to a Riftborn empire, after all.

Unfallen shortcut: Certain choices in the Unfallen faction quest (Head and Heart chapter options) can unlock Forest-type planets directly, bypassing the standard terraforming tech requirement for some planetary routes. This is niche but worth noting if playing Unfallen.

‚öñÔ∏è The Strategic Calculus: When to Terraform and When to Stop STRATEGY ‚ñæ

Terraforming trades current FIDSI output for future population capacity. A Sterile planet produces high Industry and Dust per pop but supports very few pops. After terraforming it to Fertile, it produces far less per pop but can hold many more pops. The net gain only materializes once those extra population slots are actually filled. Before that, you are paying the Industry cost of terraforming plus receiving reduced output on the planet ‚Äî a double penalty. This is why terraforming is a mid-to-late game tool, not an early one.

The core question before terraforming: Will I fill those extra pop slots before the game ends? If the answer is no, the investment never pays back. If yes, how long will it take and what do you lose in the meantime?

SKIP IF NEGATIVE ANOMALIES ARE PRESENT
Anomalies apply per-pop penalties. Terraforming a planet increases its max population ‚Äî which means more pops exposed to the anomaly penalty. A planet with a ‚àí4 Food/pop anomaly that can hold 2 pops costs you 8 Food. After terraforming to 5 pop capacity, that same anomaly costs 20 Food. Terraforming a planet with severe negative anomalies compounds the damage. Anomaly removal (via the Science & Exploration tree) should precede terraforming in most cases.

FIDSI implications by output type:

  • FOODAlways improves from terraforming ‚Äî Fertile planets produce more Food, support more pops, and grow faster. Food is the output most consistently helped by terraforming regardless of which route you take.
  • INDUSTRYPenalized early. Sterile planets produce the highest Industry per pop. Moving to Neutral or Fertile reduces per-pop Industry output immediately. The penalty is partially recovered late-game if you build the Chlorophyll Chemistry improvement (converts excess Food to Industry at max pop) or the Climate Engineering improvement itself (+4 Science/pop on Fertile planets). If you're in a war posture and need Industry now, delay terraforming your best Industry planets.
  • DUSTPenalized early. Sterile planets also produce strong Dust per pop. The Optimized Logistics tech unlocks the Adaptive Taxation Systems improvement (+4 Dust/pop on Fertile and Temperate), which can recover the Dust loss late-game. Before that improvement is built, expect a Dust dip on any terraformed planet.
  • SCIENCEMost penalized by terraforming ‚Äî Cold planets (Barren, Arctic, Ice) produce the highest Science per pop, and moving them toward Temperate or Fertile reduces that advantage. The exception is the Boreal branch (Cold/Fertile): Boreal keeps the Cold tag, preserving Science bonuses while also gaining Fertile's pop cap increase. The Climate Engineering improvement also adds +4 Science/pop on Fertile planets, partially compensating. If your strategy depends on Science output, either terraform exclusively to Boreal or hold Cold planets unchanged.
  • INFLUENCEFlat improvement ‚Äî Fertile planets grant a modest Influence bonus per pop, and specialized improvements (Xenology, Supralight Aerodynamics, Nano Aquaculture) further amplify Influence on Fertile planets. Influence-focused empires benefit from terraforming more than most.
BOREAL AND JUNGLE AS TERMINAL TARGETS
These two planet types are often the correct stopping point and should not be further terraformed to their respective Temperate upgrades. Boreal (Cold/Fertile) keeps the Cold Climate tag ‚Äî your Cold-affinity populations and Cold-boosting improvements continue to apply, while you gain Fertile's population cap increase. Terraforming Boreal further to a Temperate planet removes the Cold tag and loses all those bonuses for a marginal additional pop slot. Similarly, Jungle (Hot/Fertile) keeps the Hot Climate tag. Unless you have a strong specific reason to push to Temperate, treat Boreal and Jungle as endpoints.

Wide vs. tall consideration: Wide empires that can move populations freely via Spaceport between many systems have less need to maximize per-system capacity. If you have 10 systems and some of them have underused fertile planets, transferring pops there may be more efficient than terraforming. Tall empires with fewer, more developed systems benefit more from terraforming as a capacity multiplier since they cannot easily source population elsewhere.

Minimum viable terraforming target: Regardless of strategy, having at least one Fertile planet per system is beneficial for population growth rate ‚Äî Food output is higher on Fertile planets, which speeds natural population growth for the entire system. Even if you do not intend to heavily terraform a system, leaving one planet on the Fertile track ensures the system grows faster without further investment.

Influence as a Resource & System Influence Zones

Influence funds your diplomacy and expands your territorial borders. No default planet type produces it ‚Äî every point must be built.
‚ú® How Influence Is Generated MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Influence has no default planet type ‚Äî unlike Science (Cold planets), Industry (Hot planets), or Food (Fertile planets), Influence is not naturally produced by any planet tag. Every point of Influence must come from System Improvements, Hero bonuses, specific Population types, Unique Planets, or anomalies that specifically grant Influence output.

The primary source for most empires is population: having population units assigned to planets in a system generates Influence for that system. The United Empire has a +1 Influence per pop affinity on top of this baseline, making them a notably Influence-heavy faction. Some minor faction assimilation bonuses also provide flat Influence per system.

Influence is affected by System Approval (not Empire Approval). This means a system running low Approval produces less Influence ‚Äî another reason to keep individual system approval high, beyond just the empire-wide multiplier chain.

UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
üó∫Ô∏è The Influence Zone: Territorial Borders MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Influence produced by a system is automatically invested in expanding that system's colored border on the map ‚Äî its Influence area. This is not stockpiled separately; it feeds the zone expansion each turn.

The Influence zone radius formula is nonlinear:

Influence Radius = ((System Influence / Game Speed Multiplier) / œÄ)^0.4 √ó (1.5 + (50 / System Influence))

In practical terms: higher Influence production expands the zone faster, but returns diminish at very high values. A system producing very little Influence will have a tight border that barely covers adjacent space; a high-Influence system can extend its zone several systems away.

What the Influence zone provides:

  • VISIONThe zone reveals all non-cloaked ships within its borders. This is passive intelligence ‚Äî no probe or fleet needed to see what's moving inside your zone.
  • COLONIZATION EXCLUSIONUncolonized systems within your Influence area cannot be colonized by other factions without declaring war first. This is your soft territorial claim ‚Äî expand your Influence zone ahead of your colony ships to lock out rivals from desirable systems.
  • SPECIAL NODE BONUSESNon-planetary special systems (asteroid fields, nebulae) inside your Influence area grant their passive bonuses to the system covering them.
  • PEACEFUL CONQUESTIf your Influence zone expands to envelop a foreign-owned system, you can potentially absorb it peacefully if you have researched the Pacific Conversion technology ‚Äî without declaring war.
BORDER CONTENTION
When two factions' Influence areas collide, a border forms between them. The side with higher Influence production pushes the border gradually backward ‚Äî visible on the map as a faint pulsing toward the direction of growth. You can see borders being contested in real time. A high-Influence frontier system actively expands your territorial claims against neighbors; a low-Influence frontier system slowly loses ground to rivals without any military action.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
üí∏ Empire-Wide Influence Stockpile: Spending MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Separately from the zone-expansion function, Influence is also stockpiled empire-wide and spent on diplomatic and political actions. These are different pools in practice ‚Äî think of per-system Influence as feeding the border, while empire-wide stockpiled Influence is the currency you spend in specific interactions.

Common Influence expenditures:

  • LAWSPassing a law has an initial cost: Max(20% of your Total Empire Points, 5). Ongoing laws cost Influence per turn (typically 1 Influence per pop in your empire for higher-tier laws). High-pop empires pay significantly more to sustain advanced laws.
  • SENATE ACTIONSInfluencing elections, giving official support to a party, intimidating voters, or reinforcing a party all cost Influence.
  • MINOR FACTIONSPraising, bribing, and assisting minor faction quests all spend Influence. Building relationship with minor factions early is one of the most Influence-efficient investments in the game ‚Äî the tribute bonuses compound over many turns.
  • DIPLOMACYProposing agreements, requesting truces, and trading assets with major factions all cost Influence. When a weaker faction uses Influence to force a truce on you, you can spend Influence to refuse and continue the war.
  • BATTLE CARD CHANGESSwitching battle tactics mid-combat costs Influence, with the cost scaling on remaining combat turns and fleet command points.
INFLUENCE IS THE HARDEST FIDSI TO PRODUCE
Influence is explicitly described as the hardest FIDSI to obtain in the game. No default planet type generates it, and most improvements that provide it are conditional on specific planet types or population compositions. Build Influence production actively and deliberately ‚Äî don't assume it will accumulate on its own. Running low on Influence at a critical diplomatic moment (a forced truce you need to refuse, an election you need to control) is a common mid-game failure mode.

The Political System: Parties, Laws & Senators

Laws are empire-wide buffs that can swing an output category by 30‚Äì50%. Getting the right laws active at the right time is one of the highest-leverage decisions in the game.
üèõÔ∏è Senate Mechanics: How It Works MECHANIC ‚ñæ

The Senate is populated by representatives allocated per system. The representative count formula differs by government: in Democracy, Republic, and Federation, each system sends one rep per population unit; in Dictatorship and Autocracy, each system sends (1 + 3 √ó System Level) representatives. This is a major strategic difference ‚Äî Dictatorship Senate composition is shaped by which systems you've developed to higher levels, not by population headcount.

Elections occur every 20 turns on Normal speed. Before results lock in, a pre-election survey shows projected vote distribution ‚Äî use this window to decide whether to spend Influence or Dust on interference. Between elections, parties accumulate political experience while holding Senate seats, unlocking stronger law tiers: Level 1 (Functional) is immediately available; Level 2 (Established) through Level 4 (Potent) require increasing tenure. A party that loses an election does not reset its experience ‚Äî it retains its level and can access those laws again immediately if re-elected.

Vote redistribution after elections: losing parties' votes are redistributed to winning parties based on ideological proximity on the politics wheel ‚Äî never to the opposite party. Example: if Pacifists lose but Scientists and Religious win, Pacifist votes go to Scientists (closer neighbor). This creates predictable drift ‚Äî a Senate race between two parties often ends with the ideologically adjacent winner absorbing the loser's base, making runaway majorities easier to sustain than they appear.

ü¶∏ Heroes as Senators MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Heroes have political affiliations that determine which party they belong to in the Senate. When a hero's party is the leading party, the hero's Senate-specific skills activate ‚Äî these are distinct from their governor or fleet commander skills and only apply while the hero holds Senate influence.

üó≥Ô∏è Elections & Interference MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Elections occur every 10‚Äì20 turns depending on game speed. The Senate vote reflects your entire empire's population composition ‚Äî each system sends representatives proportional to its population makeup. Pre-election, a survey button shows you the projected vote distribution, giving you a window to intervene before results are locked in.

Election interference options (available depending on government type):

  • OFFICIAL SUPPORTFree action ‚Äî gives a small boost to a chosen party's vote share. Available to all governments.
  • REINFORCECosts Influence ‚Äî gives a strong boost to a chosen party. Most effective at swinging a close race.
  • INTIMIDATECosts Influence ‚Äî suppresses a party's vote share. Useful for keeping a dominant rival party from gaining a second Senate seat.
  • PRIMARY ELECTION / STRONG ARMRepublic-specific ‚Äî use Dust to influence election outcomes directly. Allows Dust to substitute for Influence in election manipulation.

üìà Long-term Political Control via Population STRATEGY ‚ñæ

Election interference is a short-term lever. Long-term political control is built through population composition. Every population type has a natural affinity for specific parties ‚Äî Imperials boost Industrialists and Scientists; Vaulters strongly back Scientists; Horatio pops tend toward Pacifists. The populations you grow, assimilate, and breed determine your Senate baseline over the course of the game.

Some population affinities also provide support to adjacent parties (parties closer on the political spectrum). This means that growing Militarists-aligned pops can also shift votes toward Scientists (their adjacent party), and Pacifists can gain votes from populations that primarily support Industrialists. Understanding these adjacencies lets you stack Senate compositions more deliberately.

The strategic implication: your decisions about which minor factions to assimilate and which alien populations to breed are not just FIDSI decisions ‚Äî they are simultaneously political decisions. An empire that carefully selects for Industrialist-affiliated minor faction pops will maintain Industrialist dominance election after election without spending Influence on interference, while an empire that assimilates randomly may find itself unable to sustain the laws it wants.

This is one of the most underutilized strategic dimensions by intermediate players: treating political alignment as a colonization and population management criterion, not just as an outcome you react to.
