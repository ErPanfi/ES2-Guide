How empires influence each other without firing a shot ‚Äî and what happens when you decide to fire the metaphorical ones invisibly.

The Diplomatic Relation Score System

Pressure, Mood, and the numbers behind your neighbour's attitude
üìä Treaty Status vs. Relationship Status MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Every pair of empires has two separate layers of diplomatic information: their treaty status and their relationship status. These are independent of each other and easy to confuse.

Treaty status is the formal contract ‚Äî it is either Cold War, War, Peace, or Alliance. It determines what military actions are legal and what agreements are available.

Relationship status is the AI's subjective opinion of you ‚Äî a qualitative label like Reserved, Welcoming, Friendly, Hostile, or Triumphant. It reflects how the AI feels and influences how likely they are to accept or refuse diplomatic offers. These labels are not a score you can directly manipulate, but they shift based on your history: sustained peace and trade agreements push toward Friendly, while unprovoked attacks and ignored tributes push toward Hostile.

RELATIONSHIP LABELS ‚Äî WIKI SOURCE
The documented labels from the fandom wiki include: Reserved (first contact), Cautious, Welcoming (desires peace), Friendly (long peace + trade), Cordial, Wounded (failed to send military aid as ally), Sneaky (former friend who sees an opportunity while stronger), Hostile (tribute demanded but not received), Triumphant (won a war ‚Äî seen during Truces), Recovering (lost a war ‚Äî seen during Truces).

üìà Diplomatic Pressure: How it Works MECHANIC ‚ñæ

During Cold War and Peace, two empires generate Diplomatic Pressure against each other. This is represented on the Diplomacy screen as blue (positive) and orange (negative) bars. The key metric is the Pressure Trend ‚Äî how many pressure points are added or subtracted each turn, ranging from ‚àí10 to +10.

Once one empire has accumulated enough pressure over the other, it can make Demands: forced transfers of Dust, resources, technology, or multi-turn tribute payments.

The factors contributing to Pressure Trend, with their point ranges:

Score Difference‚àí5 to +5Higher game score = positive pressure on the opponent. Max ¬±5 points.
Borders Friction‚àí5 to +5Net influence difference where zones overlap. Stronger border = positive pressure on the weaker neighbour.
Systems Covered by Influence¬±8Covering an opponent's system entirely: +8. Being covered: ‚àí8. This is the single highest-magnitude factor.
Diplomatic Choices¬±0.5Refusing a demand to stop attacking: +0.5 for 10 turns. Accepting: ‚àí0.5.
Diplomatic Treaties (Bureaucratic Imbroglio)¬±2.5Forcing this diplomatic choice on the opponent: +2.5 to you for 10 turns; ‚àí2.5 to them.
Hacking (Penumbra DLC)¬±0.5Back-tracing an enemy's hack: +0.5. Getting traced back: ‚àí0.5.
THE INFLUENCE ZONE DOMINANCE EFFECT
The "Systems Covered by Influence" factor (¬±8) completely dwarfs all other Pressure contributions. A late-game empire whose influence zone envelops a neighbour's systems can generate so much pressure that demands become a near-constant threat. Maintaining your own system's Influence output is therefore a defensive act, not just a colonisation tool.

Certain empire-level modifiers also multiply all incoming or outgoing pressure. Notable examples from the wiki: the Z'vali "Spirit" trait adds +25% diplomatic pressure toward enemies; the Amoeba Law adds +50%; and the Citadel of Demography building adds +50%.

A Trade Agreement lets both sides route trade through each other's subsidiaries. Because distance is a multiplier on Dust income from trade, routing a trade company HQ to a distant subsidiary in an ally's empire can generate substantially more Dust than intra-empire routes. The strategic case for a Trade Agreement often outweighs the Influence upkeep cost once both empires have Trade Companies.

üßÆ Demands, Tribute & Gifts STRATEGY ‚ñæ

Once you have accumulated enough pressure over an enemy, the Negotiation Screen unlocks a Demands bar showing available demand tiers. You can demand Dust, strategic or luxury resources, technologies, or an Economic Tribute (periodic Dust/Science donation over a set number of turns).

From the opposing side: if an AI empire has sufficient pressure over you, it will send Tribute or Gift demands. A tribute request requires a donation large enough to satisfy the AI within that turn. Complying gives a relationship boost; ignoring worsens relations. Paying tribute does not end a Hostile relationship on its own ‚Äî making peace afterwards is advisable to reset the dynamic. There's a case for not paying at all against the Cravers, who cannot be brought to peace by design.

Gifts (voluntary transfers) also reduce how quickly Pressure accumulates, delaying the AI's ability to file an official Demand. They can also be used during a Force Peace offer to sweeten the deal enough for acceptance.

UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
üåê Influence Conversion: Bloodless Conquest STRATEGY ‚ñæ

Influence Conversion (formerly called Pacifist Conversion) allows you to absorb another faction's systems without declaring war. Once you have researched SLC Systems in Stage 4 of the Empire Development tech tree and your influence zone envelops a foreign system, a turn countdown appears next to that system. When the timer expires, the system flips to your empire. The conversion speed is determined by how much your Influence output exceeds the defender's.

The United Empire has a unique ability to spend Influence to accelerate this process. This mechanic also applies in reverse ‚Äî a neighbour with dominant Influence production can absorb your border systems without a single fleet movement. Countering it requires either matching their Influence output or eliminating the encircling systems militarily.

PRECONDITION
Influence Conversion requires researching SLC Systems in Stage 4 of the Empire Development tree. Before that research, influence zones are aesthetic and strategic but cannot physically capture foreign systems.

War Declarations, Cold War & Peace Treaties

The rules of engagement, War Momentum, and the Force Truce system
‚öîÔ∏è What Cold War, War, and Peace Actually Allow MECHANIC ‚ñæ

All empires start the game in Cold War. In Cold War, fleet combat can occur in neutral territory or if you enter a colonised system belonging to another empire ‚Äî but attacking first displeases that empire and affects the relationship status. Many empires tolerate exploration ships early on but become hostile to military fleets near their systems.

War removes all geographic restrictions on fleet combat. Fleets can battle anywhere, and systems can be invaded with Manpower through Ground Battle. Winning a Ground Battle captures the system. War also opens unique Senate law tiers for Militarist parties (see T4).

Peace unlocks the full agreement suite: Trade Agreements, Science Agreements, Map Sharing, Co-operative Agreements, Alliance, and Close Borders. Empires can return from Peace to Cold War ‚Äî this is distinct from declaring War.

A Truce is available only during War. It ends the war temporarily. Truces have their own mechanics (Force Truce, see below) and leave relationship labels like Triumphant/Recovering visible during the truce period.

UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
‚öñÔ∏è War Momentum & the Force Truce System MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Once war is declared, War Momentum replaces Pressure Trend. It ranges from ‚àí10 to +10 per turn and accumulates a score based on:

  • FLEET BATTLESWinning adds points; losing deducts points. Exact per-battle magnitude marked "Details Unknown" in the wiki.
  • GROUND BATTLESSame win/loss dynamic as fleet battles.
  • BLOCKADESGuarding (blockading) an enemy system: +0.1 per system. Enemy blockading your system: ‚àí0.1 per system.

When War Momentum reaches 80% or 20%, the dominant or losing side may send a Force Truce offer. This is described by the wiki as an anti-snowball mechanism allowing the losing side a chance to recover. Rejecting a Force Truce costs Influence ‚Äî a significant constraint if your Influence pool is depleted from other costs. The Force Truce offer is only sent to one member of an Alliance, not all members.

AI ALLIANCE BEHAVIOUR ‚Äî COMMUNITY NOTE (WIKI)
The wiki notes: "When you are in alliance with AIs, they have a large chance of accepting truces even when you have more than enough influence to refuse it." If you're in an alliance and winning decisively, don't rely on AI partners to hold the line against a Force Truce.

Outside of Force Truce, a normal Truce offer can be made at any point via the Diplomacy screen. The AI's willingness to accept depends on War Momentum, current Mood/relationship status, and how lopsided the terms are.

üí° Alliance Mechanics & Joint Victory MECHANIC ‚ñæ

An Alliance requires Peace status and is capped at no more than half the remaining factions in the game. Minimum 4 empires in the game to form one at all. Consequences of entering an Alliance:

  • SHARED WARAny war declaration by one member applies to the whole Alliance. However, each member individually has the option to step out when a fellow Alliance member starts a war.
  • FACTION PACT BONUSEach Alliance member can sign a pact with one other Alliance member granting an effect linked to that ally's faction identity. The bonus improves over time up to a maximum.
  • COORDINATION TOOLA coordination icon on the End Turn button (galaxy view, bottom right) lets you mark intentions on the galaxy map for allies to see.
  • SHARED VISION & JOINT VICTORYAllied empires share map visibility. Winning conditions that require a Joint Victory count for all members simultaneously. Note: some victory requirements scale upward proportional to Alliance size.

The Pacifist party's forced law provides a Dust/FIDS bonus for each empire in Alliance, incentivising Pacifist-dominated empires to grow alliances rather than wage war.

Weaponising your Senate against rival empires

Each election cycle determines which 1‚Äì3 parties hold seats (depending on government type). Parties gain political experience while in power; experience unlocks higher-tier laws. Law upkeep cost scales with empire population: the cost per turn for most party laws is law level √ó empire population in Influence. üó≥Ô∏è Election Manipulation & Coalition Control STRATEGY ‚ñæ

Elections happen every 20 turns (Normal speed), at end of turn. Immediately before an election resolves, you can take an Election Action to manipulate the outcome. Available actions depend on government type; they include: giving official support (+small boost), spending Dust to boost a party, spending Influence for a strong boost, intimidating citizens away from a party, or reinforcing a target party. You can also simply request a Survey to preview the projected outcome before committing.

Population composition shapes Senate composition. Each system's representatives are drawn from its population's innate political affinities. Growing specific population types, assimilating minor faction pops, and capturing systems from enemies all shift your political balance. Planning which minor factions to assimilate is partly a long-term Senate engineering decision, not just a FIDSI calculation.

Coalition control strategy: if you need Militarist laws to fund a long war, you must ensure Militarists keep winning elections consistently enough to level their experience. A single election loss evicts their laws immediately. Conversely, if you want to block a party (say, Pacifists who are about to impose peace-preferring laws), you can spend resources at election to undermine them specifically.

Hacking: The Penumbra Espionage System

Bandwidth, routing, traces, and the race between hack and counter-hack
üíª The Fundamentals: Bandwidth, Operations, and Routing MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Hacking is a Penumbra DLC mechanic available to all factions from Turn 1. Every empire begins the game with one simultaneous hacking operation and can increase this cap by researching specific technologies (the first unlock is in Autonomous Materials in the Exploration/Science tree).

Bandwidth is your hacking resource pool. It is not a per-turn currency ‚Äî it is a fixed allocation. Running defensive or offensive programs consumes bandwidth. Cancelling an active hack also costs bandwidth. You cannot spend bandwidth you do not have, so managing the pool is essential before committing to a program-heavy strategy.

To initiate a hack: select a source system (any system you control), then click a path through the galaxy to your target. The hack travels along starlanes or through empty space at the following documented speeds (from the wiki):

  • STARLANE2.9 distance units per turn.
  • FREE MOVEMENT (empty space)0.75 distance units per turn. Maximum hack distance over empty space: 23 units.
  • NODE COSTHacking through a node requires ~240 speed to complete. Exact value marked uncertain in the wiki (shown as "240(?)"), so treat as approximate.

Your own controlled systems are bypassed instantly by your hacks ‚Äî they do not cost time. This is a routing strategy tool: inserting your own colonies into a hack path gives the hack free passage while forcing an enemy trace to spend time hacking through those nodes on its way back.

UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
üî¥ Traces: The Race You're Always Running MECHANIC ‚ñæ

When your hack passes through a system that has a Defensive Program installed, that system automatically launches a Trace ‚Äî a counter-hack that travels back toward your hacking source. Traces are the primary risk of offensive hacking.

The core mechanic of the hack/trace contest (from the wiki):

  • TRACE SPEEDTraces travel at 2√ó the speed of the hack over empty space.
  • TRACE STARTA trace only begins once your hack actually arrives at the target planet. The contest is therefore: can your hack finish its work at the target before the trace makes the round trip back to your source?
  • BACKDOOR EXCEPTIONHacks originating from a Backdoor do not trigger a trace at the Backdoor's host system. If traced back, the Backdoor is destroyed instead of applying a penalty to you directly.
  • NO DEFENSIVE PROGRAMIf the target system has no Defensive Program installed, you can hack it with zero consequences ‚Äî no trace is ever launched. This applies symmetrically: your unprotected systems are sitting ducks.
ROUTING STRATEGY TO BEAT TRACES
From the wiki: traces travel at 2√ó hack speed but must fight through intermediate nodes. A single-hop hack usually gives the trace enough time to catch up. Routing through two or more nodes ‚Äî especially through your own systems (which the trace must hack through while your hack bypasses) ‚Äî typically generates enough distance to complete the hack before the trace returns. The community rule of thumb: one-hop hacks on defended targets are usually unsafe; two-node paths are usually sufficient.

When a trace succeeds, the defender can choose from options including Blackmail (preventing you from denying their next hacking-stop request), Destroy Backdoor (if traced to one), or Shut Down (cancels your future operations temporarily). A completed hack always finishes even if the trace also completes on the same turn ‚Äî so if you're one turn away from a successful hack, pressing End Turn is still worth it even if the trace is closing in.

UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
üò¥ Sleeper Agents: Persistent Espionage Presence MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Embedding a Sleeper (a Level 1 hack result) converts one population unit in the target system into a covert agent. Sleepers do two things once you set a threshold in your Hacking screen:

  • VISIONGrants ongoing visibility of that system's activity.
  • RESOURCE DRAINSiphons Dust from the enemy and redirects it to you. Multiple sleepers per system are possible (up to the system's population count) and the drain scales.

The enemy receives no notification when a Sleeper is embedded ‚Äî but the resource drain shows up as a small, oddly-named Dust deduction in their FIDSI panel (examples from the wiki: "Dubious Accounting Practices", "Charitable Donations", "Horatio Consultancy Charges"). An attentive opponent can infer they've been infiltrated from these entries.

To remove Sleepers from your own empire: run the Domestic Surveillance program on the affected system. This reverses approximately one Sleeper every few turns but imposes a significant Approval penalty on that system ‚Äî a meaningful trade-off in early to mid-game when Approval is a FIDSI multiplier.

üåç Hacking Minor Factions, Pirates & the Academy MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Hacking targets are not limited to major factions. Each target type offers different outcomes:

Minor Factions: Hacking a minor faction system allows you to boost their Approval of you (strong early relationship boost), impersonate an ambassador to reduce their relationship with a rival empire by 5, steal their resources even when at war, or hack their economy to increase yields by 75% (normal resources) / 50% (strategic/luxury) while guaranteeing relation rewards regardless of war status.

Pirates: Pirate systems can be hacked to steal resources or alter their relationships with players. The video transcript notes this is "not too useful" compared to simply destroying pirates, but pirates can also hack you ‚Äî watch for unexpected resource drains if pirates are active in your region.

The Galactic Academy: Treated like a player's home system for hacking purposes (Level 2 outcomes). The Academy has its own Hacking node and can be hacked for tech theft or political disruption effects that apply to the Academy's own governance, affecting galactic events.

Umbral Choir‚Äìspecific outcomes (not available to other factions): Install Transmigration Beacon, Create Sanctuary, Abduct Sleeper(s), Recover Lost Home System. These are Umbral Choir mechanics outside the scope of this general guide.

üõ°Ô∏è Defensive Programs MECHANIC ‚ñæ

Defensive programs are installed on your systems. They trigger automatically when an enemy hack passes through or targets that system, launching a Trace. Each defensive program also removes detected Backdoors on installation.

Critically: a system with no defensive program generates no trace at all. Your system cannot protect itself passively ‚Äî protection requires bandwidth investment.

Undefended inner systems can often be left exposed early on ‚Äî the enemy still has to route through defended outer nodes.

üî¨ Domestic Surveillance & Anti-Sleeper Ops STRATEGY ‚ñæ

The Domestic Surveillance program is the specific counter to Sleeper agents. Running it on a system periodically removes embedded Sleepers ‚Äî approximately one every few turns. The cost is a substantial Approval penalty on that system for the duration. Given that Approval is an empire-wide FIDSI multiplier, launching Domestic Surveillance on a high-population system during a critical economic phase can be counterproductive.

The detection heuristic from the video transcript: if you see small inexplicable Dust deductions in your FIDSI breakdown with nonsensical labels (examples: "Scandal Payoffs", "Ocean Moisturizing", "Budget Miniaturization"), you have at least one active Sleeper draining your economy. Use the Hacking screen's Sleeper tab to see which systems are affected and how many agents are present, then decide whether the drain is large enough to justify the Approval cost of Surveillance.

The transcript's practical advice: if the drain is small or you're on an unprotected low-value system, the Approval penalty of running Domestic Surveillance often costs more than the Sleeper steals. Reserve it for heavily infiltrated high-pop systems or when the Dust drain has become significant relative to your income.
