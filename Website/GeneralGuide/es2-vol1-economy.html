Inside your Empire:
Material & Money

FIDSI is not a dashboard — it's a system of levers with dependencies. This volume covers the full calculation stack, how population multiplies everything, how science costs scale, what the Economy & Trade tree actually unlocks, how resources and the Marketplace interact, how system development levels work, what trade companies do and when they're worth building, how Economic Behemoths function, and what an honest empire assessment looks like at Turn 30. Approval depth is in Vol.2. Manpower depth is in Vol.4.

01

FIDSI: Full Calculation & Multiplier Stack

Base × Pop × Approval × Hero × Law. Each layer multiplies the previous one.
📊 How the Output Number Is Built MECHANIC ▾

Every FIDSI number you see in the system panel is the result of a multiplication chain, not a simple sum. Understanding each layer tells you where intervening makes the most difference.

The chain works like this: each planet has a base value per population unit for each FIDSI output — this depends on the planet type (Fertile planets produce the most Food; Hot planets produce the most Industry; Cold planets produce the most Science). That base value is then multiplied by the number of population units assigned to that planet. This is why population is your primary lever: doubling population on a planet doubles its output, before any other modifier applies.

On top of the base × pop product, several modifiers stack:

  • IMPROVEMENTSSystem Improvements add flat bonuses (e.g. "+10 Industry on Hot planets") or percentage bonuses on top of the base production. These apply per system and are additive with each other before multiplying with other layers.
  • HERO BONUSA governor hero assigned to a system applies their skill bonuses to that system's output. These are typically percentage multipliers on one or more FIDSI. The hero's bonus compounds with improvement bonuses.
  • APPROVAL MULTIPLIERApproval acts as a global multiplier on Dust and Science (empire-wide) and on Food and Influence (per-system). Industry is entirely exempt from Approval modifiers at every tier. See the Approval table below.
  • LAW MODIFIERSActive laws can add flat bonuses per population, percentage multipliers on specific outputs, or conditional bonuses tied to planet type or strategic resources. Laws apply empire-wide.
  • LUXURY RESOURCE SLOTSOnce system development levels are unlocked, assigning a luxury resource to a system slot grants a permanent percentage bonus to a specific FIDSI type for that system. These compound with everything above.
READING THE TOOLTIP
Hovering over any FIDSI output in the system panel shows the full breakdown: base planet production, pop multiplier, improvement contributions, and hero bonuses listed separately. Use this actively when deciding where to place population or which improvement to build next. The numbers are transparent — the game is not hiding the math.

😊 Approval: The Multiplier Stack MECHANIC ▾

Approval is not a sixth FIDSI — it is a multiplier that sits on top of the production chain. It operates in two separate scopes: System Approval affects Food and Influence output for that individual system; Empire Approval (a population-weighted average across all your systems) affects Dust and Science empire-wide. Industry is immune at every tier.

★ Experienced players treat 85% as a maintenance target, not just a lucky ceiling. The gap between Devoted (+30% Dust/Science) and Loyal (+15%) is large enough at mid-to-late game output levels that holding above 85% is actively worth building around — through laws, luxury assignments, and expansion timing — rather than treating it as a bonus you happen to collect.

There is no gradient between tiers — 31% and 69% Approval produce identical output. This makes Approval a binary gate, not a continuous dial. Dropping from 85% to 84% costs you the full +30% Dust/Science bonus empire-wide in one turn. Rising from 69% to 70% unlocks +15% on the same outputs immediately.

THE EXPANSION TRAP
Every new colony system reduces Empire Approval. If colonizing a new system drops you from 85% to 84%, you lose +30% on all Dust and Science empire-wide — a penalty that may outweigh that colony's entire output for many turns. The question before every colonization is not "is this system good?" but "does this system produce more than what I lose from the Approval drop?" Full treatment in Vol.2.

⚙️ Manpower & the Food Conversion Chain MECHANIC ▾

Manpower is sometimes called the sixth FIDSI. It is generated by converting a portion of Food production. The base conversion rate starts at 10% (provided by the Colonial Base improvement), and additional Manpower buildings increase this rate. The conversion is automatic and stops when your Manpower stockpile is full.

Manpower is used to crew ships (consumed on build), to defend systems against ground invasion, and in some trade interactions. It is also consumed when you invade enemy systems — available troops depend on how much Manpower you have loaded onto your fleets. Full treatment in Vol.4.

Two late-game Food conversions are also worth knowing now: once a planet is fully populated and the Super Biofuel Factory improvement is built (requires Chlorophyll Chemistry, Stage 5), excess Food converts to Industry. This makes Food indirectly valuable as an Industry multiplier in developed systems.

Food consumption is nonlinear with population count. The scaling is approximately pop1.28, meaning each additional population unit costs more Food than the one before it. A system with 1 pop consumes ~5.4 Food/pop; at 9 pops this rises to ~10.1 Food/pop. High-population systems need disproportionately strong Food production just to stay solvent — you cannot assume the Food surplus from a 3-pop system will carry a 9-pop one.

📉 Dust Inflation, Buyout Costs & Science Scaling MECHANIC ▾

Dust Inflation is a global multiplier applied to all Dust-denominated costs — Marketplace prices, Buyouts, and Upkeep. The formula:

Dust Inflation = Global Dust Production × 0.000175

Every 5,714 Dust/turn of production adds ~1.0 to the inflation multiplier. An empire producing 10,000 Dust/turn pays 1.75× on all those costs. This is the mechanic that prevents raw Dust production from being a free win — the more you produce, the more expensive everything becomes proportionally. Note that Dust income does not scale with inflation; only costs do.

LUMERIS INFLATION TRAP
Lumeris colonization costs 200 Dust × Inflation per outpost. At 15,000 Dust/turn (~2.6× inflation), each new colony costs ~520 Dust instead of 200. If Dust income does not outpace inflation, the expansion engine stalls. The Lumeris have a unique tech that temporarily deflates by 0.25 for 5 turns — time it to your expansion windows.

Buyout costs scale nonlinearly with the Industry cost of the item, then multiply by inflation:

Buyout Cost = (Industry Cost1.2125 + 50) × Inflation

02

Population Mechanics & Movement

Population is your production multiplier. Managing where it grows — and moves — is active gameplay.
👥 How Population Grows MECHANIC ▾

Food is stockpiled locally within each system. When the stockpile reaches 300 Food, a new population unit spawns. Each additional 300 Food produced in the same turn beyond that threshold spawns another unit of the same population type — meaning high Food production can generate multiple pops per turn in a single system.

The population type that grows next is determined by a growth score: each turn, every population type in the system receives +50 to its growth score. The type with the highest score is selected to grow next, and its score resets to 0 after spawning. This means if you have multiple population types, they will cycle through growth rounds in sequence unless you actively manage the Food supply or population composition.

Food consumption scales non-linearly with population count. A system with 1 pop consumes approximately 5.4 Food/turn; a system with 9 pops consumes approximately 90.5 Food/turn — roughly 10.1 Food per pop, nearly double the rate at low counts. This means high-population systems require significantly more Food investment to sustain growth than small ones.

STARVATION
If a system's net Food production goes negative, the stockpile drains first. When the stockpile reaches −300 Food, the population unit with the highest growth score dies. After conquest, newly acquired systems often run negative Food temporarily (mutinous penalty), which means pop loss is a real risk if you don't stabilize them quickly.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
🚀 Population Movement via Spaceport MECHANIC ▾

Once you research the Spaceport technology (Economy & Trade tree), you can manually transfer population between systems. Select the system you want to move pops from, open the population panel, and drag the pop units into the Spaceport slot. The transport ship then travels physically along star lanes to the destination system.

The transport is vulnerable in transit — an enemy fleet on the star lane can intercept and destroy it, killing the population aboard. Secure your lanes before moving valuable populations through contested space.

Population movement is an active optimization tool, not just an emergency measure. Common uses:

  • SPECIALIZATIONMove population types to planets where their FIDSI affinity matches the planet's Climate tag. A pop with an Industry affinity bonus belongs on a Hot planet (Lava, Ash, Desert…); a pop with a Science affinity bonus belongs on a Cold planet (Barren, Ice, Arctic…). The compounding here is real: the pop's affinity bonus adds on top of the planet's base FIDSI output, both driven by the same Climate tag — so a Hot-affinity pop on a Hot planet gets the full base Industry output plus their bonus, whereas the same pop on a Cold planet contributes neither. Fertility works the same way: a Fertile-affinity pop on a Fertile planet (Terran, Ocean, Forest…) benefits from tag-matched Food output. Planet traits are covered in depth in Vol.2.
  • COLONY SEEDINGInstead of waiting for an outpost to grow its own population through food, you can import pops from a full system to kickstart a new colony's labor force immediately.
  • APPROVAL MANAGEMENTPopulations have political affiliations. Moving specific pop types can shift your system-level approval and your Senate composition simultaneously.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
🧬 Population Types & Empire Bonuses MECHANIC ▾

Your empire accumulates population types through natural growth, minor faction assimilation, and conquest. Each type carries its own per-planet FIDSI bonuses — these are applied when that pop is working on a planet whose Climate or Fertility tag matches the pop's affinity condition. Check the pop tooltip in the system panel to see the exact tag requirement. The three Climate tags are Hot (Industry-oriented), Cold (Science-oriented), and Temperate (balanced, fewer bonus interactions). The three Fertility tags are Fertile (Food-oriented, 0 Approval penalty, high pop cap), Neutral (moderate, minor Approval penalty), and Sterile (extreme Industry/Dust peaks, heavy Approval penalty, low pop cap). A pop that gets a bonus "on Hot planets" will trigger on any Hot planet — Lava, Ash, Desert, Mediterranean, Jungle — regardless of that planet's Fertility status. The tag system is additive: place the right pop type on the right planet type and you get the planet's base output plus the pop's affinity bonus stacking on top.

Beyond per-planet bonuses, population types also grant empire-wide collection bonuses at specific thresholds (typically 10, 20, and 50 units of that type across your empire). These bonuses can be powerful: as an example, having 50 Lumeris pops empire-wide grants trade route blockade immunity and adds 25% of trade company income to Influence. Tracking these thresholds and deliberately breeding toward them is an intermediate-level optimization.

Each population type also has a political affiliation — this determines which Senate party they vote for, and therefore shapes your available laws. See Vol.2 for political system depth.

Science Production & the Research Queue

Science costs scale with every tech you've already researched. Queue discipline matters more than raw output.
🔬 How Science Output Is Generated MECHANIC ▾

Science is an empire-wide pool — all systems contribute to a single shared output that funds research. Unlike Industry (which is per-system), your total Science per turn is the sum across every colonized system, then modified by Empire Approval. Cold planets produce the most Science base output per population unit.

The main ways to increase Science output:

  • PLANET TYPECold planets carry the highest Science base value per pop. Prioritize placing population on Cold planets in systems you want to run as Science centers, and build Cold-boosting improvements there.
  • SYSTEM IMPROVEMENTSScience improvements like Public-Private Partnerships give conditional flat bonuses (e.g. "+10 Science on Fertile planets"). Stacking several in a system with multiple qualifying planets can produce dramatically high Science per turn in a single system.
  • LAWSSome laws trade one resource for Science. The Chronical Eczema Act (Scientist party) gives more Science per population at a happiness cost. If you're driving toward a Science victory and Approval is manageable, this is worth evaluating.
  • EMPIRE APPROVALScience is affected by Empire Approval at the same rate as Dust. Being Ecstatic (+30%) versus Content (0%) is a 30% Science multiplier across your entire empire — a massive difference at high output levels.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
📈 Technology Cost Scaling MECHANIC ▾

Technology costs are not fixed — they scale with the total number of technologies you have already researched. Every tech you unlock increases the base cost of every subsequent tech. This means the order in which you research matters: researching low-value techs early inflates the cost of everything you want later.

The scaling formula uses the total unlocked tech count as an exponent, and then multiplies by a Stage Multiplier based on which ring of the tech tree the technology sits in:

StageCost MultiplierNotes
Stage 1×1.25Innermost ring — relatively cheap
Stage 2×2Second ring
Stage 3×7.5Sharp jump — mid-game techs
Stage 4×20Late-game techs, very expensive
Stage 5×50Near-endgame
Stage 6 (Wonder)×50 (modified)Victory condition techs

The quadrant (Science & Exploration, Economy & Trade, Empire Development, Military) has no inherent cost multiplier — what matters is the Stage. However, faction-specific quadrant discounts can reduce costs within a specific tree.

RESEARCH BLOAT
Researching techs you don't immediately need raises the cost of everything you do need. The jump from Stage 2 (×2) to Stage 3 (×7.5) is particularly punishing — unnecessary Stage 2 techs make your Stage 3 priorities significantly more expensive. Be deliberate: every tech you unlock is a cost multiplier applied to your entire future research.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
📋 Queue Discipline STRATEGY ▾

The research queue accepts multiple techs at once — but queueing many techs in advance is a trap, especially in the first 30–40 turns. Conditions change faster than queues do. You may queue a tech that requires Titanium but find you're not producing Titanium yet; or queue an expansion tech while your Approval is deteriorating and expansion is the last thing you should be doing.

The recommended practice in the early game: research one tech at a time, and only queue the next when the current one is near completion. This forces deliberate evaluation of your empire state at each decision point.

Leftover Science does not disappear — it carries over to the next research for one turn. This means if a tech completes mid-turn, the overage contributes to whatever you queue next, provided you queue it promptly. Alt+clicking a tech in the queue moves it to the front, which is useful for reacting to sudden changes in priority.

The Economy & Trade Tech Tree: Industry & Dust

The tree that builds your economic foundation. Industry comes first; Dust infrastructure follows.
⚙️ Tree Structure & Priority Logic MECHANIC ▾

The Economy & Trade quadrant is the primary source of Industry and Dust improvements, system development upgrades, trade company access, and Marketplace unlocks. It is the most universally relevant tree in the first 30 turns for any faction not running a pure Science or Military strategy. You need 1 tech to unlock Stage 2, 2 to unlock Stage 3, 4 to unlock Stage 4, and 3 to unlock Stage 5.

Stage 1 — Two techs, both worth taking. Xenolinguistics (Industry affinity — Industrialists) unlocks Xeno-Industrial Infrastructure (Industry improvement) and Titanium Refining. This is the near-universal first research priority: the Industry improvement effectively doubles early home system output on most planet types, and Titanium Refining lets you exploit any Titanium deposits you've already found. The second Stage 1 tech, Plasma Metallurgy (Industrialists), unlocks Interplanetary Transport Network (another Industry improvement) and Hyperium Capture. Both techs are cheap (131 Science base), both contribute to unlocking Stage 2, and both improve Industry — take them in sequence.

Stage 2 — Five techs; two are mandatory, three are situational. Galactic Commodities (Pacifists) unlocks a Miners Union Industry improvement and — critically — the G-Goods Exchange, which activates the Marketplace empire-wide. This is a required pick: the Marketplace is a core economic tool from this point forward. Multi-Thread Management (Pacifists) unlocks the Intergalactic Supermarket Dust improvement and the Accelerated Production buyout improvement, which reduces buyout costs. The other Stage 2 techs are: Astrofinance (Pacifists) — Dust system improvement + Dust planetary improvement; Atmospheric Filtration (Pacifists) — Approval improvement + unlocks Ash planet colonization; and Behemoth Exploitation 1 (Industrialists, Supremacy DLC) — Behemoth mining modules. The two Dust improvements are worth taking when your Dust income is thin; the Ash colonization is faction-dependent. You need any 2 of these 5 to open Stage 3.

Stage 3 — The economic core unlocks here. The tier header unlocks Basic System Development (System Plan Level 2) automatically when you enter Stage 3 — this is the modernization upgrade that opens the second luxury resource slot and increases pop capacity. Among the Stage 3 techs: Neural Robotics (Industrialists) unlocks Predictive Logistics (Industry system improvement), Adamantian Refining, and Industrial Zones (Industry planetary improvement) — a high-priority pick for Industry-focused empires. Commercial Frameworks (Pacifists) unlocks the Trade Company HQ and Subsidiary buildings — the only way to build Trade Companies. Impactless Sites (Pacifists) unlocks a Dust improvement, the G-Services Exchange (a Marketplace upgrade that expands what's available for purchase), and — notably — the Vacate System decolonization action, which lets you sell an underperforming outpost back for Dust and remove its overcolonization penalty. Extreme Atmospherics (Industrialists) unlocks an Industry improvement, Antimatter Capture, and Gas-Giant colonization.

Stage 4 — Industry and Dust scaling for established empires. Key picks: Quantized Economics (Pacifists) — Dust system and planetary improvements. Crust Engineering (Industrialists) — Industry planetary improvement and Orichalcix Refining. Stage 4 also contains the G-Services Exchange upgrade enabling hero and mercenary purchases on the Marketplace, and Quadrinix Refining access. You're building toward completion here — pick what your empire's current bottleneck is (Dust vs. Industry vs. resource access).

Stage 5 — Late-game efficiency improvements. Stage 5 contains the Industry planetary improvement Self-Mining NPO (via Autonomous Mining) and the turn-reduction improvement Urgent AI Research (Science, via same tech). The P.O.W.E.R. Armor tech unlocks an Industry system improvement and a population-boosting empire improvement (Floating Superstructures). These are strong but expensive — only relevant when your Industry and Science foundations are already mature.

WHAT THIS TREE DOES NOT CONTAIN
The Economy & Trade tree does not provide ship hulls (those are in Empire Development) or most military modules. It also does not contain any direct Industry-to-Science or Industry-to-Dust conversion improvements — that mechanic is governed by the conversion rates in the system panel and does not require a specific tech unlock. Do not research off-path techs expecting to find a conversion technology here; invest those tech slots elsewhere.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
💰 Dust: What It Funds MECHANIC ▾

Dust is the general-purpose currency. Its two mandatory functions are upkeep (ships, buildings, heroes all cost Dust per turn) and the Marketplace. Running negative Dust causes upkeep failures — ships and improvements stop functioning. Beyond that, Dust can be spent to:

  • BUYOUTAccelerate construction by spending Dust to complete a queued building or ship immediately. The cost formula is nonlinear: ((Industry Cost)^1.1) + 100. Buying out small, nearly-complete projects costs much less per Industry point than buying out large fresh ones. Rushing 7 cheap improvements costs roughly 25% less than rushing one expensive equivalent.
  • RETROFITUpgrading ships to a new design costs Dust proportional to the difference between the old and new module set. Retrofitting often-used ship designs repeatedly is a significant ongoing Dust expense — sequence retrofits carefully.
  • OUTPOST GROWTHSpending Dust speeds up outpost-to-colony conversion, supplementing the Food supply route.
  • SELL SHIPSShips can be sold for Dust. In the early game with short build times, building cheap ships and selling them can be an effective Dust generation method if Industry is abundant.

Strategic & Luxury Resources. The Marketplace.

Strategic resources gate ship modules. Luxury resources multiply system output. The Marketplace connects supply to need.
⚡ Strategic Resources: Gates for Military Power MECHANIC ▾

Strategic resources are the gating material for advanced ship modules. Without them, you cannot equip the relevant weapon, defense, or support modules — you can still build ships, but they're limited to basic loadouts. The six strategic resources in order of availability: Titanium, Hyperium (visible from the start with basic probes), then Adamantium, Antimatter (require mid-tier curiosity detection), then Orichalcix, Quadrinics (require advanced curiosity detection from the Science & Exploration tree).

Beyond ship modules, each strategic resource provides a passive per-population FIDSI bonus on any planet where a deposit is present and the exploitation tech has been researched:

ResourceTierPer-Pop BonusExploitation Tech (E&T stage)
Titanium1+1 IndustryXenolinguistics (Stage 1)
Hyperium1+1 SciencePlasma Metallurgy (Stage 1)
Adamantian2+2 IndustryNeural Robotics (Stage 3)

Tier 1 deposits spawn frequently on Tier 1 planets (colonizable from the start) and can be spotted from the galaxy map from turn 1. Tier 2 deposits require Evaporation Inhibitors to access curiosities. Tier 3 deposits are rare and low-abundance — Orichalcix spawns on Sterile planets, Quadrinix on Gas planets. Their primary value remains military access; the per-pop bonuses are a secondary benefit worth noting when deciding which planets to prioritize for population placement.

Strategic resources are also tradeable on the Marketplace. Selling surplus Titanium or Hyperium for Dust is a valid early-game income strategy, especially if your military needs are low. The AI actively buys strategic resources — prices respond to supply and demand across the galaxy.

💎 Luxury Resources: FIDSI Multipliers & Population Boosts MECHANIC ▾

Luxury resources have two distinct uses: system development slots and population mood boosts.

System development slots: Each modernization level has one or more luxury slots. Assigning a luxury to a slot costs a quantity of that resource and grants the system a permanent bonus. The bonus type depends on which luxury you assign — there are eight FIDSI/support categories — and the bonus strength depends on the luxury's tier:

Luxury TierResources Consumed per SlotBonus TypeRepresentative Effect
Common (T1)40 / 75 / 125 (Levels 1–3)Flat system bonuse.g. +60 Food, +60 Industry, +30 Influence on system
Uncommon (T2)20 / 40 / 60 (Levels 1–3)Per-population bonuse.g. +4 Food/pop, +4 Industry/pop, +4 Science/pop
Rare (T3)10 / 20 / 30 (Levels 1–3)% per system levele.g. +15% Food/Science/Dust per system level

The implication of this structure is that Tier 1 luxuries are best used early on new systems (flat bonuses matter most when per-pop production is low), Tier 2 luxuries pay off on systems with high population, and Tier 3 luxuries are strongest on high-level systems — their % bonus multiplies against a larger base. Never waste a Tier 3 slot on a fresh colony.

Population mood boosts: Each population type demands a specific luxury resource. Fulfilling that demand gives the population type an Approval bonus and doubles its growth weight (from 50 to 100 base chance per turn). This means satisfying a population's luxury demand simultaneously improves local Approval and accelerates the growth of that pop type — a meaningful feedback loop for system political alignment, since growing more of a pop type shifts Senate votes toward their affiliated party.

TIER 3 SLOT DISCIPLINE
Tier 3 luxury resources are scarce and the bonus they provide is substantially larger than Tier 1 or 2. Never assign a Tier 3 luxury to a low-output system or an early slot. Reserve them for your highest-production systems and their highest modernization slots, where the percentage bonus multiplies against the largest base.

🏪 The Marketplace: Supply, Demand & Pricing MECHANIC ▾

The Marketplace unlocks through the Economy & Trade tree and allows buying and selling both strategic and luxury resources for Dust. An upgraded Marketplace (a further tech) also allows purchasing heroes and mercenary ships.

Prices are dynamic and respond to galaxy-wide supply and demand. Early in a game, rare resources command high prices; as more empires produce and sell them, prices fall. If you dump a large quantity of one resource onto the market simultaneously, you'll suppress its price for several turns. The AI actively uses the Marketplace — you can see which empire is buying or selling after researching the relevant tech.

Practical uses:

  • SELL SURPLUSIf you produce more of a strategic resource than your ships require, sell the excess for Dust. Early Titanium surpluses can fund your first wave of improvements.
  • BUY GAPSIf you need 2 Adamantium to complete a ship design but don't produce it, buy it rather than delaying or redesigning. At moderate quantities, the Marketplace is cheaper than the opportunity cost of a multi-turn delay.
  • PRICE WATCHINGThe Marketplace reflects what other empires are building. A sudden drop in Hyperium price means someone is selling — possibly because they upgraded away from Hyperium-dependent modules. Timing your purchases around price dips can save significant Dust over a long game.

System Development & Planet Specializations

Modernization levels unlock luxury slots and population capacity. Planetary specializations shape individual planet output.
🏙️ System Modernization Levels MECHANIC ▾

Systems can be upgraded through modernization levels (1 through 5) by assigning luxury resources to their development slots. Each level requires researching the corresponding Economy & Trade tech first, then assigning the required number of luxury resources, and then building the upgrade in the system's construction queue.

Modernization levels provide two compounding benefits: additional luxury resource slots (unlocking more FIDSI bonuses for that system) and increased maximum population capacity (allowing more pops, which multiplies base production further). Higher levels require more luxury resources — Levels 4 and 5 require Tier 2 and Tier 3 luxuries respectively.

Level 2 modernization additionally unlocks the Spaceport, enabling population transfer. This is the most direct reason to prioritize getting your most productive systems to Level 2 early — it opens the population optimization loop.

RESOURCE SELECTION IS PERMANENT
When you initiate a Development project you must choose which luxury resource(s) to assign to that level's slot(s). This selection cannot be changed once confirmed. Before committing, consider which luxury resources you currently have in surplus, which you are likely to acquire more of, and which FIDSI output that system is being built around. Assigning a Food luxury to a system you later specialise for Industry is a permanent inefficiency. Plan the system's long-term role before confirming the slot.

🪐 System Improvements vs. Planetary Specializations MECHANIC ▾

The game distinguishes four categories of buildable improvements, each with different scope:

  • SYSTEM IMPROVEMENTSApply to the entire system (all planets). Can be built in any system you own. Examples: Xeno-Industrial Infrastructure, Public-Private Partnerships. These are your workhorses — build them everywhere applicable.
  • UNIQUE IMPROVEMENTSApply to the entire empire but can only be built once total. A laurel wreath icon identifies them in the tech tree. Examples: Arc University. Build these in your highest-output system to maximize the benefit, but the effect is global.
  • EMPIRE IMPROVEMENTSApply to the entire empire automatically when researched — no construction required. A full-wreath icon. These are passive bonuses that activate immediately on research. Examples: Supplied Happiness Program.

POLITICAL IMPACT OF SPECIALIZATIONS
Each specialization you build adds Senate support for its affiliated party from that system. Building Industry specializations pushes systems toward Industrialists; Influence specializations push toward Religious. If you're deliberately engineering Senate composition (see Vol.2), your specialization choices are also political choices — not just output optimization.
AUTOMATION POLICY
Systems have an automation policy that auto-queues improvements matching a chosen focus (Science, Industry, etc.). This is convenient for low-value conquered systems you don't want to micromanage. However, automation overrides manual improvement choices — if a system has a unique deposit or anomaly you want to exploit, disable automation and manage it manually to avoid losing those benefits.

Trade Companies & Trade Routes

A supplementary Dust income stream. Worth building in the right circumstances — not a priority for every faction or strategy.
🚢 How Trade Companies Work MECHANIC ▾

Trade Companies are unlocked via Commercial Frameworks (Economy & Trade tree, approximately Stage 3). You build a Trade Company HQ in one system and Subsidiaries in other systems. The network of routes between them generates Dust (and small amounts of Industry and strategic resources) each turn.

Income scales with route distance — longer routes between HQ and Subsidiaries generate more Dust. This means placement matters significantly: a Subsidiary on a system far from your HQ via peaceful trade agreement lanes earns more than a Subsidiary one hop away. The relationship requires a Trade Agreement with any faction whose territory the route passes through.

Each Trade Company can support multiple Subsidiary ships. Upgrading the company level (by building additional HQ improvements) increases the number of active routes and their income.

HQ PLACEMENT RULE
Place your Trade Company HQ in your highest-Industry system — not your highest-Dust system. HQ construction and leveling are paid in Industry; the income comes from the route network once built. Place Subsidiaries at the most distant reachable systems from the HQ to maximize route income.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
⚖️ When to Build Trade Companies STRATEGY ▾

Trade companies are not universally worthwhile. The opportunity cost is real — the HQ and Subsidiaries consume Industry and Dust upkeep, and the Commercial Frameworks research occupies a tech slot. Evaluate before committing:

  • BUILD WHENYou have a Counselor hero with trade-related skills (the income multiplier can make routes substantially more valuable). You are playing a faction with trade bonuses (Lumeris convert trade income to Influence; Umbral Choir have enhanced trade mechanics). You have peaceful neighbors with distant systems reachable via Trade Agreement, maximizing route length.
  • SKIP WHENYour Dust income from FIDSI is already healthy and you'd rather spend the Industry and tech slots on expansion or military. You're in an aggressive war posture where trade agreements are unstable. Your available systems are clustered closely, keeping routes short and income low.

For most factions in a standard game, one well-placed Trade Company with 3–4 Subsidiaries is sufficient to justify the investment. Building multiple companies is primarily a Lumeris or Counselor-hero strategy.

The Economic Behemoths

A unique ship class unlocked mid-game. Economic Behemoths orbit systems to boost output or mine special nodes for resources.
🌌 Unlocking & Building Behemoths MECHANIC ▾

Behemoths are a ship class added by the Supremacy DLC. They are not available from the start — they unlock via a cooperative global quest called Words in Private, which requires all factions in the galaxy to collectively research a minimum number of Military technologies. Once the quest completes, every faction gains access to the Behemoth Blueprints tech, which must then be individually researched to start building Behemoths.

Exception: the Hissho faction starts with a Behemoth available from turn one.

Once blueprints are unlocked, three default Behemoth chassis designs are provided: Economic Behemoth, Scientific Behemoth, and Military Behemoth. These can be further customized in the ship design screen. Behemoths have a fleet limit — you cannot have more active Behemoths (including Citadels, which also count) than your faction's cap, which is determined by techs and certain hero skills.

UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
📈 Economic Behemoth Functions MECHANIC ▾

The Economic Behemoth chassis accommodates mining and yield modules rather than weapons. Its primary functions:

  • SYSTEM ORBIT BONUSModules that buff orbited systems provide +10 to FIDSI (Food, Industry, Dust, Science — not Influence by default) per turn to any system the Behemoth is docked at. This is a flat bonus, not a percentage — its relative value decreases as systems scale but remains significant in the early-mid game.
  • PLANET MININGSpecific modules allow the Behemoth to mine planets for strategic or luxury resources that the planet cannot otherwise produce. This is the primary use of Economic Behemoths mid-game — parking one at a resource-rich system extracts additional strategic resources directly.
  • SPECIAL NODE EXTRACTIONParking a Behemoth at special nodes (asteroid fields, nebulae, and similar non-system map features) provides bonus strategic resources or Science per turn. Some nodes are only accessible via Behemoth, making them worthless without one.

Behemoths cannot be embedded in regular fleets — they operate alone. However, if a fleet and a Behemoth are at the same system simultaneously, they can reinforce each other in combat. Military Behemoths are covered in Vol.4; Scientific Behemoths and Special Nodes are covered in Vol.3.

UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
🏰 Behemoth Specializations: Obliterator & Citadel MECHANIC ▾

Behemoths can be converted into permanent specialized forms requiring additional tech research:

Obliterator: A Military Behemoth variant that fires charged shots capable of destroying entire star systems. Reloads over multiple turns; firing costs Orichalcix and Quadrinics, with the cost decreasing the longer it has been since the last shot. When an obliterator fires, every empire in the galaxy receives a notification (but not the target location). Obliterator shots are slow enough to intercept visually on the map. This is a Vol.4 topic — mentioned here because the defense against it (the Citadel) is economy-relevant.

Citadel: A Behemoth converted into a permanent stationary defense installation on a specific system. It cannot be moved or changed after placement. A Citadel blocks Obliterator shots targeting its host system — the primary reason to build one. Citadels count against your Behemoth cap, so placing too many reduces your mobile Behemoth capacity. Only deploy Citadels on your highest-value systems (home system, critical resource hubs) where permanent protection justifies permanently consuming a Behemoth slot.

Empire Diagnostic Routines

The primary bottleneck is not always the lowest number on screen. This is how you read a stuck empire and sequence the fix.
🔗 The Three Coupled Systems MECHANIC ▾

Vol.1's mechanics don't operate independently. They form three coupled systems where a failure in one suppresses the output of the others. Diagnosing a stuck empire means identifying which coupling is most broken, not which number is lowest.

This is a mid-game diagnostic framework — the point in the game where you have 4+ systems, are at or near your overcolonization limit, and feel like growth has stalled despite a reasonable FIDSI base. It is not a build-order priority guide. Early game, while you are within your overcolonization limit, expanding does not trigger Approval penalties — colonising freely and prioritising Industry is correct, and Approval is a background check rather than a primary concern. The couplings below become live constraints once your empire is large enough that each new system costs −10 Approval empire-wide. That is when the diagnostic sequence matters.

Coupling 1 — Science ↔ Industry. Science unlocks what Industry can build. The productive state is both roughly in pace: Science completes a tech in ~5–10 turns while Industry is building the improvements that tech just unlocked. The two failure modes are mirror images of each other, and the true diagnostic signal is the same in both cases: whether the backlog of unlocked-but-unbuilt improvements is growing or shrinking. (Note: this backlog framing is an analytical framework derived from the underlying mechanics — no community source consulted for this guide explicitly articulates it this way. The direction is consistent with experienced player advice but treat it as a reasoned inference, not a sourced claim.)

(a) Science outpaces Industry — each tech you research unlocks improvements faster than Industry can build them. The backlog grows turn after turn. Whether those improvements are queued or sitting unqueued in the tree is irrelevant — queuing them doesn't build them faster if Industry output is insufficient. The symptom is a pile of available improvements that never seems to clear. Researching more techs here makes things worse: each additional unlock adds to the backlog and inflates all future tech costs. (b) Industry outpaces Science — the backlog is empty because nothing useful has been unlocked yet. Industry finishes what's available and then sits idle waiting for the next tech. The symptom is systems with genuinely nothing left to build. An empty queue here is a legitimate signal — but only if you've already verified there are no unlocked improvements remaining anywhere in the tree. Neither stat in isolation tells you which mode you're in. The diagnostic question is: is the pool of unlocked-but-unbuilt improvements getting larger over time? If yes, Industry is the bottleneck. If it's draining to zero and staying there, Science is.

Coupling 2 — Population ↔ FIDSI output. Population is the multiplier for nearly all FIDSI. A system at 4/9 pop capacity is not producing half what it could — it's producing less, because later pops stack on top of base planet output and improvements. The practical implication: a system with a large population gap is often a better investment target than a new colony. New colonies start at near-zero output and take many turns to develop. An existing underpopulated system can roughly double its output with 4 additional pops, with no colonization cost and no Approval hit.

Coupling 3 — Expansion ↔ Approval ↔ FIDSI multiplier. This coupling is inactive while you are within your overcolonization limit — colonising a new system below the threshold costs no Approval. Once you exceed the limit, each additional system adds −10 Approval empire-wide. If that penalty crosses a tier threshold (e.g. 71% → 69%, dropping from Loyal to Content), you lose +15% on all Dust and Science simultaneously. The new system contributes near-zero FIDSI at turn 1. The payback window — how many turns until the new system produces more than what the empire-wide tier loss costs — is typically 10–25 turns depending on system quality and how quickly you can populate it (note: this estimate is not sourced to a specific reference; treat as an analytical approximation). Expansion past your limit is not unconditionally bad, but the cost is real and must be calculated rather than ignored.

THE DIAGNOSTIC SEQUENCE
When your empire feels stuck, check in this order — not because later items don't matter, but because earlier ones suppress everything downstream:

1. Is Empire Approval below a tier threshold? This is the first check in a mid-game diagnostic because it is invisible suppression — if you're at 67% instead of 70%, all Dust and Science are already reduced by −15% before you read them. Diagnosing Industry or Science output while the multiplier is sub-threshold means you're optimising against a distorted baseline. Fix the multiplier first, then re-read the numbers. (This ordering applies to diagnosis only — early game, while within your overcolonization limit, Approval costs you nothing per new system and industry-first expansion is correct.)

2. Are your key systems severely underpopulated? A 3/8 pop system is wasting most of its FIDSI potential regardless of what improvements you've built. Population gap = latent production waiting to be activated.

3. Is Science and Industry in pace? Neither the queue length nor whether improvements are queued tells you directly which is the bottleneck. The real question is: is the pool of unlocked-but-unbuilt improvements growing over time? If every few turns another tech completes and the improvement backlog keeps getting longer — whether those improvements are queued or not — Industry can't keep up with Science's output. Stop researching and let Industry drain the backlog. If the backlog empties and stays empty between tech completions, Science is the constraint — Industry is idle waiting for new unlocks.

4. Is Food sustainable at projected population? Food consumption is nonlinear — a system growing toward max pop will hit Food stress sharply. Starvation cascades: pop dies → FIDSI drops → further instability.

📋 Turn 35 Failure Snapshot — Worked Example STRATEGY ▾

Here is a compound failure state. Read the snapshot and identify what went wrong before reading the diagnosis.

Snapshot — Turn 35, Normal Speed
Systems colonized5 (System 5 colonized ~5 turns ago)
Empire Approval61% — Content tier
Empire Industry560/turn total
Empire Science460/turn total
Empire Dust250/turn total
System 3 (best Cold system)2 pop / 5 max. Has 2 Cold planets with no Science improvements built.
System 5 (newest)1 pop / 4 max. Contributing ~20 Industry, 30 Science, 20 Dust.
Improvement backlog6 improvements unlocked across E&T and S&E trees, unbuilt. Stage 3 E&T tech completing in ~1.5 turns will unlock 2–3 more.
Tech queueStage 3 E&T tech in progress — 680 Science cost, ~1.5 turns remaining.

Primary failure — Approval at 61%. The tier below Loyal (70%) means all Dust and Science empire-wide are taking a −15% penalty. At 460 Science/turn, that's roughly 80 Science/turn suppressed — close to the output of a decent Cold system. The player is looking at Science numbers that are already degraded and may not notice. The most likely cause: colonizing System 5 pushed Empire Approval across the 70% threshold. System 5 is currently contributing ~70 FIDSI units/turn while the empire-wide tier loss is costing multiples of that.

Secondary failure — System 3 is wasted. 2 pops on a 5-slot system with 2 Cold planets. Cold planets produce the highest Science per pop, but the 3 empty slots produce nothing. At even 40 Science/pop (Cold planet base), the idle potential is ~120 Science/turn sitting unactivated. The system has high ceiling but essentially zero utilisation.

Tertiary — growing improvement backlog. Six improvements are unlocked and unbuilt, and the Stage 3 E&T tech completing in ~1.5 turns will unlock 2–3 more. The backlog is not draining — it is growing. This is the signal that Industry cannot keep pace with Science's unlock rate, regardless of how those improvements are queued. Researching yet another tech at this point adds to a pile that Industry is already falling behind on, while inflating the cost of every future tech in the process.

Corrective sequence — in order:

  • 1STStop expanding. Do not colonize a 6th system until Approval recovers to 70%+, or until Xeno Anthropology (ED Stage 3, +1 overcolonization threshold) is researched.
  • 2NDPause Science research and drain the improvement backlog. With 6 unlocked improvements already available and more incoming, let Industry work through them before unlocking further. Prioritise Science improvements on System 3's Cold planets first — that's where the per-pop output is highest and where the population gap (see below) will activate the most value.
  • 3RDGrow System 3. Prioritise Food production on your Food-surplus systems and use Spaceport transfers to seed population into System 3's empty slots. This activates the latent Science output sitting idle there.
  • 4THRe-evaluate System 5 honestly. At 1 pop / 4 max contributing ~70 FIDSI/turn and costing an empire-wide Approval tier, it may be running a net negative for another 15–20 turns. This does not mean raze it — but it means the expansion timing was premature and the recovery window must be acknowledged.
THE UNDERLYING PATTERN
This failure state is not unusual. It's the predictable result of expanding to a new system before the previous systems' population gaps were filled, causing an Approval drop that suppressed the empire-wide multiplier, while idle Industry and under-staffed Cold planets amplified the Science shortfall. Each failure reinforces the others. The fix is always the same: restore the multiplier first, then activate latent capacity, then resume expansion from a stable base.
