Outside your Empire

Exploration mechanics, galaxy features, and the factions that exist beyond your borders — from movement fundamentals to the strategic depth of minor faction assimilation.

01

Exploring: Movement Points, Star Lanes, Wormholes and Warp Travel

How fleets move through the galaxy and the timing rules that can cost you a turn
🧭 Movement Points and the Star Lane Network MECHANIC ▾

Every fleet has a pool of Movement Points (MPs) consumed each time the fleet moves along a star lane to an adjacent system. MPs are a per-ship stat displayed on the ship design screen. Fleet speed is capped by its slowest ship — one low-MP vessel in your fleet degrades the entire formation. If you retrofit a scout ship with a stack of engines, that scout alone can give the whole fleet a meaningful speed boost via fleet accelerator support modules.

The star lane network is the primary highway: two systems connected by a visible lane can be traversed in one movement action, provided the fleet has enough MPs. Once you have vision on a system — either by visiting it or by a probe passing through — that system remains visible permanently without a ship stationed there. Discovered systems also auto-reveal all star lanes emanating from them.

FLEET SPEED BOTTLENECK
When building mixed fleets, always check the slowest ship's MP value. A single colony ship or support vessel dragging your strike force to 6 MPs instead of 10 costs you real turns on long-range operations. Either keep slow ships in separate fleets or use fleet accelerator support modules to compensate.

The Auto-Explore fleet stance fans the fleet out and autonomously follows open star lanes, revealing adjacent systems each turn. Useful in the early game, but be aware the AI pathing will not prioritize high-value directions — manual guidance beats auto-explore once you've scouted enough to see where the good systems are.

UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
⏱️ The End-of-Turn Movement Rule MECHANIC STRATEGY ▾

This is the rule that catches players out most often: if a fleet's movement order spans multiple turns, the final leg resolves at the end of the turn — not the beginning. This has a concrete tactical consequence.

Suppose a fleet is four turns away from a destination system and you set a multi-turn waypoint, then hit End Turn. The fleet will arrive at the destination system and sit there — but only after everything else on that turn has already resolved. If you intended to arrive and then attack a pirate fleet in that system on the same turn, you cannot: the attack opportunity has already passed for that turn. The pirates may also have moved by the following turn.

ALWAYS MOVE MANUALLY FOR SAME-TURN ACTIONS
Whenever you need to arrive at a system and take an action there on the same turn — attack, establish a blockade, collect a curiosity — move the fleet manually before ending your turn. Multi-turn queued movement will cost you an entire action window on arrival.

The Sleep fleet stance parks the fleet in orbit and wakes it automatically when a probe or enemy enters the system. Useful for stationing a patrol ship at a minor faction's system to intercept pirate spawns without consuming your attention each turn.

The Guard stance is distinct from Sleep: a guarding fleet actively intercepts any enemy ship entering the system, forcing it into orbit and halting its remaining MPs. While a fleet is guarding an enemy system, it also begins suppressing that system's resource output — luxury and strategic resources stop flowing to the defender. This is the blockade mechanic.

🌀 Wormholes, Warp Travel and Unconnected Systems MECHANIC ▾

The galaxy is divided into constellations — clusters of systems connected internally by star lanes. Constellations never connect to each other via star lanes. The only ways to cross between constellations are warp drive (free movement through open space, unlocked by Baryonic Shielding) or wormholes. There are typically around 3 wormholes discoverable in each constellation, each connecting to a nearby constellation. Wormholes are substantially more numerous near the galactic core.

The galactic core is its own constellation — usually containing large, high-value systems and almost always hosting The Academy. Systems there are harder to defend due to exposure from multiple directions, but the economic and strategic rewards are significant. Sending probes toward the core early to locate The Academy and map those systems before rivals do is consistently worth the investment.

Wormholes are bidirectional shortcuts between distant systems. Unlike star lane travel, wormhole transit expends all remaining movement points for that turn — but only any amount of MPs is required to enter. A fleet with 1 of 9 MPs remaining can still use a wormhole. The practical implication: move along star lanes first, then end the turn's movement by jumping through the wormhole, so the full-MP drain doesn't waste a standing movement budget.

Wormhole detection requires Autonomous Materials (Science S3), which unlocks the Hawking Radiation Dowser. Without it, wormholes in unexplored space won't appear on your map. Source: confirmed from in-game observation (wiki article also lists the Hawking Radiation Dowser under Autonomous Materials, S3).

Free movement — travel to systems not connected to the star lane network — is unlocked by Baryonic Shielding (Science T2), which grants the Warp Drive (Free Movement) module. This is what allows access to unconnected systems and special nodes.

Compact Warp Methods (Science T3) is a distinct and separate technology. It does not grant the core warp capability — that is Baryonic Shielding's role. Compact Warp Methods instead unlocks Moonbase Alpha (a system improvement) and Eco Habitats, plus a Moon Exploration planetary improvement. In practice, it is the tech that makes moon colonization meaningful infrastructure rather than just accessible.

Further movement upgrades continue through the tree: Science T4 adds a warp boost via N-Dimensional Topologies (Deep Space Fuel Scoop) and an Accelerated Warp Drive module. Science T5 unlocks the Anti-Matter Engine and Hyperlight Acceleration.

The Low Kelvin Sciences technology (Science T4) unlocks Ultra-MRI Astronomy, which gives system vision of special nodes — the asteroid fields, black holes, and nebulae that science behemoths exploit. Without it, those nodes don't appear on your map at all.

COLONY SHIP TIMING
A common early mistake is immediately dispatching the colony ship toward an unexplored system. Hold it. With two probes, you have a one-in-three chance of the best available system being in the wrong direction. Spending 1–2 turns scouting adjacent lanes before committing the colony ship costs less total turns than moving it the wrong way and backtracking.

The Science & Exploration Tech Tree

Probes, movement, science production — and the two early techs that change what you can find
🗺️ Tree Location and Structure MECHANIC ▾

The Science & Exploration tree occupies the bottom (south) quadrant of the tech circle. Stage unlock thresholds are 1 / 3 / 3 / 3 technologies required to unlock Stages 2 / 3 / 4 / 5. Note Stage 3 requires three Stage 2 techs — you cannot cheaply leapfrog to the mid-game tech tier. This is a more demanding unlock curve than the Military tree (1/2/3/4).

Each stage also grants free empire-wide unlocks on reaching the threshold: Stage 1 gives Common Curiosities and the String Gravitics Engine module; Stage 2 gives Warp Drive and Nano-repair Bots; Stage 3 gives Uncommon Curiosities and the Hyperium Engine; Stage 4 gives Rare Curiosities and Titanium Colonizers; Stage 5 gives the Anti-Matter Engine.

On a medium galaxy at normal speed, Stage 5 may not be reachable until well past turn 100. Factor detrimental anomalies into your colonization decision — don't assume a fix is coming.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
📊 Science Production Buildings and the Research Queue STRATEGY ▾

The Science tree also contains science output buildings — most notably Public–Private Partnerships, which mirrors Xeno-Industrial Infrastructure from the Economy tree but for science instead of industry. On a high-adjacency planet, this building can near-double your science production from a single system. On a Terran Fertile planet, for instance, it provides the same kind of step-change that Xeno-Industrial does for industry.

Science has a storage cap of two turns' worth of production. If you're producing 44 science per turn, you can bank up to 88 before the overflow is wasted. This means it's safe to leave the research queue empty for one turn without losing output — but not two. Plan your queue so you're picking the next tech before overflow kicks in, not after.

QUEUE ONE TECH AT A TIME
Any science overflow from completing a tech automatically rolls into the next queued tech. If you leave the queue empty, overflow science is banked up to the two-turn cap. Queuing multiple techs in advance doesn't lose you anything, but the habit of queuing one and then checking what's next ensures you make deliberate choices rather than defaulting to whatever was pre-selected.

Probes & Expedition Power

The two ways probes are used, and why your hero should always be on the scout ship
📡 Probe Mechanics: Range, Lifetime and Cooldown MECHANIC ▾

Probes are launched from ships that carry a probe module — Exploration or Hero ships only. The starting exploration ship comes with Improved Probes (2 stock). Probes can be added to hero ships as well, which is one of the best reasons to assign your starting hero to the scout fleet early — additional probe stock and vision multiply your early coverage meaningfully.

Each probe module has three stats that govern effectiveness:

  • VISIONThe detection radius around the probe's position as it moves. Higher vision reveals more of the surrounding area with each tile traveled.
  • STOCKHow many probes the module holds simultaneously. A module with Stock 3 means three separate probes can be in flight at once before any cooldown triggers.
  • COOLDOWNTurns to restock one probe after it depletes. A 1-turn cooldown means a probe is essentially always available; a 4-turn cooldown creates genuine coverage gaps.
ModuleReq. TechVisionStockCooldownCost
Improved Probes(initial)124 turns30 Industry
Titanium ProbesMachine Bacteria (S2)221 turn40 Industry + 1 Ti
Hyperium ProbesMachine Bacteria (S2)332 turns60 Industry + 1 Hy

The choice between Titanium and Hyperium is non-obvious. Titanium Probes have only a 1-turn cooldown — effectively always ready — making them reliable for sustained coverage even without staggering. Hyperium Probes have better vision (3 vs 2) and more stock (3 vs 2) but a 2-turn cooldown; the extra range makes them stronger for deep-space exploration where you want to see farther ahead. Explorer hulls also get a built-in −50% probe restock duration, which halves these cooldowns further, making both options faster than the base numbers suggest.

UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
🔍 Expeditions: Probes Used to Investigate Curiosities MECHANIC STRATEGY ▾

The second use of probes is to launch an expedition at a curiosity site. This is distinct from throwing a probe into open space. When you click the expedition icon next to a curiosity on a planet, the probe is consumed (starting a cooldown) and after some turns returns results: anomaly discoveries, resource deposits, loot, or quest triggers.

There is an alternative: investigating a curiosity via the system's build queue costs 100 Industry. At game start that is several turns of production. Using probes is free except for the cooldown. Always prefer probe expeditions over the build-queue method unless your probes are all on cooldown and the resource you might find would pay back the industry cost quickly (e.g., a luxury or strategic deposit in a system you're about to colonize).

HERO XP FROM PROBE DISCOVERIES
Heroes gain experience for each curiosity discovered — including those found by probes launched from a scout ship in the same fleet. This is why keeping the hero assigned to the exploration fleet, not stationed as a governor, is the correct early-game move. Every probe expedition nets hero XP at zero additional cost.

Some curiosities can trigger the discovery of exile population ships. These small civilian vessels automatically move toward your nearest system and, if they arrive safely, add a population unit. Exiles can include rare faction pops. There is no way to redirect their path once spawned — if pirates are between them and your system, you may lose the pop.

Curiosity Tiers, Types & Rewards

What curiosities are, what they yield, and why scanner technology gates your reward ceiling
🔭 Curiosity Types and What They Tend to Yield MECHANIC ▾

Curiosities appear as small icons on planets. Each has a type that predicts the reward category and also increments a specific political ideology in your empire when investigated:

TypeHigh ChanceLow ChancePolitical Output
AtmosphericAnomalyLootScientist
SubterraneanLuxury or Strategic Resource DepositLootScientist
Life FormAnomalyLuxury Resource Deposit, LootEcologist

Resource Deposit rewards scale with curiosity tier: Level 1 can only reveal Common resources (Titanium, Hyperium, or common luxuries). Higher tier curiosities can reveal Uncommon and Rare deposits. When a resource deposit is found, you also receive a small immediate quantity of that resource — useful early for marketplace access.

Loot rewards vary widely: fixed FIDSI amounts, a random ship, a unique module, a unique system improvement, or a unique battle tactic. These are not predictable but are consistently useful.

THE TRAP OUTCOME
All curiosities carry a small chance of failing entirely (no reward) or triggering a trap — spawning a small pirate fleet in the system that immediately attacks. This probability is low but real. Investigating a home-system curiosity in the first 10 turns is particularly high-risk: a pirate spawn next to your colony ship or lightly armed scout before you have a combat fleet available can be crippling. Atmospheric and Life Form types have the highest anomaly probability — which can cut both ways.

🔬 Curiosity Detection: Two Stats, Not One MECHANIC ▾

Your empire has two separate curiosity stats that advance independently: Curiosity Detection Level (which curiosities you can see on the map) and Expedition Power (which curiosities your fleet can actually investigate). A curiosity that exceeds your Expedition Power will appear on the map but remain un-investigable until your power catches up.

Both stats are advanced by researching specific techs — not by reaching a stage threshold:

Tech (Stage)Detection LevelExpedition Power
(starting)21
Baryonic Shielding (S2)32
Evaporation Inhibitors (S3)43
Ultra-Deep Habitats (S4)44

The stage-gated unlocks (Common / Uncommon / Rare curiosities existing in the world) are a separate layer on top of this — they determine which curiosity tier is generated in the galaxy. The Detection and Expedition Power stats determine whether your ships can interact with them. In practice: reach Stage 3 to unlock Uncommon curiosities empire-wide, and research Baryonic Shielding to actually see and probe them.

Note that Evaporation Inhibitors is the Primary Terraforming tech — its curiosity detection advancement is a secondary benefit that often goes unnoticed. If you're already researching it for terraforming purposes, your detection stats jump to 4/3 as a side effect.

Special Nodes and Science Behemoths

The passive FIDSI and tech-cost advantages that make science behemoths worth their unlock cost
⭐ Special Nodes: What They Are and How to Find Them MECHANIC ▾

Special nodes are galaxy-map locations outside the colonizable system network. They include asteroid fields, black holes, collapsing stars, nebular clouds, neutron stars, solar nebulae, and (Vaulters DLC) rejuvenation fields. They are not connected to the star lane grid and won't appear on your map without the Low Kelvin Sciences technology (Science S4), which unlocks Ultra-MRI Astronomy.

Each node type provides two things to a behemoth in orbit: a tech cost reduction in a specific tree branch, and a yield of two types of strategic resources (requires the relevant exploitation tech to activate). Each node reduces costs in a different branch — matching a behemoth to the right node for your current research priority is the core placement decision:

Node TypeTech Cost Reduction BranchStrategic Resources
Black HoleScience & ExplorationTitanium + Hyperium
Neutron StarScience & ExplorationOrichalcix + Quadrinix
Asteroid FieldEconomy & TradeAdamantian + Antimatter
Solar NebulaEconomy & TradeTitanium + Hyperium
Nebular CloudsEmpire DevelopmentOrichalcix + Quadrinix
Rejuvenation Field (Vaulters DLC)Empire Development—
Collapsing StarMilitaryAdamantian + Antimatter

All nodes apply a −2% / −3% Science cost penalty to a different branch when orbited — this is a minor negative that comes with the territory. The net value is strongly positive: the behemoth's own Science output (+80 or +160/turn from node modules) and the tech cost reduction in the matching branch far outweigh the penalty.

The Umbral Choir's home system is located at a special node by default, and they can plant Transmigration Beacons on other nodes — enabling them to receive that node's FIDSI effects on their home system and, as a last resort, teleport their home system there.

UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
🚀 The Behemoth Unlock Prerequisite MECHANIC ▾

Behemoths (introduced in the Supremacy DLC) are not available from the start. Access is gated behind a cooperative galaxy-wide quest: all players collectively need to unlock a combined threshold of Military tech tree technologies. The threshold scales with game speed settings. Once reached by the galaxy as a whole, every player gains access to the Behemoth Blueprints technology — the prerequisite for all behemoth construction and specialization techs.

The Hissho faction is the exception: they can begin the game with a behemoth and their Senate Leader trait can raise the behemoth count limit, which is normally capped by researching specific technologies.

Three base chassis designs unlock with Behemoth Blueprints: Economic, Scientific, and Military. These are the starting points for further specialization.

BEHEMOTH TECHS COST 25% MORE
All behemoth-specific technologies carry a built-in +25% science cost premium over equivalent-tier standard techs. Factor this into your research budget when planning the node-exploitation tech chain — each step costs meaningfully more than a comparable standard tech at the same stage.

The node-exploitation tech chain for the science behemoth runs through the Science tree as follows:

  • S2 — BEHEMOTH ANALYZERSUnlocks Basic Node Analyzer and Stellar Node Study 1 modules. Enables initial special-node interaction.
  • S3 — BEHEMOTH BASIC CLIMATOLOGYUnlocks Advanced Node Analyzer and the Climate Engineering behemoth module. Also the entry point for terraforming and anomaly-reduction capabilities (see T7).
  • S4 — BEHEMOTH ADVANCED CLIMATOLOGYUnlocks Advanced Climate Engineering and Stellar Node Study 2. The second tier of node yield and the full terraforming/reconstruction capability.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
📈 Science Behemoth: Passive FIDSI and Tech Cost Reduction STRATEGY ▾

The science behemoth's core value proposition comes in two layers:

Layer 1 — System orbit bonus: A behemoth orbiting any system (yours or otherwise) provides a flat +10 to all FIDSI categories (Food, Industry, Dust, Science — not Influence) on that system. This is a passive bonus requiring no further action. The AI regularly exploits this by parking behemoths over their developed systems as both a boost and a defense anchor.

Layer 2 — Tech cost reduction: With the Stellar Node Study modules equipped, the behemoth grants a −2% (Stellar Node Study 1) or −3% (Stellar Node Study 2) cost reduction to the tech branch matching the node type it's orbiting. This is per-node, per-behemoth — two behemoths at two different node types each reduce their respective branches independently, but two behemoths at the same node type do not double the reduction on that branch.

The exact module values from the wiki are:

ModuleTech RequiredScience OutputCost Reduction
Basic Node AnalyzerBehemoth Analyzers (S2)+50 Science, +50 Dust, +20 Influence—
Stellar Node Study 1Behemoth Analyzers (S2)+80 Science−2% matching branch
Advanced Node AnalyzerBehemoth Basic Climatology (S3)+25% Special Node Effect amplifier + resource yields—

SCIENCE VICTORY CORE LOOP — REVISED
The −2%/−3% reduction per behemoth per branch is smaller than it might initially appear in isolation, but it compounds: a single behemoth at a Black Hole gives −2% to your Science & Exploration branch continuously throughout the game. Combined with the +80/+160 Science output per turn and the Advanced Node Analyzer's resource yields, the behemoth pays back its research investment in compressed Science timelines and reduced tech costs over many turns. The goal is not to chase a massive single-turn discount — it's to establish a persistent mid-game advantage that widens with time.

Behemoths cannot be embedded in a regular fleet — they move and fight alone. This means a military behemoth replacing your strike fleet is almost always a weaker choice than building one alongside a conventional fleet. A military behemoth's module count per slot is multiplied by ×4 (same as all large ships), but the isolation penalty usually outweighs raw firepower for most combat situations.

Anomalies: Activation, Risk & Removal

Three anomaly categories, their FIDSI impact, and the tech path to neutralize detrimental effects
⚠️ The Three Anomaly Categories MECHANIC ▾

Anomalies are persistent modifiers on individual planets, applied at discovery (via curiosity expedition or automatic on colonization). They fall into three categories:

CategoryEffectExample
BeneficialPure FIDSI bonus — always positiveAntonov Rings: +Dust, +1 Science per population
DetrimentalPure FIDSI penalty — always negativeStrong Magnetic Field: significant FIDSI nerf on that planet
MixedHas both a positive and a negative component simultaneouslyPositive bonus to one FIDSI type, penalty to another

Anomalies apply at the planet level, not the system level. A system can have multiple planets each carrying different anomalies. When evaluating a system for colonization, the anomaly mix on all its planets should factor into your decision — a highly detrimental anomaly on a planet you'll rely on heavily can undermine a system that looked promising on paper.

UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
🔧 Anomaly Removal: OCP Investigation and Autonomous Materials MECHANIC STRATEGY ▾

Two Science tree technologies address anomaly effects — but they are at very different points in the research timeline:

  • AUTONOMOUS MATERIALS (S3)Unlocks Simple Anomaly Reduction. Strips the negative component of mixed anomalies while leaving the positive component intact. Accessible in the mid-game — a realistic early-to-mid priority if you have mixed anomalies on core planets.
  • ONTOLOGICAL REV-ENG (S5)Unlocks OCP Investigation Unit. Fully nullifies detrimental anomaly effects on colonized planets. This is a Stage 5 technology with a 5,256 base Science cost — late-game, not early-game.
THE OCP TRAP — DON'T PLAN AROUND IT
A detrimental anomaly on a colonized planet will be costing you FIDSI from the moment of colonization until Stage 5 research is complete. On a medium galaxy at normal speed that gap can easily be 80–120+ turns. A "Strong Magnetic Field" applying −4 Industry / −2 Science per turn for 100 turns is a 600-point cumulative Industry loss and a 200-point Science loss before the fix even lands. Treat detrimental anomalies as permanent penalties when evaluating systems to colonize, not as temporary problems. The fix exists — it just costs far more than it looks.

Anomaly reduction via the terraforming behemoth (Behemoth Basic Climatology module, Science S3) offers a middle path: the behemoth can reduce anomalies on colonized planets, including detrimental ones, without requiring Stage 5. This is covered in detail in T7. If you have a severe detrimental anomaly on a high-value planet and behemoths are available, this may be your fastest practical remedy.

Terraforming Behemoths

Four behemoth actions, two module tiers, and the pre-colonization edge that standard terraforming can't replicate
🔧 Module Chain and Tech Prerequisites MECHANIC ▾

Terraforming behemoth capability comes from two modules unlocked via the Science tree's behemoth climatology chain. Always note the 25% behemoth tech cost premium — both technologies cost more than a standard Science Stage tech at the same tier.

Tech (Stage)Module UnlockedCapabilities
Behemoth Basic Climatology (S3)Climate EngineeringTerraforming + anomaly reduction on valid targets
Behemoth Advanced Climatology (S4)Advanced Climate EngineeringAll of the above, faster + destroyed/depleted planet restoration

Using either module for terraforming always requires two separate prerequisites: the behemoth module tech (above) and the relevant standard terraforming tech for the target planet conversion. If you have the Climate Engineering module but haven't researched Advanced Terraforming (Climate Engineering tech, Science S4), you cannot perform Advanced Terraforming conversions even with a behemoth. The button will be greyed out. Both gates must be open.

The terraforming chart used by behemoths is identical to standard terraforming — the same conversion routes, the same planet-type restrictions. Gas Giants cannot be terraformed regardless of method. Behemoths are not a shortcut through the chart; they are an alternative delivery mechanism for the same actions, with unique targeting rules (see below).

UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
⚙️ How the Industry Mechanic Works: Accumulation and Stacking MECHANIC ▾

Behemoth terraforming is not instant. Each Climate Engineering module produces an Industry value per turn that accumulates until the threshold for the target action is met — exactly like a build queue. The exact values per module, from the wiki:

ModuleTerraformationAnomaly ReductionPlanetary ReconstructionDepletion Cancellation
Climate Engineering60 Industry/turn40 Industry/turn—passive
Advanced Climate Engineering120 Industry/turn80 Industry/turn80 Industry/turn2 depletion pts/turn

Two stacking rules apply:

  • WITHIN ONE BEHEMOTHMultiple Climate Engineering modules on the same behemoth add their Industry values together. A behemoth with three modules progresses roughly three times faster than one with a single module.
  • ACROSS MULTIPLE BEHEMOTHSMultiple behemoths orbiting the same system all contribute their Industry to the same active action. Parking two science behemoths over a system cuts the completion time roughly in half.
ONE ACTION PER SYSTEM AT A TIME
All behemoths in a system work on the same single action simultaneously. You cannot have behemoth A terraforming Planet 1 while behemoth B terraforms Planet 2 in the same system. Multi-behemoth setups accelerate a single action, they do not parallelize across planets. Plan your terraforming queue accordingly — the fastest approach is to stack all available behemoth Industry on one planet at a time.

🎯 The Four Behemoth Actions and Their Targeting Rules MECHANIC ▾

A terraforming behemoth with the appropriate module can perform four distinct actions. The targeting rules differ between them:

The anomaly reduction action is notably broader than terraforming: it can target colonized planets you own, which the terraforming action cannot. This makes it the only behemoth action that can improve an already-inhabited world's anomaly situation without waiting for OCP Investigation (Stage 5 standard tech). For severe detrimental anomalies on high-value colonized planets, a behemoth with Climate Engineering is a meaningfully faster route than the tech tree alone.

UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
💡 The Pre-Colonization Edge and the Reconstruction Pipeline STRATEGY ▾

The single most strategically distinctive thing a terraforming behemoth can do is terraform planets in neutral systems before colonizing them. Standard in-system terraforming requires colonization first — you must own the planet and have a population on it before a build action can be queued. A behemoth bypasses this entirely.

In practice: if you identify a neutral system with a Toxic planet sitting one step away from Steppes or Savannah (Primary Terraforming), you can send a behemoth there, complete the conversion, and then colonize a higher-tier planet rather than a Toxic one. The colonized planet's population capacity and FIDSI profile are immediately better. You've pre-paid an Industry cost (via the behemoth) to receive a better starting state for that colony.

The reconstruction pipeline is the advanced version of this: an Obliterator-destroyed planet in any system can be restored by a behemoth with Advanced Climate Engineering. Restored planets always come back as Lava. Since Lava→Desert is a valid Primary Terraforming step, a behemoth can chain reconstruction directly into terraforming in the same orbit — restore the planet to Lava, then immediately begin converting it toward a more habitable type. This is the primary counter-play against Obliterator warfare in systems you want to settle.

TIMING THE PRE-TERRAFORM
Pre-terraforming a neutral system is only strategically sound if no other major faction is likely to colonize it first. A behemoth parked at a neutral system is not fighting and not producing elsewhere. If a rival colonizes while your behemoth is mid-terraform, you've invested Industry into improving their planet. Scout the contested neighborhood before committing.

Pirates: From Threat to Asset

How pirates spawn, why they matter, and how to turn their presence into an advantage
☠️ Pirate Spawning: Minor Factions as the Source MECHANIC ▾

If the Pirates option is enabled at game setup, pirates spawn exclusively from minor faction systems. There is no other spawn source. This has a direct strategic implication: finding and controlling a minor faction's system controls the pirate spawning point for that galaxy node.

Pirates can travel between galaxy nodes — a pirate spawned from a minor faction in a distant arm can eventually reach your systems. However, they originate from the minor faction's specific location, which means keeping a ship at that system gives you persistent interception capability.

On Endless difficulty, pirate fleets field modernized ship designs — often better-equipped than early-game major faction ships. Treating them as a minor nuisance on higher difficulties is a mistake. A pirate fleet that catches your colony ship or lightly armed scout can end a colonization attempt outright.

UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
⚔️ Turning Pirates into XP Farms STRATEGY ▾

Because pirates respawn from minor faction systems on a predictable cycle, a fleet stationed at the spawning system fights them as they appear. This converts the pirate threat into a controlled hero and fleet XP source. The pattern is straightforward:

  • STEP 1Locate the minor faction system that is your region's pirate spawn point.
  • STEP 2Station a fleet there on Sleep or Guard stance.
  • STEP 3Each pirate spawn triggers a battle — your fleet fights and gains XP. Pirates sometimes retreat rather than fighting to the end.

For the Hissho, killing pirates also generates Kei (their unique resource), making this strategy especially potent. In the early game when Kei generation is low, a pirate farm near a minor faction can sustain the Kei economy while the empire is still small.

PIRATE RETREAT BEHAVIOR
Pirates will sometimes retreat from a losing battle rather than fighting to destruction. This means your fleet won't always finish a pirate engagement in one turn. The retreat behavior appears to be probabilistic — the exact programming logic isn't documented in available sources.

Minor Factions: Long-term Tributes and Assimilation

Three paths to a minor faction's system, and why passive tribute income compounds throughout the game
🤝 The Relation Bar: Influence Spending and Passive Tribute MECHANIC ▾

Each minor faction has a Diplomatic Relation bar that fills from 0 to 100 via Influence spending. The two active Influence actions are Praise (a direct relation point purchase, cost increases with each use) and Assist (triggers a quest — completing the quest grants relation points, often more efficiently than Praise).

Even before assimilation, a minor faction with a filled or partially filled relation bar generates passive tribute each turn — Manpower, Dust, Science, and/or Food depending on the minor faction's type. In the early game when your FIDSI numbers are small, a single well-developed minor faction relationship can contribute more Science per turn than your entire civilization's output. This is not an exaggeration: some Horatio playthroughs show a minor faction relationship accounting for 30%+ of total science output by the mid-game.

AT 50 RELATION: DEVELOPMENT GRANTS
At 50 relation points, some minor factions unlock a special action called Development Grants. This costs 15 of a randomly selected luxury resource (usually purchasable from the Marketplace) and provides a significant science boost. This is one of the best early-game science accelerators available and worth planning your Marketplace purchases around if you have a minor faction nearby.
UNDERSTOOD & APPLIED
🏛️ Three Assimilation Paths: Praise, Assist, and Conquest MECHANIC STRATEGY ▾

Once the relation bar reaches 100, the Assimilate button unlocks. Assimilation converts the minor faction's system into one of your own: the population stays, you gain full control of the system and can build there freely. The minor faction's population type brings its own FIDSI traits and political party affiliation into your empire — who you assimilate shapes your population composition over time.

PathMethodCost / Risk
PraiseSpend Influence repeatedly until relation = 100, then assimilateInfluence-heavy; Praise cost escalates with use
Assist QuestAccept their quest, complete it, collect relation pointsVariable — quests range from trivial to multi-turn military requirements
ConquestDeclare war, station a ship with Manpower, invadeCosts Manpower; no Influence required; hostile populations may integrate poorly

Not assimilating is also a valid choice. Maintaining a minor faction as a tribute source without absorbing them keeps your Overcolonization count lower (which matters for Approval), gives you continued passive FIDSI from the relationship, and avoids absorbing a population that might destabilize your political balance. The decision to assimilate should weigh the system's FIDSI value against the Approval cost of adding another colony.

If you don't assimilate, the minor faction's population will still naturally migrate into your systems over time as you develop your empire and their pops find your systems more attractive. You gain the population benefits without the assimilation overhead.

Turn 30 Assessment: Science Victory Condition

Four Endless technologies, six Tier 5 prerequisites each — and why the commitment window is earlier than it looks
🔬 The Win Condition: Four Endless Technologies MECHANIC ▾

The Science victory is won by researching all four Endless technologies. These are late-game techs that sit at the very top of the research tree and are not available from the start — each one unlocks only after you have researched three Tier 5 technologies in two specific tree quadrant combinations.

Endless TechnologyPrerequisite QuadrantsTier 5 Required (each)
Might of the EndlessMilitary + Empire3 in each
Wealth of the EndlessMilitary + Economy3 in each
Power of the EndlessScience + Empire3 in each
Genius of the EndlessScience + Economy3 in each

The implication is significant: a science victory demands broad tech tree investment, not deep specialization in the Science quadrant alone. You need Tier 5 Military technologies to unlock two of the four Endless techs. A player who fully neglects the Military tree cannot win by science. This is the built-in self-defense requirement — you must research enough military tech to unlock the prerequisites, which means you will also have access to meaningful military capability by the time you're in contention for a science win.

ALLIANCE SCALING — A HARD TRAP
In an alliance, the number of Endless technologies required increases — but each player must research their own set independently. You cannot research additional Endless techs on behalf of an ally. Once you've researched your four, you win only if your allies have independently completed their portions too. Unlike Wonder victory, where one player can build all the Obelisks themselves, a science victory within an alliance requires synchronized progress across all members. Entering alliances late specifically to push a science win rarely works — your allies may not be close to their own Endless techs.

🗺️ The Tech Path: What "Tier 5 in Two Quadrants" Actually Means STRATEGY ▾

Each tree quadrant has five stages. Stage 5 technologies are the most expensive and require progressive research through earlier stages. To unlock all four Endless techs you need three Stage 5 completions in each of Military, Empire, Science, and Economy. In practice you need at minimum 3 Stage 5 techs in every quadrant to satisfy all four unlock conditions simultaneously.

Stage unlock thresholds (techs required from previous stage to unlock the next) differ by tree:

Military is the steepest: you need 4 Stage 4 techs before Stage 5 opens. Economy's Stage 4 requires 4 Stage 3 techs. These are the bottlenecks that make the "broad game" requirement concrete — you cannot rush Stage 5 Military with a minimal investment.

Key questions at turn 30:

  • QUESTION 1What stage are you in for each quadrant? If you're still in Stage 2–3 for Military or Economy at turn 30, your Endless tech timeline is under pressure.
  • QUESTION 2Have you unlocked behemoths and the Stellar Node Study modules? A science behemoth orbiting a Black Hole or Neutron Star gives a −2% (Study 1) or −3% (Study 2) reduction on Science & Exploration tech costs, plus +80/+160 Science/turn — the persistent compound advantage that makes science victories competitive over time. If you don't have one orbiting yet, this is the most urgent accelerant available.
  • QUESTION 3Are you being pressured militarily? A science victory player who ignores their fleet until Stage 5 is near risks being conquered before the win condition is reachable. You need enough military presence to deter or repel aggression throughout the mid-game.

⚡ Accelerants: Unique Buildings and Behemoth Stacking STRATEGY ▾

Two once-per-galaxy unique buildings are major science victory accelerants: the Endless Research Park and the Intergalactic Technology Center. Both provide substantial science bonuses but can only be built once across the entire galaxy — whichever empire builds them first locks out all others. Racing to build these before a scientifically competitive opponent is a meaningful priority.

The science behemoth's tech cost reduction stacks across different node types: a behemoth at a Black Hole gives −2%/−3% to Science & Exploration, while a second at a Neutron Star gives another −2%/−3% to the same branch (different node, same branch). Combined with the flat Science output bonus per module (+80 or +160/turn per behemoth), the compound effect across many turns represents the largest per-investment FIDSI leverage available in the mid-to-late game. Community experience benchmarks science victories at roughly turn 50 on fast speed in medium galaxies on Endless difficulty for optimized play — but this requires behemoths deployed early, the unique buildings if accessible, and strong Science-quadrant development throughout.

The Sophons are the canonical science victory faction, with bonuses that make the Science quadrant faster and cheaper. Riftborn and Vaulters are noted as close alternatives. The Nakalim face structural disadvantages due to their scientific penalties and are poorly suited to this path.

SCIENCE VICTORY IS A BROAD GAME, NOT A NARROW ONE
The prerequisite structure forces you into every quadrant. A player going for a science victory who focuses only on the Science tree is building toward a wall. Deliberate mid-game investment in Military Tier 3–5 and Economy Tier 3–5 — even if you never plan to win militarily or economically — is not a detour. It is the actual path.
