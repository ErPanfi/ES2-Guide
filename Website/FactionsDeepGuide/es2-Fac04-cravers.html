ES2 // Fac.04 // The Cravers

🪳 The
Cravers

A locust swarm wearing the skin of a civilization. The Cravers cannot stop expanding — every planet they occupy degrades permanently, so they must always move forward. In the hands of a player who understands the depletion engine, this becomes a weapon: a war machine that fights harder the more it destroys, subsidized by colonies that are deliberately consumed and abandoned. The player who treats depletion as a crisis will lose. The player who treats it as the plan will dominate.

00

Faction Identity: The Consumption Machine

Every trait feeds the same loop: consume, deplete, conquer, repeat.
🪳 Core Traits, Government, and Starting Conditions OVERVIEW ▾
◈ CRAVERS — THE ENDLESS SWARM ◈

The Cravers are a unique faction defined by a forced economic clock: their planets deplete over time, permanently losing output until they become worthless. This is not a drawback to be mitigated — it is the design. The faction is built to sustain itself through constant conquest and to leverage the depleted planet count as a late-game military multiplier. Understanding this reframes everything: you are not building a stable empire, you are building a consumption machine that happens to have cities on it.

ETERNAL WAR — THE DEFINING CONSTRAINT
Cravers cannot sign truces, cold wars, or non-aggression pacts. They begin in a state of permanent war with every major faction in the game — except other Cravers. Diplomatic isolation is total. This means there is no "peaceful phase" at any point in a Craver game. You are always at war, always acquiring new systems to offset depletion, and always under threat. The flip side: you are never wasting political capital on diplomacy that cannot yield results. Every Influence and hero moment goes to internal development or military preparation.
AUTOCRACY — THREE LAW SLOTS, NO ELECTIONS
Cravers are locked into Autocracy permanently. This means: three law slots (more than Dictatorship's two), no election cycles, no Anarchy risk, and — critically — only Craver population units vote. Non-Craver pops have zero political influence. The Militarist party typically dominates, which is appropriate: most Craver-optimal laws are Militarist. Three law slots in a stable Autocracy is a genuine advantage that is often underappreciated. Plan your law combination to always fill all three slots productively.
BIG FLEETS II — THE STRUCTURAL MILITARY ADVANTAGE
Cravers receive Big Fleets II as a faction trait, giving +2 Command Points on all fleets and −25% ship upkeep. More Command Points at no tech cost means larger effective fleets earlier than any other faction. Combined with −25% upkeep, Cravers can sustain large fleets that would bankrupt other factions. This is not a bonus — it is the baseline you design around. Never field small fleets. Your fleets should always be at the Command Point cap.
APPROVAL LOCKED TO MILITARIST OUTPUT — THE CORE VULNERABILITY
Craver Approval is tied almost entirely to being at war and winning. Their approval sources are predominantly Militarist laws and buildings that require active conflict. In periods of relative inactivity (waiting for fleets to rebuild, navigating early exploration), Approval can crater. The approval crisis toolkit (Topic 02) addresses this directly — but the fundamental truth is: a Craver empire at peace is a Craver empire in trouble.
HIDDEN HULL MODIFIER — WHAT THE TOOLTIP DOESN'T SAY
Craver ships have a hidden modifier set: −10% base HP, −10% base hull production cost, and +10% Manpower capacity per ship. The −10% HP means individual ships are slightly squishier than tooltips suggest. The −10% build cost partially offsets upkeep (already reduced by Big Fleets II). The +10% Manpower means every ship carries more ground troops for invasion. Combined with Big Fleets II, the net effect is: build more ships than opponents, fill them with troops, and invade fast.
TRANSCRIPT02 (Pangolin)
LUXURY RESOURCES ON CRAVER POPS — THE HIDDEN EXCEPTION
Luxury resources normally double the FIDSI output of the population type they match. For Craver pops, this doubling does not apply — Craver pops receive only a flat +2 FIDSI from luxury resources regardless of type. This has direct strategic implications: applying luxury resources to your Craver pops is nearly worthless. Applying them to non-Craver (enslaved) pops in your systems, where the normal doubling does work, is dramatically more valuable.

⏱️ The Husk: Depletion Clock and Starting Conditions MECHANIC ▾
WHAT IS THE HUSK?
The Cravers start on a unique planet called the Husk — a pre-depleted home world. At game start, the Husk already has approximately 116.5 out of 150 depletion points. Depletion accrues at roughly +2 per turn base. This means the Husk reaches full depletion (and ceases providing useful output) in approximately 17 turns from turn 1 — before most other factions have settled their second colony. This is not a soft warning; it is a hard timer. If you are still relying on the Husk as a primary economic system at turn 20, you are behind.
HOW DEPLETION WORKS — THE MECHANIC
Every Craver-occupied planet accrues depletion points each turn. Depletion points reduce that planet's FIDSI output proportionally. At 150 depletion points (full depletion), the planet's output is reduced by approximately 50% of its base value (the depletion bonus partially compensates — see Topic 01). Non-Craver pops on a Craver-owned planet also contribute to depletion accumulation but at a lower rate than Craver pops. A planet with only non-Craver pops depletes more slowly.
ESCAPING THE HUSK — THE TURN 1 PRIORITY
Your first colony ship must leave on Turn 1 or Turn 2, headed for the best available system in your starting neighbourhood. The Husk dying is not a crisis if your second (and ideally third) system is already producing. Treat the Husk as a temporary production platform, not your economic core. By turn 10–15, your first proper colony should be the engine of your empire. The Husk can still provide Manpower (military staging) and some residual output well past full depletion — it is never truly useless, but its economic role ends fast.
DON'T PANIC ABOUT THE HUSK
New Craver players often spend early turns trying to prop up the Husk with buildings. This is a trap. Every Industry point spent improving the Husk is Industry not building your first real colony or your first fleet. Accept the Husk's fate, plan around it, and move on.

Depletion & Population Math

The +150% FIDSI bonus is active from turn 1 on fresh planets — and it degrades as you consume them. Understanding the depletion curve is the difference between thriving and collapsing.
📊 The Depletion Bonus: +150% FIDSI While Fresh, −50% When Depleted MECHANIC ▾
CRITICAL: THE BONUS IS ACTIVE FROM THE START, NOT AT THE END
The +150% FIDSI bonus applies to planets that are not yet depleted. A fresh Craver planet produces at ×2.5 its base output from turn 1. As depletion accumulates, this bonus degrades. When the planet reaches full depletion, the bonus is gone entirely — and output drops to approximately −50% of base, making a fully depleted planet roughly half as productive as it was when empty. The strategic direction is therefore the opposite of what you might assume: you extract maximum value before depletion, then abandon or minimally maintain the planet.
THE DEPLETION CURVE IN PRACTICE
A fresh planet outputting 40 Food base produces 100 Food at game start (×2.5 with the undepleted bonus). As your Craver pops consume it, the bonus degrades toward zero. At full depletion, that same planet produces roughly 20 Food (−50%). The slope is not a cliff — it is a long gradual decline — but the message is clear: every turn you spend on a planet past its productive peak is a turn you are bleeding output. The goal is to have your next colony already running before the current one's bonus has significantly degraded.

This also reframes the role of Slave Driver on depleted planets. As Panczasu describes: Slave Driver on depleted worlds makes them "not completely useless — kind of still rubbish but not as rubbish." It is a recovery tool, not a design state to aim for.
PLANET SIZE AND DEPLETION TIMING
Larger planets take longer to fully deplete, because they have more base capacity before the clock runs out. When choosing which planet in a multi-planet system to colonise first, the bigger planet buys more time to build infrastructure before you must move on. This is a non-obvious but consistent heuristic: if you have a forest and an arid in the same starting system and can only colonise one immediately, the larger planet gives you a longer productive window.

THE UI TIMER LIES
The in-game depletion timer assumes static population. As your pops grow, they add more depletion per turn — accelerating the real deadline beyond what the tooltip shows. Never treat the displayed depletion counter as exact. If your planet shows "8 turns to depletion" but your population is actively growing, the real window may be 5–6 turns. Build infrastructure and queue your colony ship accordingly.

⛓️ Slave Driver: The Non-Craver Pop Multiplier MECHANIC ▾
HOW SLAVE DRIVER WORKS
Any planet with at least one Craver population unit receives the Slave Driver modifier on all non-Craver pops in that system: their FIDSI output is multiplied by ×2. A single Craver pop is enough to activate it — additional Craver pops do not stack the bonus further. The Craver pop itself produces normal output (no multiplier, just base stats). The enslaved pops produce double. This creates an optimal configuration: exactly 1 Craver pop per planet, with as many non-Craver pops as possible filling the remaining slots.
THE APPROVAL COST OF SLAVERY
Each non-Craver pop enslaved under Slave Driver imposes a −10 Approval penalty per enslaved pop, empire-wide. A system with 1 Craver and 4 non-Craver pops costs −40 Approval to the entire empire. Multiply this across 5 systems and you have −200 Approval before any other penalties. This is the core tension of the Craver economy: Slave Driver is spectacularly productive, but its Approval cost grows directly with the number of slaves you maintain. Managing this without dropping below the Happy (70) or Ecstatic (85) thresholds is the central challenge of Craver empire management.
OPTIMAL POP CONFIGURATION PER SYSTEM
Per community consensus and 4Xalch analysis: the math favors 1 Craver + (N−1) non-Craver per system where N is the total planet pop capacity. One Craver activates Slave Driver for all others. Additional Craver pops beyond the first reduce the number of doubled-output slaves without adding any bonus. The exception is the Chain Gang Program (below): additional Craver pops serve as Manpower converters if you are running that strategy.
CHAIN GANG PROGRAM — CRAVER POPS AS MANPOWER FACTORIES
The Chain Gang Program is a building that converts 300 Food into 300 Manpower per turn per Craver pop in the system. Two Craver pops in a system with Chain Gang Programs can produce 600 Manpower per turn — a significant military resource. However, the Food drain is real: a system running 2 Craver pops + Chain Gang needs substantial Food surplus or it will starve. The community strategy guide notes this as the core mechanic of the Standard Militarist strategy: keep Craver pop count low for economic efficiency, use Chain Gang to generate Manpower without relying on conquest alone.

THE SLAVE MATH IN PRACTICE
A system with 1 Craver + 3 Unfallen pops (base +3 Food, +2 Industry each): the 3 Unfallen produce 2× output → +6 Food and +4 Industry each → +18 Food and +12 Industry from 3 pops. If you applied their preferred luxury, that doubles again to +36 Food and +24 Industry from 3 non-Craver pops, plus the 1 Craver producing normal stats. This is why the Standard Militarist strategy maintains a large non-Craver majority: the multiplicative stack with luxury resources is enormous.

Approval Crisis Toolkit

Approval is the limiting factor on every Craver strategy. Know every lever before you need it.
⚖️ Militarist Laws: The Primary Approval Engine MECHANIC ▾

Craver Approval sources are dominated by Militarist laws. These require active warfare and victories to function — which is fine, because Cravers are always at war. Under Autocracy, three law slots should be filled at all times. The following values are Autocracy-bracket values from the laws screen.

THE JINGOIST JOY BILL IN PRACTICE — INCLUDING MINOR FACTIONS
The values in the table (+25 major / +15 minor) are the Autocracy forced-law bracket values — these only apply when Militarists are the dominant party in an Autocracy. Base values are +15 per major war and +5 per minor war. For Cravers in Autocracy, you always get the enhanced values. In a game where you are simultaneously at war with 3 major factions and 2 minor factions: +75 (major) + +30 (minor) = +105 Approval from this single law. This is the largest single approval source available to Cravers and the reason you must maintain active wars at all times.

The implication: pirates and minor factions encountered during exploration are not nuisances — they are approval resources. Declare war on every minor faction you find, even if you have no immediate plans to conquer them. Each war declaration costs nothing under Eternal War rules, and each active minor war adds +15 Approval permanently while the war is live. Panczasu explicitly plans around having multiple minor faction wars active simultaneously as approval padding, independent of any conquest intent.

⛪ Religious Path and Emergency Approval Tools MECHANIC ▾
SAINTS & SINNERS — THE WAR-INDEPENDENT APPROVAL SOURCE
Saints & Sinners (Religious, Level 3) grants +20 Approval to all systems, plus an additional +20 Approval per Hero assigned to each system. This is the only significant Craver approval law that does not require active warfare. In the Religious strategy, placing heroes as system governors across your empire turns Saints & Sinners into an approval floor that can hold you at Ecstatic even during inter-war gaps. Note: this is a flat bonus, not a "content lock" — it does not mechanically prevent Approval from dropping, it simply adds a large constant to the total.
SPECIES STABILITY ACT — THE RELIGIOUS L1 CORNERSTONE
The Religious Level 1 law Species Stability Act provides +5 Dust per Original Empire Population pop and limits new pop growth to only the Original Empire Population type. For Cravers in a Religious strategy this is significant: it slows (or stops) unwanted non-Craver population growth while providing passive Dust. It also interacts with Us or Them Decree differently than in a pure Militarist game — the Dust generation can fund additional hero hirings, which compound Saints & Sinners.
FEEDING PITS — TWO DISTINCT USES
As an emergency approval tool (mid/late game): The Feeding Pits system improvement provides +25 Approval for 10 turns and removes the −10 Approval penalty from enslaved pops in that system for the duration. In an approval crisis, queuing it across your systems buys 10 turns of headroom while you rebuild the wartime approval base.

As a turn-1 opener (situational, high-risk): Feeding Pits can be used on turn 1 to immediately consume your starting non-Craver pop, converting them into Food and Approval for 10 turns. This sounds counter-productive, but Panczasu documents the case for it: because Cravers are extremely efficient on undepleted planets, the population recovers faster than you'd expect — and the food/approval pulse funds an aggressive early war. The condition is strict: only do this if you are certain you can completely conquer your nearest neighbour within ~15 turns (fast game speed). If that window is not available from your scouting, play the standard opening.
LP_PANCZASU
CATHEDRAL OF THE SWARM — THE LATE-GAME APPROVAL ANCHOR
The Cathedral of the Swarm is a unique Craver system improvement (high tier) that provides substantial permanent Approval bonuses and has secondary effects that compound with depleted planet count. It is the structural endgame approval solution for the Religious strategy and a useful supplement for Militarist paths. Build it in your highest-population system first for maximum effect.
APPROVAL BUDGET REALITY CHECK
A mid-game Craver empire with 6 systems, 3 Craver + 9 slaves per system = −90 Approval from slavery alone. Add overcolonization: −30. Total structural penalty: roughly −120. You need at least +190 from all positive sources combined just to reach Happy (70). Jingoist Joy Bill in 3 major wars: +75. Us or Them with 3 captured Home Systems: +60. Spoils: +occasional. That's +135 from two laws — still short. You need Saints & Sinners, or heroes on systems, or Feeding Pits to bridge the gap. Budget this before you expand, not after Approval collapses.

The Three Strategies

Community consensus identifies three distinct Craver archetypes, each built around a different approval solution and population philosophy.
🗂️ Standard Militarist — The Optimised Path STRATEGY ▾
PHILOSOPHY
Keep Craver population count low (1 Craver per system for Slave Driver only), use Chain Gang Program to generate Manpower without scaling Craver pops, maintain large slave populations for doubled FIDSI output, and drive approval through Militarist laws with constant active wars. Victory target: Supremacy.
POPULATION TARGET
1 Craver + maximum non-Craver per system. This minimises depletion rate (fewer Craver pops = slower depletion), maximises Slave Driver output, and keeps the per-pop approval penalty manageable. The Craver pop exists solely to activate Slave Driver. Use Chain Gang Program buildings to convert Food into Manpower rather than adding more Craver pops.
LAW COMBINATION
Jingoist Joy Bill + Us or Them Decree + Spoils of War Act. All three are Militarist, all three scale with active warfare. In a 3-major-war game, this combination produces approximately +140–160 Approval from laws alone, which is sufficient to offset a moderate slave population and overcolonization.
WEAKNESS
Requires constant active wars. In an isolated start (few neighbours), or if all nearby enemies are eliminated too fast, the approval base collapses. Also requires micromanagement of Chain Gang Food drain vs. Manpower output. The most optimal strategy, but the most demanding.

💣 All-In Militarist — Fast Depletion, Fast Victory STRATEGY ▾
PHILOSOPHY
Lean into Craver pops heavily — fill every system with Craver population to maximise depletion speed, reach the +150% bonus faster, and generate massive Manpower for constant invasions. Use Feeding Pits as the approval emergency tool when the Slave Driver penalty accumulates. Victory target: Supremacy via overwhelming military force.
POPULATION TARGET
Maximum Craver pops, accepting that non-Craver pop count (and thus Slave Driver benefit) will be lower. The tradeoff: faster depletion progression toward the +150% bonus, and higher Manpower generation across all ships (from the hidden +10% Manpower trait). This strategy effectively accepts lower FIDSI in exchange for faster military output.
APPROVAL MANAGEMENT
Jingoist Joy Bill remains essential. Feeding Pits are the emergency buffer. With high Craver pop counts, the −10 Approval per slave penalty is lower (fewer slaves), so the absolute penalty is smaller — but the positive approval sources must still be active. This strategy is more tolerant of brief war gaps because the slave penalty is lower.
WEAKNESS
Sacrifices the Slave Driver output advantage. Lower overall FIDSI means slower tech and empire development. Only viable as a blitz strategy: win before the economic disadvantage compounds. If the game goes long, Standard Militarist or Religious outperforms this path.

⛪ Religious Cravers — The War-Independent Path STRATEGY ▾
PHILOSOPHY
Replace the warfare-dependent Militarist approval core with Saints & Sinners + hero governor placement, allowing approval to remain high even between wars. Use the three law slots for a hybrid combination: one Militarist (Jingoist or Us or Them), plus the Religious tier laws (Species Stability Act, Saints & Sinners). Victory target: flexible — Supremacy or Conquest depending on map.
LAW COMBINATION
Species Stability Act (Religious L1) + Saints & Sinners (Religious L3) + Us or Them Decree (Militarist). Saints & Sinners provides +20 flat to all systems, plus +20 per assigned hero. A Craver empire with 5 systems and 1 hero per system gains +200 Approval from this law alone. Combined with Us or Them: a highly manageable approval budget even with significant slave counts.
HERO MANAGEMENT REQUIREMENT
This strategy requires hero acquisition and deployment as system governors across your empire. Every hero slot filled on a system adds +20 Approval to that system empire-wide. Hiring from the Galactic Academy regularly is therefore a mandatory economic action, not a luxury. Budget Dust accordingly.
WEAKNESS
Reaching Religious L3 laws requires influence investment and time. In the early game before Saints & Sinners is available, you are still approval-vulnerable. And the Religious path costs one Militarist law slot — losing Jingoist or Spoils of War reduces your wartime Approval ceiling. This path is strictly better than Militarist only once Saints & Sinners is online and heroes are deployed.

Early Game Playbook (Turns 1–30)

The Husk is dying. Your neighbours are enemies. Use both facts.
🗓️ Turn-by-Turn Opening Framework STRATEGY ▾
TURN 1
Colony ship out, hero to Revealer fleet. The colony ship must leave Turn 1. No exceptions. Scout for your nearest habitable system simultaneously — two Revealers covering different directions doubles your information. Queue Jingoist Joy Bill immediately; you are already at war with everyone (Eternal War trait), so it pays immediately.

First research — Xenobiology: Panczasu's consistent opening regardless of starting planet type. The reason is not colonisation unlocks — it is that Xenobiology unlocks Public-Private Partnerships, a key early economic building that significantly boosts Industry output. Cravers are not the brute-force barbarians they appear; early Industry investment is what lets you actually build the ships and improvements your strategy demands. Research military tech second, not first.
TURNS 1–10
Scout hard, find pirates, find minor factions — and declare war on all of them. Craver scouts should be prioritizing: pirate locations (for Manpower), minor faction locations (for both future slave pop acquisition and immediate Jingoist Joy Bill approval), and nearest major faction home systems (for future Us or Them value). Every minor faction you find is a free +15 Approval once you declare war — do this immediately on discovery, even if you have no intention of conquering them for many turns. The Husk is still producing; use this window to build your first military ships and queue your first colony's infrastructure.

Faction quest choice: When the faction questline presents the early branch decision, the community consensus is to take the Destroy path for the Cyborg Stomachs unique improvement reward, which provides permanent Food bonuses that help sustain the Chain Gang Program. Specific values should be confirmed from the in-game quest screen.
TURNS 8–15
First colony must be fully operational. This is your economic lifeline after the Husk degrades. Prioritise improvements that match the colony's planet type and that feed your first Chain Gang Program. Begin acquiring non-Craver pops via minor faction assimilation — you need slaves to make Slave Driver worthwhile. If the first minor faction you reach has a decent pop type, assimilate them.

First invasion planning: Identify your first conquest target. It should be: a minor faction or weak isolated major faction system within 2–3 turns of travel. The Termite corvette (if available) deals approximately 30 Manpower damage per turn at siege, making it the preferred siege weapon for early invasions. Send a fleet of Termites to the target system, reduce system Manpower to zero, then invade.
TURNS 15–25
First conquest completed or in progress. By turn 20, you should have taken at least one system from a neighbour. This activates Us or Them Decree value (if the system was a Home System), provides non-Craver pops for Slave Driver, and — if the conquered faction was a major faction — gives Approval bonuses from active war. Do not make peace. Ever. Eternal War means this is permanent anyway, but do not prioritise negotiation over expansion.

Luxury resources: Apply luxury resources to your non-Craver pop systems. Do not apply them to Craver pops (flat +2 FIDSI — worthless). For enslaved populations, the normal doubling applies on top of Slave Driver, creating a 4× multiplier on the base value for correctly luxuriated slaves.
TURNS 25–35
Second conquest and fleet expansion. With Big Fleets II providing extra Command Points, your fleet should always be at the CP cap. Queue ships continuously. Identify your second conquest target — ideally a Home System from a major faction, which advances Us or Them Decree. Begin assessing your approval budget: if slavery penalties are accumulating, now is the time to invest in Feeding Pits or reach for Saints & Sinners on the Religious path.

🔬 Tech Priorities and the Population Transfer Unlock STRATEGY ▾
RESEARCH OPENING — XENOBIOLOGY FIRST
Panczasu's consistent opening in his tutorial series, stated explicitly as near-universal: Xenobiology as the first research, regardless of starting planet type. The primary reason is not colonisation unlocks — it is access to Public-Private Partnerships, an early economic building that significantly boosts Industry. Don't be fooled by Cravers' reputation as a purely military faction: Industry output in the first 15 turns determines how fast you build your first fleet, your first Chain Gang, and your first colony's infrastructure. Researching military tech on turn 1 leaves you slower than necessary on all of these.
ERA 2 PRIORITY — BASIC SYSTEM DEVELOPMENT
Once past the opening, Basic System Development is a high-priority research in Era 2. It unlocks Space Boards, which allow population transfer between your systems. This is critical for Craver plague strategy: as planets deplete, you need to redistribute populations — moving slave pops to fresh systems where they can be productive under Slave Driver, and pulling Craver pops to systems with Chain Gang Programs. Without population transfer, you are locked into whatever pop distribution happened naturally, which is almost never optimal. Panczasu frames this as essential infrastructure, not optional.
LP_PANCZASU
ATMOSPHERIC FILTRATION — THE EXPANSION ENABLER
Ash planets appear frequently in Craver starts and mid-game expansion zones, and are productive (high Industry output). Atmospheric Filtration is the tech that allows Ash planet colonisation. Panczasu names it as an almost-always-wanted Era 2 research. If your scouting reveals several Ash systems in your expansion corridor, prioritise this early to open those colonisation options.
AVOID REDUNDANT TECHNOLOGY — THE CUMULATIVE COST WARNING
Every research you complete increases the cost of all subsequent research in that era by a small amount. Panczasu explicitly notes the temptation to grab colonisation techs for planet types you may not use (Mediterranean, Gas Giant, Barren) — and warns against it. Colonising a type you won't need for many turns delays your real priorities for a compounding cost. Research only planet-type techs when you have confirmed targets of that type in your near-term expansion zone.

📜 Faction Quest: Branch Choices and Final Rewards STRATEGY ▾
CHAPTER 1 — DESTROY (INDUSTRIALIST) vs. CAPTURE (RELIGIOUS)
The first branch choice determines your entire questline path and party alignment.

DESTROY (Industrialist): Reward is 50 Titanium/Hyperium, then Chapter 2 leads toward the Infinity Shield defense module. Locks into the Industrialist questline.

CAPTURE (Religious): Reward is a free Tier 2 Cruiser (keep it — it outclasses anything you can build early) plus the Hive Hall improvement (no upkeep for heroes assigned to that system). The wiki's own route suggestion favors this: the free T2 Cruiser is immediately deployable for bullying neighbors and assimilating minor factions, saving you the Industry cost of building a combat ship yourself. This branch locks into the Religious questline and is the path to acquiring Reyaryn Cious, the unique Virtual Endless hero.
CHAPTER 2 BRANCH — INDUSTRIALIST PATH
CONQUER (Industrialist): Build Signal Seeker (+50 Industry, +10 Vision) in a Minor Civ home system. Reward: Infinity Shield defense module (HP +10%, Shield +270, Energy Defense +31 — strong early combat tech).

STUDY (Religious): Use 50 Titanium + 50 Hyperium to explore a curiosity. Reward: Reyaryn Cious (Hero, level 8 on arrival, Religious/Guardian class, Virtual Endless faction). See below for his skill tree.
CHAPTER 2 BRANCH — RELIGIOUS PATH
INVESTIGATE (Industrialist): Obtain 2 marked systems. Reward: Infinity Shield (same stats as above — this module is available via both major branches).

Ships & Fleet Doctrine

More ships, more manpower per ship, cheaper to run. The design philosophy follows from Big Fleets II.
🚀 Hull Analysis and Doctrine MECHANIC ▾
THE HIDDEN HULL MODIFIER — DESIGN IMPLICATIONS
All Craver hulls carry: −10% HP, −10% build cost, +10% Manpower capacity. The −10% HP means individual ships trade slightly below their apparent stat line. The −10% build cost, combined with Big Fleets II's −25% upkeep, means Craver fleets are cheaper both to build and maintain than any comparable faction. The +10% Manpower per ship means a 6-ship Craver fleet carries ~10% more invasion troops than the equivalent fleet of any other faction. Stack this with fleet CP upgrades and the Manpower laws (Jingoist, Chain Gang) and Craver ground invasion capacity becomes exceptional.
FORAGER / SCAVENGER
ATTACKER (SMALL)
Craver's baseline attacker. With the −10% build cost modifier, these are the cheapest-to-spam ships in the game. Use them for early fleet padding — fill Command Points with Foragers to reach the CP cap cheaply. Do not over-invest in module upgrades on these hulls; they are expendable. Their value is volume. Upgraded Scavenger: gains slots and becomes a more capable weapons platform for mid-game, but still best used in numbers rather than as individual powerhouses.
SODRA / SLICER
HUNTER (MEDIUM)
Craver's primary mid-range combat ship. Per Pangolin's analysis, the base Sodra has a strong slot configuration for its class. The hidden −10% HP applies here, so avoid committing Hunters into unsupported engagements — use them as part of a full fleet, not as solo raiders. Best weapon loadout: beam weapons for breaking through enemy shields at range (effective against 90% of fleet HP across all flotillas, vs. kinetics at ~25%).
MARAUDER
COORDINATOR (MEDIUM)
The Craver coordinator carries invasion modules and troop capacity. The +10% Manpower trait makes these ships exceptionally good invasion platforms. Standard doctrine: load with Boarding Pods or Invasion Modules, bring 3–4 to any system assault. With the Termite siege strategy (below), Marauders follow after Manpower drain and execute the invasion without needing a long ground war. Note from ESAcademy: scrapping older Marauders when upgrading to a new hull tier is recommended — each Marauder scrapped returns significant resources and avoids upkeep on ships below the current tech level.
TERMITE
CORVETTE / SIEGE
The Craver's unique siege asset. The Termite deals approximately 30 Manpower damage per turn at siege — no other corvette-class ship matches this. Standard siege doctrine: send 3–4 Termites to a target system, orbit for 3–5 turns to drain Manpower to near zero, then bring in the main fleet and Marauder coordinators for the actual invasion. The Termite is not a combat ship — do not use it in pitched battles. Its value is entirely in the pre-invasion Manpower drain role.
WEAPON SELECTION — BEAM VS KINETICS
Per Pangolin's fleet analysis: beam weapons deal damage to 90% of the total fleet HP spread across all flotillas, while kinetic weapons deal damage to approximately 25% of HP in the targeted flotilla only. This means beams are dramatically more efficient against full fleets. Cravers should default to beam loadouts for their primary combat ships. Kinetics (railguns, mass drivers) are only preferred when specifically targeting shielded protector flotillas that beam weapons cannot penetrate.
TRANSCRIPT02 (Pangolin)
BIG FLEETS II IN FLEET DESIGN
The +2 Command Points from Big Fleets II are permanent and apply to every fleet without any tech or upgrade investment. This means every Craver fleet should be built to its CP cap — any unused CP is lost value. When designing fleet compositions, fill the last 2 CP slots with cheap Forager/Scavenger units rather than leaving them empty. Volume is free for Cravers relative to other factions.

🪖 Ground Invasion: Tanks Only, Skip Infantry Upgrades STRATEGY ▾
THE GROUND COMBAT PRIORITY — TANKS
Craver ground invasions rely on Manpower, which is plentiful thanks to Chain Gang Programs and the +10% Manpower hull trait. The unit composition in ground battles is: tanks are the primary combat unit; infantry are inefficient and should not be upgraded. Panczasu's reasoning: every resource invested in upgrading infantry is wasted because the AI battle resolution converts tanks and air units between types as losses accumulate — upgrading infantry just makes you fill those secondary conversion slots faster rather than keeping your effective tank count high. Replace infantry with tanks as quickly as available, and invest upgrade resources exclusively into tanks.
LP_PANCZASU
SIEGE DOCTRINE — DRAIN MANPOWER BEFORE INVADING
The standard Craver siege sequence: send dedicated siege ships (Termite corvettes if available, or any ship with siege modules) to orbit the target system. Each turn of siege drains the defender's Manpower. Once the system Manpower is near zero, bring in the invasion fleet with Coordinators loaded with ground troops. The Craver +10% Manpower per ship means your invasion fleets carry more troops than equivalent fleets of other factions — but this advantage is wasted if you invade before draining defender Manpower. Patience before the invasion pays off in lower troop losses.
HEROSHIP AS A DESIGNATED DAMAGE SPONGE
A deliberate late-game fleet tactic: equip the hero's ship with the module that increases its chance of being targeted, plus heavy self-repair or regeneration modules. The enemy — AI or human — will concentrate fire on it, burning their DPS budget while your actual combat ships work through their fleet uncontested. Panczasu explicitly calls this "throwing the AI" and frames it as an intentional design decision, not a side effect. For this to work, the hero's ship needs enough HP and repair to survive the focused fire for several rounds. A hero with strong personal defensive skills compounds the durability further.
LP_PANCZASU_P2
PREEMPTIVE BOMBING — READ THE DEFENDER FIRST
Before committing to a full invasion, TecToss on Impossible difficulty recommends using preemptive bombing to probe the defender's response. Watching how they react (do they conscript? do they scramble a fleet?) tells you whether your advantage is sufficient to proceed immediately or whether you need another turn of siege. If your military power greatly exceeds theirs, skip the probe and invade directly — the extra turn costs you more than it saves.

Endgame: Empire of Ash

The depleted empire loop — when the ash itself becomes a weapon.
💀 The Depleted Planet Military Multiplier STRATEGY ▾
DEADLY INTENT BILL — THE ENDGAME LAW
Deadly Intent Bill (Militarist L4) provides +33% Damage on Weapon Modules and +33% Troops Damage — a flat combat multiplier that applies to your entire military simultaneously. This is the most powerful combat law available to any faction and requires Militarists to reach Potent (Level 4) experience. At +33% to both ship weapons and ground troops, every fleet engagement and every invasion resolves faster and at lower cost.

CORRECTION: THE "+1% PER DEPLETED PLANET" MECHANIC DOES NOT EXIST IN THE BASE GAME
Multiple sources including LP transcripts described Deadly Intent as "+1% damage per depleted planet owned" — a scaling mechanic that would make the law more powerful the more planets you deplete. The wiki laws database has no such mechanic. The law grants a flat +33%. This may have originated from a mod (ESG), an outdated version, or a misread. The flat +33% is already excellent; the law does not need per-planet scaling to be worth taking.
WIKI_LAWS
THE DEPLETED EMPIRE LOOP — WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENS
The mature Craver endgame works differently than the fresh-planet extraction phase. Conquered systems begin fresh (+150% FIDSI bonus active), are worked hard by slaves under Slave Driver, and deplete over time. Once fully depleted, they drop to ~−50% base output — but Slave Driver on depleted worlds partially recovers this, and Chain Gang buildings convert whatever Food they still produce into Manpower. The rear of your empire becomes a Manpower factory rather than an economic engine. Your economic core should always be your newest, freshest conquests at the frontier. Your depleted rear systems contribute via Deadly Intent (depleted planet count) and Chain Gang Manpower. These are distinct roles — plan your building queues accordingly.
POPULATION REDISTRIBUTION — THE LATE-GAME ECONOMIC CYCLE
As older systems approach or reach full depletion, they accumulate surplus pops that are no longer needed locally — their diminished output doesn't justify the population count. The correct response is to ship them out: transfer both surplus Craver pops and slave pops to newly conquered fresh systems where the +150% undepleted bonus makes every worker highly productive. Panczasu describes this as "endless creative immigration" and treats it as a core late-game habit: depleted rear systems continuously export their excess workers to the frontier.

The logistics require Space Boards (from Basic System Development) to be built in the source system. When configuring transfers, always include at least one Craver pop in the shipment to any fresh system — so Slave Driver activates immediately on arrival rather than waiting for natural migration. Shipping a mix of one Craver and several slave pops to a fresh system gives you the full multiplicative stack from turn one of occupation.
LP_PANCZASU_P2
CATHEDRAL OF THE SWARM — BUILD IT ON A FRESH SYSTEM
The Cathedral of the Swarm is the Cravers' unique late-game system improvement: a permanent approval anchor with additional bonuses. Counter-intuitively, do not build it in your most productive (currently highest-output) system — that system is likely near depletion and will halve in output before long. Instead, build it in a system that is fresh or recently colonised and expected to remain viable for the rest of the game. You want your permanent approval anchor tied to a system that will still be functional in 20 turns, not one that becomes an ash heap next week.
LP_PANCZASU_P2
ACADEMY ENDGAME QUEST — HEROES CANNOT BE REPLACED DURING IT
At some point in the late game, the cooperative/competitive Academy quest triggers (all factions simultaneously must choose sides: support the Academy or oppose it). Cravers naturally align against the Academy. During this quest, the Academy is closed: no new heroes can be hired or appear until it resolves. Panczasu's warning is direct — do not throw heroes into fights you might lose during this period. A dead hero is gone permanently until the quest ends, and losing a key Admiral mid-endgame significantly degrades your fleet's effectiveness. Play conservatively with heroes from the moment the quest triggers.
LP_PANCZASU_P2
AUTOMATION POLICIES — DO NOT USE THEM
Automation policies allow the AI to queue improvements in your systems automatically. For most factions this is a convenience; for Cravers it is a liability. Craver system management requires precise per-system decisions: which systems get Chain Gang Programs, which get science converters, which get Feeding Pits. Automation cannot reason about depletion timelines or pop configuration and will queue generic improvements in systems where they actively waste resources. Panczasu catches automation queuing a high-cost improvement in a nearly-depleted system — wasted Industry on a world about to become an ash heap. Keep automation off and manage queues manually.
LP_PANCZASU_P2
TIMING: US OR THEM → DEADLY INTENT SWAP
There is a law slot decision point in the late game. Us or Them Decree provides +20 Approval per captured Home System — valuable in the mid-game when you are still building that count. Deadly Intent Bill provides a flat +33% damage to all weapons and troops — the most powerful combat multiplier in the game, but it does nothing for Approval. The swap question is entirely about your Approval budget: if your other sources (Jingoist, Saints & Sinners, heroes, Cathedral) can hold you at Happy without Us or Them, then swapping in Deadly Intent is correct as soon as you unlock it. If dropping Us or Them would risk falling below Happy, wait until your Approval foundation is solid.

Note: Deadly Intent requires Militarists to have reached Level 4 (Potent) experience, which typically occurs in the mid-to-late game. It is not available from turn 1.
THE INSATIABLE MAW QUEST REWARD
The Cravers' faction questline culminates in the Insatiable Maw — a unique, extremely powerful late-game unit or ability (specific details vary by quest branch). Prioritising questline completion provides this reward in time for the endgame military push. Do not neglect questline conditions during your expansion phase: check which conditions are active and hit them through normal play where possible.

VICTORY PATHS
Supremacy / Conquest (primary): The natural Craver endgame. The military multiplier from Deadly Intent, Big Fleets II, and the +10% Manpower hull trait makes both viable. Conquest (majority of systems) is faster and less demanding than full Supremacy (eliminate all opponents). Either works; choose based on map density and remaining opponents.

Science Victory (legitimately viable — possibly easiest): Panczasu, who runs this faction at tutorial depth across multiple sessions, states directly that Science Victory is his pick for the easiest Craver win condition. The +150% FIDSI bonus on fresh planets applies to all resources including Science — meaning a Craver empire that is constantly expanding onto fresh planets can sustain exceptional Science output. If you are uncomfortable with the full military endgame, or the map has a Sophon neighbour making Supremacy dangerous, the Science path is not a fallback — it is a genuine primary option.

Other victories: Economic and Diplomatic victories require infrastructure and relationships that Eternal War makes difficult to build. Score Victory is reachable if the game goes very long. Neither is a recommended target, though both are achievable by an experienced player who builds around them from turn 1.
