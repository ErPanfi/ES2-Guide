ES2 // Fac.12 // The Vaulters

The
Vaulters

Space Vikings who escaped a dying world on a single ship — and built an empire from scattered outposts connected by teleportation gates. The Vaulters are the archetypal tall faction: fewer systems than their neighbors, but each one is exceptional. Their instant colonization, Golden Age FIDSI surges, and portal network create a playstyle that is fundamentally different from every other faction in ES2. If you want to understand what "going tall" actually means, mechanically and strategically, this is the faction that teaches it.

00

Faction Identity: The Tall Empire

Fewer systems than anyone else. Each one hand-picked. All of them one turn away from each other.
🌀 Core Traits and the Vaulter Identity OVERVIEW ▾
◈ VAULTERS — THE NOMADIC SCIENTISTS ◈

Most factions in ES2 race to colonize as many systems as possible in the first 30 turns. The Vaulters cannot do this and should not try. They have one colonization ship — the Argosy — which cannot be built, bought, or replaced by another. What the Argosy does instead is colonize instantly, without outposts, and then return to service within 2–3 turns, triggering a Golden Age FIDSI surge on every new system. The result is fewer colonies than competitors, but each colony arrives already producing at full capacity, skips the food-drain period entirely, and is connected to every other system in the empire via teleportation gates. The Vaulters do not build a contiguous border — they cherry-pick the best systems scattered across the galaxy and link them into a unified network.

INVOLUNTARY NOMADS — THE STARTING CONDITION
The Vaulters do not start on a home system. They begin on the Lodestone of the Nine — a special Rejuvenation Field anomaly that has no habitable planets. From turn 1, you control the Argosy and must fly it to a system to found your first colony. The Rejuvenation Field provides modest Dust income and powerful ship health regeneration to any fleet that passes through it. If it falls within your influence sphere, it is a genuine tactical asset — but it is not your capital.
METAFOLDING — THE DEFINING AFFINITY
Vaulters can build Portals: system improvements that allow any fleet (military or civilian pop-transport ships) to teleport instantly between any two portal-equipped systems, regardless of distance, consuming all movement points. Allies may also use your portals. This affinity defines the entire late-game: your empire has no "shape" problem — every system is equally reachable from every other system on any given turn.
MATERIAL EXPERTISE — USING STRATEGICS AS LUXURIES
Vaulters can spend Strategic Resources (Titanium, Hyperium, Adamantian, Antimatter, Orichalcix, Quadrinix) as System Development upgrades, alongside or instead of Luxury Resources. This decouples their economic progression from Luxury RNG — a significant advantage in low-luxury starts.
CORSAIRS — PRIVATEERS FROM TURN 1
Most factions access the Privateers action only after researching a mid-to-late-game technology. Vaulters have it from the start, and can use their own faction ships in privateer mode (not just marketplace ships). Their unique fleet action, the Non-Aggression Pact, can buy temporary Cordial status with the Pirate League for a single Influence payment.
BLACK THUMB II — THE FOOD PENALTY
Vaulters produce −15% Food on all systems. In practice this is rarely crippling: the Golden Age bonus on new colonies covers the shortfall during the vulnerable early period, and by the time Golden Age wanes, a single Drone Network or food-generating system improvement compensates. It does mean that food-starved opening positions hurt more than average, and planet type selection at turn 1 matters.
CORE WORLDS — APPROVAL SAFETY NET
While the empire is below its Overcolonization threshold, Vaulter systems receive +10 Approval. This is an active incentive to stay tall — the moment you expand past the threshold, you lose this bonus while gaining the overcolonization penalty on top of it. The game rewards restraint with a direct FIDSI multiplier bonus.
VAULTERS IN ONE SENTENCE
Find the best systems in the galaxy, colonize them instantly, connect them with teleportation gates, and maintain a smaller empire at higher quality than every neighbor can match at any given turn.

01

The Argosy System

One ship. No replacements. Understands the cooldown or the whole faction falls apart.
🚀 Instant Colonization & the Two-Timer System MECHANIC ▾

Other factions consume a colony ship to create an outpost, then feed that outpost food for 8–15 turns while it matures. Vaulters do neither. When the Argosy colonizes a system, the target planet becomes a full colony immediately — no outpost stage. No food drain. The Argosy then docks in the new system and becomes available for redeployment in 2–3 turns.

THE TWO TIMERS — HARD CEILING VS. SOFT CEILING
After the Argosy becomes available (2–3 turns), two separate countdowns are visible:
  • FREE COLONIZATIONAfter approximately 10 turns, the next colonization costs nothing. This is the hard ceiling on colonization speed — you cannot push faster than roughly one free colony per 10 turns.
  • BUYOUT COLONIZATIONBefore those 10 turns expire, you can colonize early by paying a cost in Dust + Titanium + Hyperium. This cost starts extremely high immediately after colonization and decreases by roughly 10% each turn until it reaches zero. So after 5 turns, the cost is approximately half the initial value; after 10, it's zero.
The practical implication: if you find an exceptional system, you can pay to colonize it early rather than waiting. If you have Titanium and Hyperium stockpiled and a high Dust income, treat the cooldown as flexible rather than fixed.
VERSION NOTE — ARGOSY READY TIME
The wiki states the Argosy becomes usable again in 2–3 turns after colonization. An older video guide (4X Alchemist, pre-patch) states 4 turns. The wiki figure is authoritative for current versions — but confirm in-game if you observe a different number, as patches may have adjusted this.
THE ARGOSY IS IRREPLACEABLE
There is exactly one Argosy. If it is destroyed before you have founded even one colony, the game ends — no one can repair it. After you have a colony, a destroyed Argosy can be repaired from that system's shipyard. Treat it as a precious strategic asset: never let it wander into a combat zone without an escort, and never park it in a system under active blockade.

✨ Golden Age: The FIDSI Surge on Every Colonization MECHANIC ▾

Every time the Argosy founds a new colony, that colony enters a Golden Age — a temporary burst of FIDSI bonuses that lasts for a variable number of turns. The exact bonus breakdown, sourced from the wiki:

BonusScope
+25 FIDSOn the new system
+25 ManpowerEmpire-wide
+15 ApprovalOn the new system
+10 ApprovalEmpire-wide
+5 InfluenceOn the new system
−50% Influence Conversion RateOn the new system (minor drawback)

The +25 to all FIDS on a new system is enormous in the early game — 4X Alchemist observed Science output of 3,750 and Industry of 75 on turn 1 of the first colony with the Golden Age active. This allows the Vaulters to immediately start building system improvements while other factions are still building their first Drone Network.

GOLDEN AGE DURATION — STACKING BY WAITING
The Golden Age duration for the next colony increases by approximately 2–3 turns for every turn the Argosy is ready but unused. There is reportedly a cap around 15 turns maximum duration. This means deliberately delaying colonization (to scout a better system, for example) is not purely a loss — you are banking Golden Age turns for the eventual colony. Note: exact accrual rate and cap per the 4X Alchemist playthrough guide; not confirmed in wiki. Treat as likely-accurate community knowledge.
STRATEGIC IMPLICATION OF GOLDEN AGE
Other factions lose output when they colonize — their core systems drain food to feed the new outpost. Vaulters gain output when they colonize. This is why the advice "expand whenever possible" applies to Vaulters when it explicitly doesn't apply to most other factions. Every colonization pays for itself immediately via the Golden Age and never taxes the existing systems.

Turn 1: System Selection & Early Scouting

The decision that shapes the entire game. You can choose — so choose well.
🌍 Reading the Starting Map and Picking Your Capital STRATEGY ▾

At game start, the Argosy shows you every system reachable from the Rejuvenation Field. The wiki confirms there is always a guaranteed 4-planet system nearby. Sometimes you can see a 5-planet option; sometimes you need to spend a turn or two scouting for one. The Golden Age bank compensates for the delay — waiting 2 turns to find a 5-planet system instead of a 4-planet one is usually worth it.

PLANET TYPE PRIORITY AT TURN 1
  • INDUSTRY-BEARINGJungle and Arid planets provide strong Industry. The Golden Age covers your early Food gap — what you actually need on turn 1 is the Industry to build improvements immediately while the Golden Age is active. A Jungle planet with Titanium is an exceptional opening.
  • STRATEGIC RESOURCES PRESENTTitanium and Hyperium in your home system are multiplicatively valuable: they power your colonization buyouts AND your Material Expertise system upgrades. Starting with both on your home system is close to an ideal roll. Do not sacrifice system quality for strategic resources, but weight them heavily in otherwise-equal choices.
  • PLANET COUNT5-planet systems are worth scouting for. The Vaulters' tall strategy means each system needs to do more total work. A 5-planet system with one fertile, one arid, one boreal, and two others will consistently outperform a 3-planet system no matter how good the individual planets are.
  • LUXURY RESOURCES PRESENTA secondary consideration. With Material Expertise, Vaulters are less RNG-dependent on luxury variety than other factions. A system with strategics and decent planet count beats a system with rare luxuries and poor planets.
THE REJUVENATION FIELD — KEEP IT IN YOUR SPHERE
Your starting anomaly provides Dust income and powerful fleet health regeneration to nearby ships. It also regenerates ships during battle if a fleet is engaged near it. If you colonize a nearby system and extend your influence sphere to include the Field, these benefits are free and permanent. Don't let your first colony's influence zone exclude it — scout the geometry before committing.

🛸 The Dart Scout: Your Most Versatile Early Asset STRATEGY ▾

The Vaulter scout ship (the Dart, class name Faering) is the best explorer ship in the game. The wiki confirms it matches Umbral Choir and Horatio's exploration capability, but unlike those factions, the Dart also has a dedicated weapon slot — making it a credible early combat ship without sacrificing probe capacity.

DART CONFIGURATION PHILOSOPHY
The Dart has up to 4 support slots available, plus a weapon slot and a defense slot. This gives you two viable early configurations:
  • PURE EXPLORER3 probe modules + max speed engines. 6 probes and high movement make it the fastest system-scanner in the game. Build 2–3 of these immediately and fan out across the galaxy before competitors claim the high-value systems you want.
  • COMBAT-CAPABLE EXPLORER2 probes + 1–2 engines + weapon slot equipped. Can blockade outposts, harass rivals in privateer mode, and assist in early combat. The same hull retrofits into a military ship in the mid-game, meaning your exploration investment converts directly into fleet expansion without building new ships from scratch.
THE REFIT PATH
Build your early Darts as explorers. When you've identified your colonization targets (turns 10–20), refit them into combat ships. You get exploration and early military coverage from the same Industry investment.

Material Expertise & System Development

The ability to use Strategic Resources as system upgrades — and why the resource tension it creates is a genuine strategic problem.
🔩 Strategic vs. Luxury: The Full Comparison MECHANIC ▾

Standard System Development upgrades consume Luxury Resources. Vaulters can instead consume Strategic Resources. The bonuses are different in structure, not just in type. Data from the Fandom wiki (fetched this session):

READING THE TABLE
Titanium and Hyperium give more total FIDSI than Common luxuries (+80 vs +60), but split across two stats. Whether this is better depends on what the system needs. A pure Industry system probably wants Jadonyx (+60 Industry) rather than Titanium (+40 Food +40 Industry). But if the system needs both stats, Titanium wins outright. The biggest advantage of Material Expertise is removing the dependency on luxury RNG: Vaulters can always develop their systems even without the correct luxury type, which is not true for other factions.

Quadrinix is exceptional. A ×150% multiplier on all existing System Development effects is the strongest possible late-game upgrade on any system that has already been developed. Prioritize securing Quadrinix in the late game for your best systems.

⚖️ The Titanium/Hyperium Tension STRATEGY ▾

Titanium and Hyperium are demanded by three competing uses: colonization cooldown buyouts, Portal construction (5× each per portal), and Material Expertise system development. This is the central resource management puzzle of any Vaulters game.

RESOLUTION FRAMEWORK
  • COLONIZATION FIRSTIf a high-value system is at risk of being claimed by an AI, the colonization buyout is the highest-priority use of your Titanium and Hyperium. A system lost to a competitor is permanent — a delayed system upgrade is not.
  • PORTALS SECONDEach portal costs 5 Titanium + 5 Hyperium + 160 Industry. In the early game, delay portals until your colonization pipeline is stable. Once you have 3+ systems, portal construction becomes the priority — the defensive and logistical value compounds with every additional system you add to the network.
  • SYSTEM DEVELOPMENT THIRDUse Titanium/Hyperium for system development when you have surplus above your colonization and portal pipeline needs. The koxsos guide (Tier 4 source) suggests a useful heuristic: prefer Titanium for development (since Hyperium is also consumed by engines and ship modules), but this depends heavily on your ship build priorities.
  • MARKETPLACE AS PRESSURE VALVESell surplus Luxury Resources to buy Titanium and Hyperium when the colonization cooldown is expiring and you're short. Vaulters generate more luxury resources than average (they can choose starting planet luxuries), so selling them to fund strategic resource purchases is a natural economic loop.

The Portal Network

Not a defensive tool. Not an offensive tool. A strategic force-multiplier that redefines what distance means.
🌐 Portal Mechanics and the Full Tech Upgrade Ladder MECHANIC ▾

A Portal is a system improvement costing 160 Industry + 5 Titanium + 5 Hyperium, with 16 Dust upkeep. It provides +10 Science to its system. Any fleet can teleport from a portal system to any other portal system in one turn — consuming all remaining movement points. Portal use is automatic: when you plot a course, the game routes you through the portal network if it's faster than star lane travel.

THE TECH UPGRADE LADDER — ALL PORTAL BONUSES
Portals gain additional functions as you research Metafolding affinity technologies. All data sourced from the wiki knowledge base:
Tech UnlockedPortal Upgrade Added
Base BuildingTeleportation + +10 Science + 16 Dust upkeep
Hardship Bonus Incentives (Metafolding affinity)Drydock: +15% Health Regen for fleets docked in portal system
Interstitial Spaces (Metafolding affinity, Economy tree)Manpower: +20 planetary Manpower/turn on portal systems
Dimensional Metafolding (Metafolding affinity of Stage 4 Dimensional Folding)Argosy II hull upgrade: Argosy becomes a mobile portal node
Hyperfolding (Vaulters-only, Stage 5)Hyperfold Hulls: portal transit costs only 1 movement point (not all MP)

The Broadcast and Wellness portal upgrades are referenced in the wiki economy article but their tech unlock sources are not fully documented in the available knowledge base — treat them as existing upgrades pending source confirmation.

HYPERFOLD HULLS — THE LATE-GAME GAME-CHANGER
Once you research Hyperfolding (Stage 5 Science & Exploration), portal transit costs only 1 movement point instead of all remaining movement points. Before this tech: a fleet teleports, then stops. After this tech: a fleet teleports and can continue moving normally. A fleet with 8 movement points can teleport across the galaxy and still patrol 7 systems worth of space in the same turn. This transforms portal-connected systems from "defensible" to "immediately reinforceable from anywhere."
ALLIES CAN USE YOUR PORTALS
Allied factions' fleets can use your portal network. With Hyperfold Hulls, the 1-movement-point cost applies to Vaulters and any allied faction that shares the Metafolding affinity. Other allies can still teleport but pay the full movement cost. This makes a portal-equipped Vaulter ally extraordinarily valuable in multiplayer and coalition diplomatic scenarios.

🧩 Strategic Applications: Defense, Offense, and Population STRATEGY ▾
FLEET UNIFICATION — ONE FLEET DEFENDS EVERYTHING
Because portal transit is instant (before Hyperfold) or nearly free (after), the Vaulters can maintain a single large fleet and respond to any attack within one turn. Other factions must distribute fleets across their borders or accept that some systems will always be exposed. Vaulters accept neither: one strong fleet, stationed in a central portal hub, can be at any system on the next turn. This means your combat power is never diluted by geography.
OFFENSIVE PORTAL NODES
A portal in a conquered forward system — or even in a system adjacent to an enemy — creates a staging point for power projection. 4X Alchemist describes this as sending "ships like hordes" once a forward portal is online. The offensive application is less about the individual system you're attacking and more about reducing the logistical cost of sustained pressure to near-zero.
CIVILIAN POP TRANSIT — THE HIDDEN POWER
The wiki makes a critical point that most guides underemphasize: portal transit works for civilian population transport ships, not just military fleets. This means Vaulters can move population from any full core system to any new distant colony in one turn, with zero interception risk. Other factions must route pop transports through hostile territory, past pirates, or across long star lane chains — a journey that can take 10–20 turns and be intercepted. Vaulters do it in one turn, every time. A new distant colony can be fully populated within a few turns of founding it. The Ruzen guide explicitly identifies this as the key to making distant isolated colonies viable.
OFFENSIVE AND DEFENSIVE SHIP DESIGN SPLIT
Because portal transit eliminates the need for defensive ships to move independently, you can design two variants of the same hull:
  • OFFENSIVE VARIANTEquipped with movement modules — engines in support slots — for fleet patrolling and strike operations that move through star lanes between portals.
  • DEFENSIVE VARIANTNo movement modules at all. The support slots freed from engines can be filled with weapons, defense, or combat support modules instead. These ships sit in a portal system, fight any fleet that arrives, and teleport to wherever the threat is next turn. They don't need to move under their own power — the portal does it.
This is a genuine efficiency gain: the same hull, optimized for its role. Ruzen's guide recommends labeling these ships explicitly (e.g. prefixing the name with "O-" or "D-") to avoid mixing them up mid-war.

Heroes, Politics & Laws

Petrov stays home. The Senate rewards Industry efficiency. Influence becomes a bottleneck you must plan around.
🦸 Petrov: Governor, Not Admiral STRATEGY ▾

The Vaulters start with Petrov Utica, an Overseer/Scientist class hero. Her skill tree is designed for system governance, not fleet combat. Both community sources and the wiki's ship data confirm she has no exceptional fleet bonuses to justify removing her from a system — and her governor abilities are too strong to waste on a fleet role.

PETROV'S KEY GOVERNOR ABILITIES
  • FARMING LOGISTICS (TIER 1)+10 Food per Fertile planet in the system at level 1, doubling to +20 at level 2. If your home system has 1–2 Fertile planets (common for Vaulters due to system selection flexibility), this translates to +20–40 bonus Food immediately. Maximum population is reached much faster.
  • SCIENCE BONUS (TIER 2)A percentage Science bonus on the governed system — reported as +20% at level 1, +30% at level 2 by the 4X Alchemist guide. On a system already producing high Science from Golden Age and improvements, this percentage multiplier is substantial.
  • STRATEGIC DEPOSIT STRENGTH (UNIQUE)Her unique starting skill increases output from strategic resource deposits in the system. Combined with a home system that ideally has Titanium and/or Hyperium, this generates additional strategics income without consuming your deposits' normal output.
HERO ACADEMY TIMING
Recruit a second hero from the Academy as early as you can afford one. Vaulters level their governors quickly due to high Industry output (more improvements built = more XP). A second hero can govern your second colony while Petrov stays on the capital, establishing a dual-governor setup that significantly accelerates both systems' development.

📜 Politics, Laws & the Influence Bottleneck STRATEGY ▾
STARTING GOVERNMENT — SCIENTIFIC/MILITARIST FEDERATION
The Vaulters begin as a Federation with Scientists as the dominant party and Militarists as the secondary. Federation gives a bonus to core world Approval and influence income. Scientists are the natural dominant party: Vaulter populations are Science-typed, their improvements tend toward Scientists, and the science bonuses compound directly into tech speed. The Militarist secondary is a useful fallback for military laws without needing to build their party from scratch.
EARLY LAW PRIORITY — DIRTY HANDS ACT
The 4X Alchemist guide makes an explicit recommendation here: choose Dirty Hands Act (−20% Industry cost on system improvements) over Lower Fleet Costs as your early law. The reasoning: Vaulters spend more Industry on system improvements than on ships in the early game, because the Golden Age enables you to build improvements immediately. A −20% discount on improvements compounds every time you construct something during the first 30 turns — which you're doing constantly. Lower Fleet Costs is useful but secondary to the empire-building priority.
INFLUENCE BECOMES A BOTTLENECK AT SCALE
Vaulters grow population quickly. More population means more influence required to maintain laws. The Ruzen guide flags this explicitly: around turns 30–40, your influence income may no longer comfortably cover law maintenance costs as population scales. Plan for this:
  • EARLYBuild the standard Influence improvements as part of your system pipeline — do not skip them in favor of FIDS buildings exclusively.
  • MID-GAMEUse hero Senate bonuses to supplement influence income. Keep a hero assigned to a Senate role if you are feeling law-maintenance pressure.
  • PORTALS AS HELPKoxsos notes that portals help with population logistics (moving pops to better Influence-generating systems), which indirectly stabilizes the Influence situation — but this requires having portals already built.

Corsairs & Pirate Relations

Blockade without war. Neutralize the pirate threat before it exists. Turn pirate bases into portal nodes.
☠️ Privateers, Blockades, and the Non-Aggression Pact MECHANIC ▾

Most factions must research a relatively late-game technology to access the Privateers action. Vaulters have it from turn 1, and can apply it to their own faction ships — not just marketplace vessels. In privateer mode, your ships are disguised as pirates and can engage in combat, blockade systems, and invade ground targets without triggering formal diplomatic consequences.

PRACTICAL CORSAIR USE CASES
  • BLOCKADE SNIPINGIf an AI has started an outpost on a system you want, switch your Dart scouts to privateer mode and blockade the outpost. A blockaded outpost cannot grow and will eventually be abandoned. You appear as pirates — no diplomatic incident. Once clear, colonize with the Argosy.
  • HARASSMENT WITHOUT WARIn the mid-game, corsair harassment of a rival's supply lines or border systems applies pressure without triggering their alliance network. The wiki confirms that if a blockaded-and-invaded system becomes a "hunting ground," the Vaulters can then colonize it.
  • EARLY AGGRESSION WINDOWKoxsos notes that the early game window (before neighbors build detection) is when corsair play is cheapest — your Darts are already out scouting, and the privateer switch costs nothing but a toggle.
NON-AGGRESSION PACT — MECHANICS
Vaulters can spend Influence to force a Cordial relationship with the Pirate League, regardless of the underlying relationship value. This is not a bribe that shifts the actual friendship score — it overrides the behavior for a fixed period: 10 turns on Normal speed per the wiki. The pirate league will not attack you during this window even if the underlying score would normally trigger hostility. You can also use standard dust bribes in parallel.
PIRATE BASES AS PORTAL NODES
The wiki confirms: if you maintain a Cordial rating with the Pirate League (via either method), pirate bases count as usable portal nodes for your fleets. This extends your portal network to pirate-controlled locations across the galaxy — potentially giving you teleportation access to areas you haven't colonized. This is a significant strategic bonus that requires almost no additional investment if you're already managing pirate relations.

Ships & Combat Philosophy

Weak T1 attackers. Exceptional scouts. Unique quest modules. Boarding as a primary win condition.
⚔️ Hull Overview and the Combat Identity MECHANIC ▾

The Vaulters do not have a military identity in the traditional sense. They receive no passive offensive bonuses (no flat damage boost, no weapon damage multiplier). Their T1 attacker is one of the weakest in the game. The wiki states directly: "it's important as the vaulters to get your higher tier ships online" — their T2 ships are competitive, but T1 is not a position of strength. Combat is not where Vaulters are designed to dominate; it is something to be managed around their strategic advantages.

THE KARVE (T1 ATTACKER) — KNOW THE WEAKNESS
The Karve has only 2 base Attack slots and broadside-only weapon mount positions — no top-mounted turret. The Pangolin ship analysis guide confirms this means the Karve has a 120° firing arc per weapon and dead zones directly ahead and aft. It is also the weakest T1 attacker in the game by module count. The practical implications: do not engage enemies ship-for-ship with Karves early. Use Darts in the combat role instead (which have a top-mounted weapon and better flexibility), and push hard to unlock T2 hulls.
VAULTER ADVANCED WARP — THE UNIQUE MOVEMENT MODULE
When you research the Advanced Topology Algorithms tech (the Vaulters' faction-specific variant of N-Dimensional Topologies), you unlock Vaulter Advanced Warp instead of the standard Accelerated Warp Drive. The wiki confirms the stats: +50 Health, +10% repair after battle, +5 movement points. This is simultaneously a speed module and a passive repair/durability module — a unique combination that no other faction gets. For the "fragile early ships" problem, this is the most accessible mitigation: install these on your Darts and early attackers to give them survivability as well as reach.
THE TIRYAK DRIVE — QUEST REWARD, NOT TO BE SKIPPED
The Vaulter faction quest (Chapter 1) offers a reward choice that includes the Tiryak Drive, a Support Module that provides high evasion — the wiki notes it gives values "most factions can only get in the lategame." Evasion is one of the most effective defensive stats in ES2 because it causes attacks to miss entirely. Stack 3–4 Tiryak Drives on your Dart scouts and you have ships that are extremely difficult to kill despite their low armor. This quest reward is worth planning your Chapter 1 objective around.
BOARDING — THE PRIMARY COMBAT IDENTITY
The Vaulters have access to Tier 2 boarding modules before other factions can research comparable options. Boarding pods deal crew damage over a battle duration and can, if the enemy crew is reduced to zero, capture the enemy ship. The wiki (Ruzen, koxsos, Pangolin guide all agree): boarding is the Vaulter combat style. Crew-kill battle cards combined with boarding pods and fast ships that close to melee range is the most effective Vaulter offensive posture. The Hellraiser Torpedo quest reward (50 DPS, 15% crit) is a secondary option for the missile slot but the boarding identity is the primary one to build around.
WEAPON TYPE — LASERS OVER MISSILES
For Vaulters, laser weapons are superior to kinetic missiles in most compositions. The Ruzen guide explains the reasoning: Vaulters' combat style involves closing to melee range for boarding. Missiles fire fewer, higher-damage salvos and are vulnerable to flak defenses. Lasers fire continuously at shorter range, which aligns with the close-quarters boarding engagement Vaulters want. Missiles are less efficient when the enemy can deflect them with point-defense as you close in.

Victory Paths & Empire Strategy

When to stop colonizing. Which victory condition the kit points toward. How to end a war without starting one.
🏆 The System Count Ceiling & Empire Quality vs. Quantity STRATEGY ▾
THE OVERCOLONIZATION THRESHOLD — YOUR SOFT CAP
Once your colony count exceeds the Overcolonization threshold, every system suffers an Approval penalty. For Vaulters this matters more than for wide factions because: (a) they lose the Core Worlds +10 Approval bonus, and (b) their system quality already depends on maintaining Devoted or Ecstatic Approval for maximum FIDS multipliers. The 4X Alchemist guide explicitly recommends staying at or below ~8 high-quality systems — not because the mechanic forces you to, but because the quality differential between a Devoted 6-system Vaulter empire and an Overcolonized 12-system Vaulter empire is stark. 8 exceptional systems consistently outperform 12 average ones.
WHAT "TALL" ACTUALLY REQUIRES
The tall strategy only works if each system you colonize is genuinely exceptional. If you colonize average systems because they're available, you lose the quality premium that makes the constrained colony count worthwhile. The scouting investment in the early game is not optional — it is the prerequisite for everything else working. Every system you add above the threshold should be better than any system your neighbors have colonized, or it's not worth the Approval cost.

🎯 Victory Conditions: What the Kit Points To STRATEGY ▾
WONDER VICTORY — THE NATURAL OUTPUT
Both the 4X Alchemist guide and the koxsos guide identify Wonder Victory as the most natural Vaulter win condition. The reasoning: Vaulters have exceptional early Industry (via Golden Age), access to strategic resources from their home system, the Dirty Hands Act Industry discount, and high Science output for rapid tech progression. All of this directly powers Wonder construction. A Titanium + Jade Onyx starting system can build competitive Wonders within the first 30 turns, establishing a lead that compounds throughout the game.
SCIENCE VICTORY — THE ALTERNATIVE
If your starting position has Hyperium and strong Science-bearing planets, a Science Victory is achievable. The Vaulters' natural Science output (starting with Xenobiology and Rare Earth Foams already researched, Science-typed population, portal +10 Science per system, Petrov's percentage bonuses) can exceed other science-focused factions in well-optimized games. The 4X Alchemist guide notes that Vaulters can "out-research a Sophon" with a Hyperium-focused system development strategy.
CONQUEST VIA CORSAIRS — CONTROLLED EXPANSION
The 4X Alchemist recommended late-game approach: identify the weakest neighbor (in their example, Horatio), use corsair fleets to clear their border systems without formal war declaration, then colonize the cleared systems with the Argosy. This expands the empire without triggering full-scale diplomatic retaliation, while staying below the system count ceiling by taking only the best available systems. Pause after each conquest, consolidate and build portals in new acquisitions, then assess whether another expansion is warranted.

First 30 Turns Playbook

Turn-by-turn priorities from the Rejuvenation Field to a functioning portal-linked network.
🗓️ Opening Sequence STRATEGY ▾
TURN 1
Assess the starting map. Note every system reachable from the Rejuvenation Field. Count planets, identify strategic resources, check planet types. If the best visible option is a 4-planet system with good types and at least one strategic resource, colonize it immediately — the Golden Age starts banking turns while you move. If you see a possibility of a 5-planet system one more turn of scouting away, spend the turn: the Golden Age accrual compensates the delay.

Build 2 Dart scouts immediately from your starting Industry. Configure them as pure explorers (max speed + probes). Set Petrov as governor on your new capital as soon as it's founded.

Research priority: Xenolinguistics if you have Fertile planets + strategics (gives both colonization tech and curiosity boost). Otherwise: whichever T1 colonization tech covers your available planet types.

Law: Dirty Hands Act immediately for the −20% Industry improvement discount.
TURNS 2–8
Fan scouts outward. You want to identify your second and third colonization targets before the Argosy is ready. Each scout should be heading toward a different constellation arm. Probe every system you reach.

Build improvements during Golden Age. The +25 FIDS boost means you are producing at full capacity from turn 1. Queue: Drone Networks → Xeno-Industrial Infrastructure → whatever FIDS building your system type favors. Do not queue ships during this window unless you have a specific threat to address — improvements return far more value per Industry point while the Golden Age bonus multiplies production.

Argosy available in 2–3 turns. Identify your target system before it returns. If you have a clear excellent option, prepare the Argosy to move immediately. If not, let it bank more Golden Age turns.
TURNS 8–15
Second colonization. Pick the best system your scouts have found. Colonize and ride the second Golden Age. If the system is already partially known to have the right resources, consider using Titanium/Hyperium to buy the colonization early rather than waiting the full 10 turns — especially if a competitor is visible nearby.

Start building toward your first portal. 160 Industry + 5 Titanium + 5 Hyperium is the cost. With two systems producing, this should be achievable within 10–15 turns of your second colony. Prioritize the portal on your capital (most central position) first.

Recruit a second hero from the Academy if it has been discovered. Put them on your second colony as governor.
TURNS 15–25
Third colonization. By now your scouts should have mapped a significant portion of the galaxy. Pick the third system to maximize quality — distance from your capital is irrelevant because portals will connect it regardless.

Non-Aggression Pact with pirates if the pirate league is becoming aggressive. One Influence payment for 10 turns of peace is cheap. If you can reach Cordial naturally through dust bribes, pirate bases begin functioning as portal nodes — map where they are relative to your intended colonization targets.

Assess combat situation. If you have hostile neighbors within range, refit 2–3 Darts into combat configuration (weapon slot + Tiryak Drive if available) and establish a defensive fleet. Station it at your most central portal-connected system.
TURNS 25–30
Portal network coming online. If capital portal is built, second portal should be the next Industry priority in your second colony. Once two portals exist, fleet unification becomes operational: one fleet covers both systems.

Material Expertise upgrades. If you have surplus Titanium or Hyperium beyond your colonization and portal pipeline, begin System Development upgrades. Titanium for Food + Industry systems; Hyperium for Dust + Science systems.

Faction quest progress. Review Chapter 1 objectives. If the Tiryak Drive reward is achievable (reach Cordial with pirates for 12 turns, or possess 4 anomalies on colonized planets), begin working toward it actively. The Tiryak Drive is worth significant detour to acquire.

By turn 30: 3–4 systems, first portal online or close to it, scouts mapped most of the galaxy, Petrov at governor level 3+, Dirty Hands Act active, 1–2 Wonders under construction or queued.
